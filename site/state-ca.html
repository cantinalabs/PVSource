California Solar Incentives & Policy

Everything for solar in California — NEM 3.0 net billing, SGIP storage, the §73 property-tax exclusion, sales-tax treatment, and the Title 24 mandate, with an at-a-glance table.

NEM 3.0Net billing
SGIPStorage
§73Property exclusion

California is the largest solar market in the United States, but it is also one of the most policy-complex. This guide summarizes the state's current net-billing rules, tax treatment, utility incentives, and permitting landscape for both residential and commercial systems, with pointers to verify time-sensitive values.

Educational reference only — not tax or legal advice. Programs change frequently. Verify every value below with the relevant state agency, your utility, and DSIRE before making decisions.

Overview

California enjoys an excellent solar resource — roughly 5.5 to 6.5 peak sun-hours per day across most of the state, with the highest yields in the southern deserts (Imperial, Riverside, San Bernardino counties) and lower values along the fog-prone coast. High retail electricity rates (among the highest in the continental U.S.) have historically driven strong solar economics.

Key regulators and agencies:

  • California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) — sets net-billing tariffs, SGIP, and rules for the three large investor-owned utilities (IOUs): PG&E, SCE, SDG&E.
  • California Energy Commission (CEC) — administers Title 24 building energy standards and equipment certification.
  • California State Board of Equalization (BOE) — administers the §73 active-solar property-tax exclusion.
  • Publicly owned / municipal utilities (LADWP, SMUD, Silicon Valley Power, Imperial Irrigation District, etc.) set their own net-metering and interconnection rules — often more favorable than the IOU net-billing tariff.

Key point: California policy differs sharply between the three IOUs (governed by CPUC) and the many municipal utilities (governed by their own boards). Always confirm which utility serves the property.

Net Metering / Net Billing

For the three IOUs (PG&E, SCE, SDG&E), California replaced traditional net metering with the Net Billing Tariff (NBT), commonly called NEM 3.0, effective April 15, 2023.

Key features of the Net Billing Tariff:

  • Exports are credited at hourly "avoided cost" (Avoided Cost Calculator) values, not the retail rate. These export values vary by hour, month, and day type and are typically a small fraction of the retail rate (often single-digit cents/kWh, spiking during evening peak hours).
  • This structure strongly favors self-consumption and battery storage — storing midday production for evening use is generally more valuable than exporting it.
  • NEM 2.0 grandfathering: customers interconnected under NEM 2.0 before the April 2023 cutoff remain on NEM 2.0 terms for 20 years from their interconnection date.
  • In March 2026, a California appellate court upheld the NEM 3.0 framework, so the net-billing structure remains in force — verify current status, as further appeals or CPUC modifications are possible.

Commercial IOU customers are likewise on net billing. Multi-tenant and multifamily properties may use Virtual Net Billing / VNEM and aggregation arrangements — see the site's commercial multifamily/VNEM material.

Municipal utilities (LADWP, SMUD, etc.) are not subject to NEM 3.0 and generally still offer more generous net-metering-style crediting. Verify with the specific muni.

For the general mechanics of these tariff structures, see Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

Bottom line on exports: Under NEM 3.0, a solar-only system exports for far less than it once did. Pairing solar with storage is now central to California residential economics.

SREC / RECs

California has a Renewables Portfolio Standard (RPS) — one of the most ambitious in the nation (targeting 100% clean electricity by 2045) — but it does not run a tradable SREC (Solar Renewable Energy Certificate) market for distributed/rooftop solar the way states like New Jersey, Massachusetts, or Illinois do.

  • There is no solar carve-out generating homeowner-sellable SRECs in California.
  • RECs (including PCC-classified RECs) are transacted at the wholesale/utility-procurement level for RPS compliance, not by residential owners.
  • California's principal distributed-solar incentive is the net-billing bill credit plus SGIP (below), not certificate sales.

For how SREC markets work in states that have them, see SREC Markets Deep-Dive.

Key point: No retail SREC income in California. Don't budget a project around SREC sales here.

State Tax Credits

California offers no state personal or corporate income-tax credit for solar PV. There is no California analog to the federal residential or commercial credit.

  • Personal income tax: no state solar credit.
  • Corporate income tax: no state solar credit.

California's incentive value comes from avoided electricity costs (high retail rates), SGIP, the §73 property-tax exclusion, and federal commercial credits — not from a state income-tax credit. For state-by-state credit comparisons, see Residential Incentives.

Verify current status at the California Franchise Tax Board and DSIRE California.

Federal Tax Credits

  • Residential (§25D Residential Clean Energy Credit): ended after December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Homeowner cash/loan purchases placed in service in 2026 or later cannot claim the 30% residential credit. See Residential Incentives.
  • Commercial (§48 / §48E ITC and MACRS depreciation): the commercial Investment Tax Credit remains available in 2026, subject to OBBBA's phase-out timeline and begin-construction / placed-in-service deadlines for solar and wind. Adders for domestic content, energy communities, and low-income access may apply. See Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Federal rules changed materially under OBBBA and are date-sensitive. Confirm eligibility and deadlines with a tax professional and the IRS.

Utility Incentives / Rebates

The flagship California incentive is the Self-Generation Incentive Program (SGIP), administered by the CPUC through the IOUs (PG&E, SCE, SDG&E, SoCalGas) plus a separate LADWP program.

  • SGIP pays rebates for behind-the-meter energy storage (batteries), on a $/kWh basis that steps down over time.
  • The most generous tiers are the Equity and Equity Resiliency categories (targeting low-income and fire/PSPS-vulnerable customers), historically $850–$1,000/kWh; general-market tiers are much lower.
  • As of 2026, general-market and several equity budgets are heavily subscribed, waitlisted, or exhausted in some territories. SGIP availability changes constantly — verify current budget and step at your utility and the CPUC.

Some municipal utilities and CCAs run their own storage or solar programs. Time-of-use rate optimization and demand-charge management matter heavily for commercial customers.

Key point: SGIP is storage-focused, budget-limited, and step-based. Check live availability at the CPUC SGIP page before relying on it.

For storage design and economics, see Battery Storage.

Sales Tax Exemption

California does not offer a full, blanket sales-tax exemption for solar equipment.

  • There is a partial state sales-and-use tax exemption for manufacturing and certain electric power generation equipment (a partial-rate exemption under CDTFA rules) that can apply to some solar generation equipment for qualifying purchasers.
  • For a typical residential rooftop purchase, expect standard state and local sales tax to generally apply to equipment.

Because this is a partial manufacturing exemption, not a residential solar exemption, confirm applicability with the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration (CDTFA) and DSIRE.

Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

California provides the Active Solar Energy System New Construction Exclusion under Revenue & Taxation Code §73 (the "§73 exclusion").

  • When a qualifying active solar energy system is installed, the added value is excluded from the property's assessed value — installing solar does not raise your property-tax bill.
  • The exclusion is a new-construction exclusion, administered by the county assessor per BOE guidance.
  • Sunset: the exclusion has been extended multiple times by the Legislature. Historically it applied to systems completed before a statutory deadline (previously Jan 1, 2027), with legislation (e.g., SB 710) extending qualifying lien dates into the 2026–2031 window, and systems that qualify before the sunset generally remain excluded until a subsequent change in ownership. The exact current sunset date is date-sensitive — verify before relying on it.

Key point: The §73 exclusion is valuable but has a legislative sunset. Confirm the current completion deadline at the California BOE Active Solar Exclusion page and DSIRE.

Permitting & Interconnection Notes

  • California has hundreds of Authorities Having Jurisdiction (AHJs) — cities and counties — each with its own permitting process, though state law pushes toward standardization.
  • AB 2188 / SB 379 require many jurisdictions to adopt instant, automated online permitting for small residential solar-plus-storage, and California has been a leading adopter of the SolarAPP+ automated permitting platform.
  • Interconnection for IOU customers follows CPUC-jurisdictional rules (Rule 21); municipal utilities have their own interconnection tariffs.
  • Title 24 (the Building Energy Efficiency Standards) mandates on-site solar PV on most new residential construction and solar-plus-storage on many new commercial building types — a design requirement rather than an incentive. See California Title 24 & Commercial Solar.

Typical residential timelines vary widely by AHJ; SolarAPP+ jurisdictions can approve permits same-day, while others take days to weeks. See Residential Permitting and Commercial Permitting.

Bottom Line

California remains a top-tier solar market driven by high electricity rates, the §73 property-tax exclusion, SGIP storage rebates, and the Title 24 building mandate — but the shift to NEM 3.0 net billing has made battery storage essential to strong residential economics. There is no state income-tax credit and no retail SREC market, so value comes from avoided energy costs, storage incentives, and (for commercial) the federal ITC and depreciation. Because net-billing rules, SGIP budgets, and the property-tax sunset are all changing, verify current specifics with the CPUC, your utility, and DSIRE before committing.
