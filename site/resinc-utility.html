Residential Incentives

Utility Residential Rebates & Programs

Utility-level incentives for homeowners — upfront rebates, storage incentives (ConnectedSolutions, SGIP, GMP BYOD), performance payments, and VPP/demand-response enrollment.

RebatesUpfront
StorageConnectedSolutions…
VPPEnroll

Beyond federal and state incentives, your local utility may offer some of the most valuable — and most time-sensitive — residential solar and storage incentives. These include upfront rebates, battery/storage incentives, performance-based payments, and demand-response / virtual power plant (VPP) enrollment that pays you for sharing stored energy. This page surveys the major program types with real examples and explains how to find your utility's offerings.

Utility programs are hyper-local and often budget-capped. Many are first-come, first-served, pause when funds run out, or open only during enrollment windows. What's available in one utility's territory may not exist next door. Confirm with your specific utility and cross-check DSIRE.

Upfront Rebates

Some utilities pay a direct rebate toward system cost — a flat amount or a $/watt figure — usually requiring approved equipment and installers and interconnection.

Feature Detail
Form Flat $ or $/watt (e.g., historically $0.10–$0.50/W in various programs)
Timing Paid at or after installation/interconnection
Constraint Budget-limited; often waitlisted
Tax note A rebate paid to you generally reduced the federal-credit basis while §25D applied — see Residential Federal Solar Tax Credit (§25D)

With §25D ended for homeowner-owned systems placed in service after Dec 31, 2025, utility rebates no longer interact with a federal residential credit — but they still stack with state credits, SRECs, and net-metering value.

Example — Austin Energy (TX). As of July 1, 2026, Austin Energy pays a $4,000 residential solar rebate for a system larger than 3 kW (up from $2,500), open to any customer who hasn't yet installed. To earn it you must complete Austin Energy's free Solar Education Course, use an Austin Energy Participating Contractor, and get a rebate confirmation letter before installation. Austin Energy credits production under a Value of Solar tariff (~9.91¢/kWh on all generation) rather than net metering, and battery owners can earn peak rewards through the Power Partner program (third-party PPAs are not allowed in its territory). See the full breakdown, including commercial capacity- and performance-based incentives, in the Texas solar guide.

Battery / Storage Incentives

As net billing spreads and export credits shrink, storage incentives have become the headline utility offering. Several programs pay substantial amounts — often in exchange for grid access to your battery (see VPPs below).

Program Territory Key facts (verified 2026)
SGIP (Self-Generation Incentive Program) California IOUs State/utility storage rebate; recent budgets focused on equity/LMI (Residential Solar & Storage Equity). Popular tiers frequently fully reserved — verify open funding
NV Energy Residential Storage Nevada Historically up to ~$3,000 (TOU) / ~$1,500 (non-TOU); new applications suspended during high-demand periods — verify status
Green Mountain Power BYOD Vermont Bring Your Own Device: buy your own battery, earn up to ~$10,500 for sharing peak capacity (roughly $850–$950 per kW enrolled). Vermont regulators lifted program caps to expand access
ConnectedSolutions MA, RI, CT, NH (Eversource / National Grid) Pays an annual incentive for battery access during peaks — typically ~$500–$750/yr per Powerwall, up to ~$3,750 over five years

Read the grid-access terms. Many high-value storage incentives (BYOD, ConnectedSolutions, SGIP-equity) require you to let the utility discharge your battery during peak events. That's usually fine — and often reserves a backup floor — but confirm how many events, for how long, and whether you keep reserve capacity for outages. See Residential Battery Backup and Battery Storage.

Performance-Based Incentives (PBIs)

Some utilities (and state programs run through utilities) pay per kWh produced over a term rather than an upfront lump sum. These reward real output, so system quality and shading matter. Examples include Massachusetts SMART (declining-block PBI) and Illinois Shines block payments — see MA SMART and SREC Markets Deep-Dive. PBIs contrast with tradable SRECs, which float with the market.

Demand Response and Virtual Power Plants (VPPs)

A virtual power plant aggregates thousands of home batteries (and sometimes smart thermostats, EV chargers, and water heaters) into a grid resource the utility can call on during peak demand. Homeowners enroll and get paid.

VPP / DR example How it works
Green Mountain Power (VT) Networks home Powerwalls and BYOD batteries into a ~50 MW VPP; pays via BYOD incentives
ConnectedSolutions (New England) Demand-response battery dispatch during peaks for an annual payment
Utility thermostat / EV programs Smaller bill credits for letting the utility adjust load during events

Benefits of enrolling:

  • Ongoing income on top of bill savings — turning an idle battery into a paying asset.
  • Some programs offer enhanced upfront rebates for committing to grid access.

Trade-offs to check:

  • Number and duration of dispatch events, and whether you keep a backup reserve.
  • Contract term and early-exit terms.
  • Whether enrollment affects your net-metering/net-billing arrangement.

See the site's Grid Services & VPP guide for the broader mechanics.

How to Find Your Utility's Programs

  1. Go to your utility's website — look for "solar," "renewables," "energy storage," "rebates," or "demand response."
  2. Search DSIRE by ZIP and filter for utility-administered incentives.
  3. Call the utility's energy-programs line to confirm current funding and enrollment windows (these open and close).
  4. Ask your installer — reputable installers track local utility programs and handle enrollment paperwork.
  5. Verify grid-access terms before enrolling any battery in a VPP/DR program.

For the full picture, combine utility programs with Residential Net Metering & Net Billing, State Residential Tax Credits & Rebates, Residential SRECs for Homeowners, Property & Sales-Tax Exemptions, and — for income-qualified households — Low-Income & Equity Solar Programs. Note the federal §25D credit ended after 2025 for homeowner-owned systems.
