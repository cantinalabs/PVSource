Community Solar

Shared solar off-site — the subscription model and bill credits, developer vs subscriber economics, the state program landscape, low-income carve-outs, and project sizing.

Shared arraySubscribers
≤ 5 MWTypical size
State programsWhere it exists

Community solar lets multiple customers share the output — and the bill savings — of a single, off-site solar array they don't own or host. Instead of putting panels on your own roof, you subscribe to a portion of a nearby ground-mount or large rooftop project, and the utility applies bill credits to your account for your share of what that array produces. It's the answer for the large share of homes and businesses that can't host their own system: renters, tenants in leased buildings, shaded sites, and organizations whose roofs or budgets can't accommodate on-site PV.

For commercial and industrial (C&I) audiences, community solar shows up in two distinct ways: as a subscriber (an off-site way to procure clean-energy savings) and as a developer/host (building and monetizing a community array as a revenue-generating asset). This guide covers the model, the roles, the economics on both sides, sizing, the state landscape, low-income provisions, subscriber management, and how it compares to on-site net metering. Illinois is one of the most active markets — see Illinois Shines & Commercial Solar for that program's specifics. For the on-site alternatives this competes with, see Commercial System Design.

Key idea: Community solar decouples where the panels are from who benefits. You get solar bill savings without owning, hosting, or maintaining any equipment.

The community-solar model

The mechanics are consistent across most state programs:

  1. A developer builds an off-site shared array — typically a ground-mount in the same utility territory.
  2. The array's capacity is divided into subscriptions (shares), sold or leased to homes and businesses.
  3. The array exports its generation to the grid; the utility meters that output.
  4. Each subscriber's account receives bill credits proportional to their share of production (a form of virtual net metering / bill crediting).
  5. Subscribers pay the developer/subscriber organization for their share — usually at a discount to the value of the credits they receive — so the net result is a lower electricity bill.

Roles in a community-solar project

Role Who Responsibility
Developer / owner Solar developer or investor Finances, builds, owns, and operates the array; captures tax benefits and project revenue
Subscriber organization (SO) Often the developer or a third party Acquires and manages subscribers, handles billing, contracts, and churn
Utility Local electric utility Interconnects the array, meters production, applies bill credits to subscriber accounts
Subscriber Home or business Buys/leases a share; receives bill credits; pays the SO at a discount
Regulator / program admin State PUC or program administrator Sets rules: credit rates, sizing caps, low-income carve-outs, consumer protections

Subscriber vs developer economics

Subscriber side

  • Savings, not ownership. Subscribers typically pay for their share of credits at a discount (commonly in the range of ~5%–20%, varying by program and market), netting a modest, ongoing reduction in electricity costs.
  • No capital, no equipment, no maintenance. No roof needed, nothing to install, nothing to repair.
  • No direct tax credits. Subscribers generally don't claim the federal ITC — the project owner does. (Tax-credit detail belongs in the site's incentive guides — see Illinois Shines & Commercial Solar for program treatment; reference, don't duplicate.)
  • Flexibility: many programs allow subscribers to cancel or transfer with notice, though terms vary.

Watch the contract: Read escalators, term length, cancellation/transfer terms, and how credits are valued. A "discount" only saves money if the credit value tracks your actual utility rate.

Developer side

  • Revenue streams (see below) plus federal/state incentives drive project economics.
  • Developers monetize the ITC and other incentives (referenced in incentive guides, not duplicated here), depreciation, and the spread between subscriber payments and project costs.
  • Subscriber acquisition and churn are the central business risk: an array only earns if its capacity stays subscribed.

Revenue streams (developer)

  • Subscriber payments for bill-credit shares (the core, recurring revenue).
  • State program incentives / RECs — e.g., renewable energy credits or program-specific payments (Illinois Shines is one example; see that guide).
  • Federal tax incentives (referenced in the site's incentive guides — not duplicated here).
  • Accelerated depreciation on the asset.

Typical sizing

  • Community-solar arrays are commonly capped by program rules, frequently at ≤5 MW (AC) per project — large enough for economies of scale, small enough to interconnect at distribution voltage and qualify under program limits.
  • Some programs set smaller caps (a few MW) or allow larger; always check the specific state program.
  • A 5 MW project might serve hundreds to a few thousand residential subscribers, or a mix of residential and commercial subscribers.

State program landscape & availability

Community solar is only available where state law or a utility program enables it — there is no single national program. As of 2026, community-solar legislation has been enacted in roughly two dozen states plus D.C. and Puerto Rico, including Arizona, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Illinois, Louisiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New Mexico, Nevada, New York, North Carolina, Oregon, Rhode Island, South Carolina, Vermont, Virginia, and Washington (NREL, ILSR Community Solar Tracker).

  • The U.S. surpassed 10 GW of cumulative community-solar capacity by the end of 2025, with continued growth projected into 2026 (American Public Power Association).
  • Markets are dynamic: programs open, expand, or close. For example, Maine ended its community-solar program in December 2025, while developers are pursuing new markets in states such as Ohio, Iowa, Pennsylvania, and Michigan.

Verify availability locally. Whether community solar exists, who can subscribe, and how credits are valued depend entirely on your state and utility — and the rules change. Confirm current program status before counting on it.

Low-income carve-outs

Many state programs require or incentivize low- and moderate-income (LMI) participation:

  • Carve-outs / set-asides reserve a percentage of each project (or program capacity) for LMI subscribers.
  • Adders or enhanced incentives reward developers for serving LMI households, affordable housing, or community institutions.
  • These provisions broaden access to households that benefit most from bill savings but are least able to host on-site solar. Illinois Shines, for instance, includes equity-focused program components — see Illinois Shines & Commercial Solar.

Subscriber acquisition & management

For developers and subscriber organizations, keeping the array subscribed is the operational core of the business:

  • Acquisition: marketing, sales, and onboarding subscribers; verifying eligibility (and LMI status where required).
  • Billing & crediting: reconciling utility-applied credits against subscriber payments each cycle.
  • Churn management: subscribers move, close, or cancel; the SO must backfill capacity to avoid unsubscribed (unearning) shares.
  • Consumer protection compliance: disclosures, contract terms, and cancellation rights mandated by program rules.

Operational reality: A community-solar project is a customer business, not just an energy asset. Subscriber acquisition cost and churn often determine whether the project pencils.

Community solar vs on-site net metering

Dimension Community solar (off-site) On-site net metering
Location Off-site shared array On your own roof/property
Ownership You subscribe; developer owns You (or a PPA host) own the system
Capital required None (subscription) Significant (or financed/PPA)
Roof/site needed No Yes — suitable, unshaded roof or land
Maintenance None (developer handles) Yours (or O&M contract)
Tax credits Captured by project owner Captured by system owner
Savings Modest discount on credit value Potentially larger (offset retail rate directly)
Best for Renters, shaded/leased sites, no-capital buyers Owners with good roofs and tax appetite

On-site systems generally deliver deeper savings and asset value for organizations that can host them; community solar broadens access to everyone else and offers a low-commitment way to procure clean-energy savings.
