Re-Roofing & Solar

Roof and array on the same clock — install before or after a re-roof, remove-and-reinstall cost and process, warranties and penetrations, solar shingles, and coordinating trades.

Roof agevs 25-yr system
R&RRemove & reinstall
WarrantiesPenetrations

A solar array is designed to last 25-30 years, but the roof beneath it usually isn't — and that mismatch is one of the most expensive mistakes a homeowner can make. Putting panels on a roof that has only a few years of life left means paying to remove and reinstall the entire system before its time. This page covers the roof-age vs system-life problem, whether to re-roof before or after going solar, the remove-and-reinstall (R&R) process and cost, penetrations and warranties, solar shingles/BIPV, coordinating the two trades, timeline, and insurance/permits. See also Residential System Design, Residential Sizing & Calculations, Battery Energy Storage, and Residential Incentives.

Roof age vs 25-30 year system life

The driving fact: the array should outlive at least one roof unless the roof is new or near-new when the panels go on.

Roofing material Typical lifespan Fits a 25-30 yr array?
Asphalt 3-tab shingles ~15-20 years Often no — likely needs replacement mid-array
Architectural asphalt shingles ~25-30 years Yes, if new/recent
Metal roofing ~40-70 years Yes, easily
Tile (clay/concrete) ~50+ years Yes (but fragile to walk on)
Wood shake ~20-30 years Marginal; fire-code concerns

Key point: If your roof is more than ~10 years into an asphalt shingle's life, plan to re-roof before solar. Otherwise you'll likely pay thousands to remove and reinstall the array partway through its life.

A quick decision rule: - Roof is new or < ~5-10 years old (and good material): install solar now. - Roof is mid-life or older asphalt: re-roof first, then solar. - Roof is failing/leaking: absolutely re-roof first.

Installing before vs after a re-roof

Re-roof first, then solar Solar first, re-roof later
Cost One-time; no future R&R Pays for R&R when the roof eventually fails
Disruption Done before panels exist Array must be removed and reinstalled later
Risk Lowest — clean slate under the array Roof ages out under a 25-yr array
Best when Roof is mid-life or older Roof is genuinely new

The strong default is to re-roof first whenever the roof is anything but new. Doing both projects together also lets you correct roof issues (ventilation, flashing, decking) while the roof is exposed, and gives the solar installer a fresh, warrantied surface to penetrate.

Warning: Many roofers won't warranty a new roof installed under an existing array, and many solar warranties don't cover removing the array for roof work. Sequencing the roof first avoids both gaps.

Remove-and-reinstall (R&R) process & cost

If solar is already installed and the roof must be replaced, the array has to come off and go back on — the R&R.

Process: 1. De-energize and disconnect the system (qualified solar tech). 2. Remove panels, racking, and rooftop conduit/wiring; store on site. 3. Re-roof (roofer's scope), including patching old penetration points. 4. Re-mount racking with new flashing/penetrations, reinstall panels and wiring. 5. Re-commission, re-inspect, and restore interconnection/PTO as the AHJ/utility requires.

Cost drivers: system size (panel count), array complexity (multiple planes, optimizers/microinverters), labor rates, whether the same company did both, and storage/scaffolding. R&R is commonly quoted per panel plus the roof cost. This recurring expense is precisely why re-roofing before solar is cheaper overall.

Key point: R&R is a labor-heavy job that recurs every roof cycle if you don't sequence correctly. The cheapest R&R is the one you never have to do — re-roof first.

Roof penetrations & warranties

Most residential racking is roof-attached, meaning lag bolts/anchors penetrate the shingles into the rafters/decking, sealed with flashing and sealant.

  • Flashing integrated under the upper course of shingles is the primary leak defense; relying on sealant alone is poor practice.
  • Penetration count scales with array size; each is a potential leak point if poorly flashed.
  • Workmanship warranty. A reputable solar installer warranties their roof penetrations against leaks (often ~10-25 years). Get this in writing.
  • Roof manufacturer warranty. Penetrating a roof can affect the roofing manufacturer's warranty unless done by a certified installer with approved methods — coordinate so the roof warranty stays intact.
  • Who's responsible for a leak? When two companies are involved, leaks can become a finger-pointing dispute. Using one company for both, or getting clear written scopes, prevents this.

Solar shingles / BIPV option

Building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) — most familiarly solar shingles/tiles (e.g., solar-roof products) — replace the roofing material itself with PV-generating shingles, rather than mounting panels on top of a conventional roof.

  • Pros: integrated, low-profile aesthetics; the roof is the solar; can make sense when you're re-roofing anyway and want a premium look.
  • Cons: typically higher cost per watt and lower power density than conventional panels; fewer installers; longer/more complex installs; repair and future-replacement ecosystems are smaller.
  • When it fits: a homeowner who needs a new roof and prioritizes aesthetics, and accepts a price premium. For pure economics, conventional panels on a new asphalt or metal roof usually win.

Key point: BIPV combines the roof and the array into one product, which is elegant when you're re-roofing anyway — but expect a meaningful cost premium versus standard panels for the same kW.

Coordinating the roofer & solar installer

The two trades must be sequenced and scoped carefully.

  • Decide the sequence first: re-roof, then solar (the usual order).
  • Share the array layout with the roofer so they can plan flashing, ventilation, and avoid placing vents/obstructions where panels will go.
  • Single-vendor option: some solar companies offer roofing (or partner with a roofer), giving one point of accountability and a single combined warranty — often the cleanest path.
  • Coordinate warranties: make sure the roof manufacturer warranty isn't voided by the solar penetrations, and that the solar workmanship warranty explicitly covers the penetrations into the new roof.
  • Match lifespans: install the array on a roofing material whose life meets or exceeds the array (architectural asphalt, metal, or tile).

Logistics & timeline

A combined roof-then-solar project is a multi-step sequence, not a single weekend:

Phase Typical duration
Roof replacement ~1-3 days
Cure/settle + scheduling gap days to a couple of weeks
Solar permitting & engineering (often in parallel earlier) weeks
Solar installation ~1-3 days
Inspection (AHJ) + utility interconnection / PTO weeks

The roofing itself is fast; the permitting, inspection, and utility PTO steps usually dominate the calendar. Ideally the solar permit/design work runs in parallel so the array can go up shortly after the new roof is done.

Insurance & permits

  • Permits: both the re-roof and the solar install typically require separate permits and inspections from the building department (and an electrical permit for the PV). A licensed roofer and a licensed solar electrician handle their respective permits.
  • Homeowners insurance: notify your insurer when adding solar — it's a roof-mounted improvement that affects replacement cost. Some insurers have requirements or surcharges for rooftop PV; a new roof underneath can also lower premiums or satisfy underwriting.
  • Storm/hail damage: if a roof is replaced due to a covered claim, coordinate the timing so the array R&R aligns with the roof work; document the system before and after.
  • Financing/incentives: roofing is generally not covered by solar incentives. Note that the federal residential clean energy credit (§25D) ended for systems placed in service after Dec 31, 2025 — confirm current incentive status in Residential Incentives before assuming any roof or solar tax benefit.

Warning: Don't assume re-roofing costs qualify for solar tax credits — they generally don't, and the residential §25D credit is no longer available for systems placed in service after 2025. Treat the roof as its own project with its own permits, warranty, and insurance implications.
