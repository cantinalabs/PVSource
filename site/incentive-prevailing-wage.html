Prevailing Wage & Apprenticeship

The labor rules that unlock the 5× credit — prevailing wage (Davis-Bacon) and apprenticeship requirements, recordkeeping, the cure provisions, and the under-1-MW exemption.

6% → 30%The 5× unlock
Davis-BaconPrevailing wage
< 1 MWExempt

The prevailing wage and apprenticeship (PWA) requirements are the single most valuable compliance gate in the federal clean-energy credit system. Meeting them multiplies the base Investment Tax Credit (ITC) or Production Tax Credit (PTC) by five — for solar, that is the difference between a 6% base ITC and the headline 30% ITC. Missing them (without qualifying for an exemption) drops a large project to the base rate and can quietly destroy a project's economics.

This page explains the two requirements, the recordkeeping and certified-payroll burden, the cure and penalty provisions that let a good-faith failure be fixed, the important <1 MW AC exemption, how PWA interacts with the domestic-content and energy-community adders, and how the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) leaves the PWA framework in place. See also Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits, The "Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA), Domestic Content Bonus, and Energy Communities Bonus.

Why it exists. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) set the base credit low and made the full credit conditional on paying construction workers Davis-Bacon prevailing wages and using registered apprentices. Treasury and the IRS issued final regulations on June 25, 2024 (effective for facilities where construction began after June 25, 2024) governing how to satisfy and document these rules.

The 5× Multiplier: 6% vs. 30%

For the technology-neutral §48E ITC (and legacy §48), the base credit is 6% of eligible basis. Satisfy PWA — or qualify for an exemption — and the credit multiplies 5× to 30%. The same 5× logic applies to the §45Y/legacy §45 PTC, and it also drives the size of the bonus adders (discussed below).

Scenario ITC rate on eligible basis
PWA not met, no exemption 6%
PWA met (or <1 MW exemption) 30%
Domestic-content adder, PWA met +10%
Energy-community adder, PWA met +10%

Because the multiplier is 5×, PWA compliance is almost always worth pursuing on any project of 1 MW AC or larger — the labor-cost premium is small next to a credit that quintuples.

The <1 MW AC Exemption

The most important carve-out: a facility with maximum net output of less than 1 megawatt AC qualifies for the full 5× (30%) credit without satisfying PWA at all. This exempts the vast majority of small commercial rooftop and small ground-mount projects.

  • Measured in AC (net output), not DC nameplate.
  • Applies to §48E/§45Y and their legacy counterparts.
  • Beware anti-abuse/aggregation: related facilities can be treated as a single facility to prevent artificially splitting a large project into sub-1 MW pieces. Confirm how aggregation applies to your integrated operation.

There is also a beginning-of-construction (BOC) grandfather: projects that began construction before January 29, 2023 were generally eligible for the increased credit without PWA. For essentially all current projects, assume PWA applies at 1 MW AC and above.

Prevailing-Wage Requirement (Davis-Bacon Rates)

All laborers and mechanics employed by the taxpayer or any contractor or subcontractor must be paid no less than the prevailing wage — the locally determined base hourly rate and fringe benefits — as set by the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) under the Davis-Bacon Act (40 U.S.C. subchapter IV of chapter 31) for the type of work in the geographic area of the facility.

Prevailing wage applies during two windows:

  1. Construction — during the construction, alteration, or erection of the facility.
  2. The credit period — for the ITC, any alteration or repair of the facility during the 5-year recapture period; for the PTC, during the 10-year credit period. Ongoing operation and maintenance wages are generally outside the requirement unless the work is an alteration or repair.

Practical points:

  • Use the correct DOL wage determination (from SAM.gov / the DOL Wage Determinations tool) for the county and work classifications.
  • If no published rate exists for a classification, request a supplemental wage determination (conformance) from DOL.
  • Wages are due weekly; document base rate + fringe for every laborer/mechanic.

Apprenticeship Requirement (Three Tests)

The apprenticeship rules have three separate components, all measured against workers from a registered apprenticeship program (registered with DOL or a State Apprenticeship Agency):

Test Rule
Labor-hours A minimum percentage of total construction labor hours must be performed by qualified apprentices.
Ratio The apprentice-to-journeyworker ratio set by the registered program must be met each day apprentices work.
Participation Every taxpayer, contractor, or subcontractor that employs 4 or more laborers/mechanics on the project must employ at least one qualified apprentice.

The labor-hours percentage steps up with the construction-start year:

Construction begins Apprentice labor-hours %
Before Jan 1, 2023 10%
During 2023 12.5%
After Dec 31, 2023 15%

Good-Faith Effort exception (apprenticeship only). If the taxpayer requests qualified apprentices from a registered program and the request is denied (or not responded to within 5 business days), the taxpayer is generally treated as satisfying the apprenticeship requirement for that request. Document every request and response — this is the primary defense when apprentices are unavailable.

Recordkeeping & Certified Payroll

The final regulations put the burden of proof on the taxpayer. Maintain, for the full compliance period:

  • Certified payroll for all laborers/mechanics (classification, hours, base rate, fringe, deductions) — including contractors and subcontractors.
  • DOL wage determinations relied upon, and any conformance requests.
  • Apprenticeship documentation: registered-program agreements, ratios, labor-hour logs, and written good-faith requests and responses.
  • Evidence tying workers and hours to the specific facility for the construction period and any alteration/repair during the credit/recapture period.

Flow-down contract clauses are essential: your EPC contract and subcontracts should require Davis-Bacon wages, apprentice use, and delivery of certified payroll, with audit and indemnity rights.

Cure & Penalty Provisions

A failure is not automatically fatal — the statute provides cure mechanisms so a good-faith taxpayer can still claim the increased credit by making the workers whole and paying a penalty to the IRS.

Prevailing-wage cure (underpayment):

Component Amount
Correction payment to each underpaid worker Shortfall + interest
Penalty to the IRS $5,000 per affected worker (standard)
Intentional disregard 3× correction payment and a $10,000/worker penalty

Apprenticeship cure (labor-hours/ratio/participation shortfall):

  • A penalty of $50 per labor hour for which the requirement was not satisfied ($500/hour for intentional disregard).
  • The good-faith-effort exception (above) can eliminate a shortfall caused by unavailable apprentices.

There is generally no penalty cure available for intentional disregard beyond the elevated amounts, and correction/penalty payments must typically be made before or when the credit is claimed (or promptly after discovery). Timeliness matters — a Periodic Payment / prompt-correction posture strengthens the "not intentional disregard" position.

How PWA Interacts with the Adders

The domestic-content and energy-community bonuses are themselves 5×-geared. If PWA is met (or the <1 MW exemption applies), each adder is worth 10%; if PWA is not met, each adder collapses to 2%.

Adder PWA met (or exempt) PWA not met
Domestic content +10% +2%
Energy community +10% +2%

So PWA does double duty: it turns the base credit from 6% to 30% and turns each bonus from 2% to 10%. On a large project stacking both adders, the swing between "PWA met" and "PWA missed" can exceed 40 percentage points of eligible basis. See Domestic Content Bonus and Energy Communities Bonus.

OBBBA Continuity

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (H.R.1, signed July 4, 2025) did not repeal or rewrite the PWA framework — the 5× multiplier, the three apprenticeship tests, the <1 MW exemption, and the cure/penalty structure remain the mechanism for unlocking the full §48E/§45Y credit. What OBBBA changed is eligibility and timing for the underlying solar credit (accelerated phase-out and the Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) rules), not the PWA rules themselves.

  • Solar generally must begin construction by July 4, 2026 or be placed in service by Dec. 31, 2027 to claim the credit; PWA still governs whether that credit is 6% or 30%.
  • Verify current IRS beginning-of-construction guidance — 2025–2026 notices tightened BOC standards, which affects both credit eligibility and which PWA labor-hour percentage applies. See The "Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA).

Worked Example: 6% vs. 30%

Facts. A 3 MW AC commercial ground-mount project. Eligible basis $4,500,000. Above 1 MW, so PWA is required to reach 30%. No adders modeled.

Eligible basis                         $4,500,000

Case A — PWA NOT met (6% base)
  ITC = 6% x $4,500,000              =   $270,000

Case B — PWA met (30%)
  ITC = 30% x $4,500,000             = $1,350,000
--------------------------------------------------
Value of PWA compliance              = $1,080,000

The $1,080,000 swing dwarfs the incremental cost of Davis-Bacon wages and apprentice hours on a project of this size. If domestic-content and energy-community adders also applied, Case B would rise to 50% ($2,250,000) while a PWA miss would cap those adders at 2% each — widening the gap further.

Compliance Checklist

  • [ ] Confirm project size in AC; if <1 MW, PWA not required (watch aggregation).
  • [ ] Determine construction-start year → correct apprentice labor-hour % (10%/12.5%/15%).
  • [ ] Pull the correct DOL wage determination(s); request conformances for missing classifications.
  • [ ] Flow Davis-Bacon + apprentice + certified-payroll obligations into all EPC/subcontracts.
  • [ ] Track certified payroll weekly for the taxpayer and every contractor/subcontractor.
  • [ ] Meet labor-hours, ratio, and participation apprentice tests; log good-faith requests.
  • [ ] Maintain prevailing wage on alterations/repairs during the 5-yr (ITC) / 10-yr (PTC) period.
  • [ ] If a shortfall is found, make correction + penalty payments promptly (avoid intentional disregard).
  • [ ] Retain all records through the full recapture/credit period for potential IRS exam.
