Kansas Solar Incentives & Policy

Solar in Kansas — net metering with system-size limits, a 100% renewable-energy property-tax exemption, and Evergy programs.

Net meteringSize limits
Property100% exempt
EvergyPrograms

Kansas offers a favorable property-tax exemption for renewable energy systems and mandates net metering for investor-owned utilities, but it has no state income-tax credit and no statewide sales-tax exemption for solar equipment. Recent rule changes affecting systems interconnected on or after January 1, 2026 meaningfully reduce the value of net metering for new customers, so timing and system sizing matter.

This guide covers both residential and commercial solar in Kansas. For background concepts, see Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures and Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Bottom line up front: Kansas's headline benefit is a 100% property-tax exemption for renewable-energy property. Net metering exists but was curtailed for systems interconnecting in 2026 and later. There is no state income-tax credit and no sales-tax exemption.

Overview

Kansas is a restructured-in-name-only, largely investor-owned-utility state. The dominant electric utility is Evergy (serving most of eastern and central Kansas), with Liberty Utilities (Empire District) in the southeast. Solar policy for these regulated utilities is set primarily by the Kansas Corporation Commission (KCC) and by statute (K.S.A. 66-1263 et seq.).

Kansas does not currently have an enforceable Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS); the former RPS was converted to a voluntary goal. That absence of a compliance mandate is the main reason Kansas has no SREC market and few state-funded incentives. The economic case for solar in Kansas therefore rests on the federal ITC (commercial) and residual federal support, net metering value, the property-tax exemption, and direct system economics.

Net Metering / Net Billing

Kansas statute requires investor-owned utilities (Evergy, Liberty) to offer net metering to customer-generators. Rural electric cooperatives and municipal utilities are generally not bound by the statewide mandate and set their own terms — always confirm with your specific provider.

System-size limits (export capacity) depend on when the system began operating:

Customer / vintage Export capacity limit
Residential, operating before July 1, 2014 25 kW
Larger commercial / governmental, older vintage 200 kW
Systems operating on/after July 1, 2024 Up to 150 kW, sized to historic consumption

Key 2026 change: For customers who interconnect on or after January 1, 2026, generation capacity is limited to 50% of export capacity, and energy-storage capacity (including EVs and portable storage) is generally excluded from sizing formulas unless the storage device can add export capacity. This materially reduces the value proposition for new net-metering customers. Verify the precise current mechanics at the KCC and DSIRE before sizing a system.

Kansas net metering has historically credited excess generation and reconciled periodically; the exact credit rate, rollover treatment, and any monthly reconciliation are set in each utility's tariff. Review your utility's net metering tariff carefully — see also Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

Residential vs. commercial: The same statutory framework applies, but commercial customers more often hit the export-capacity ceiling and may face demand charges that change the economics. Commercial customers should model against their full rate schedule, not just the energy charge.

SREC / RECs

Kansas has no solar carve-out and no active SREC compliance market. Because there is no enforceable RPS obligation, utilities are not required to purchase Solar Renewable Energy Certificates.

  • Residential: RECs from a home system generally have little to no monetary value in Kansas absent a voluntary buyer.
  • Commercial: Larger projects may sell unbundled RECs into voluntary/regional markets, but pricing is market-dependent and not guaranteed. Confirm current voluntary-market conditions before assuming REC revenue.

State Tax Credits

Kansas offers no state income-tax credit for solar — residential or commercial. There is no equivalent to the former federal residential credit at the state level.

Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are governed by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) and prior law:

  • Residential (§25D): The residential clean-energy credit ended after December 31, 2025. Systems placed in service on or after January 1, 2026 no longer qualify for the 30% residential credit. Homeowners who did not place a system in service by year-end 2025 cannot claim §25D.
  • Commercial (§48 / §48E): Businesses may still claim the commercial Investment Tax Credit under the technology-neutral §48E framework, subject to prevailing-wage/apprenticeship rules, domestic-content and other bonus adders, and OBBBA phase-down/timing provisions.

See Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits for the current commercial ITC rules, deadlines, and bonus structure. Confirm all federal deadlines against the IRS and current guidance.

Utility Incentives / Rebates

Kansas does not have a broad statewide solar rebate. Utility offerings are limited and change frequently:

  • Evergy: Historically has offered demand-response, time-of-use rate options, and periodic pilot programs rather than a standing per-watt solar rebate. Check the current Evergy rate schedules and clean-energy program pages for any active residential or commercial offerings.
  • Liberty Utilities (Empire District): Confirm current program availability directly.

Utility programs open, close, and change budgets frequently. Verify current Evergy / Liberty offerings on the utility website before relying on any rebate.

Sales Tax Exemption

Kansas has no statewide sales-tax exemption specific to solar-energy equipment for residential or commercial buyers. Standard state and local sales tax generally applies to equipment purchases. Verify current treatment at the Kansas Department of Revenue and DSIRE.

Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

This is Kansas's strongest solar incentive. Kansas statute provides a property-tax exemption for property used predominantly to produce and generate electricity from renewable-energy resources, including solar.

  • Residential and commercial: The exemption has been interpreted to apply to both residential and commercial solar systems — the added assessed value of a qualifying renewable-energy system is exempt from property tax (effectively a 100% exemption on the system's value).
  • Battery storage caveat: The exemption has been read not to extend to battery-storage systems in residential installations — meaning residential storage may still be assessable. Verify current scope with the Kansas Department of Revenue / county appraiser and DSIRE.

Because property-tax treatment can hinge on local assessment practice, commercial developers should confirm exemption eligibility with the county appraiser during due diligence.

Permitting & Interconnection Notes

  • Interconnection with Evergy/Liberty follows the utility's tariffed process; new interconnections on/after Jan 1, 2026 are subject to the revised net-metering sizing rules above.
  • Cooperatives and municipal utilities set their own interconnection and compensation rules — obtain their specific application packet early.
  • Permitting is handled at the local (city/county) level; requirements vary. Coordinate structural, electrical, and (for commercial) fire-access and setback requirements with the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ).
  • Commercial projects may trigger additional utility study and cost-allocation steps at larger sizes — budget time accordingly.

Bottom Line

  • Residential: With §25D gone after 2025, the Kansas residential case now leans on net metering (reduced for 2026+ interconnections) and the 100% property-tax exemption. There is no state income-tax credit, no sales-tax exemption, and only limited utility incentives. Model your utility's current net-metering tariff carefully.
  • Commercial: The federal §48E ITC remains the primary driver, stacked with the property-tax exemption and net metering up to the export-capacity limits. Watch the 50%-of-export-capacity rule for 2026 interconnections and confirm REC value before counting on it.
