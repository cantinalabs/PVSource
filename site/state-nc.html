North Carolina Solar Incentives & Policy

Solar in North Carolina — Duke Energy net metering and PowerPair, the 80% property-tax appraisal exclusion, and the (expired) state credit and sales-tax history.

State creditExpired
Property80% exclusion
DukePowerPair

North Carolina is a major solar state on the utility-scale side, but its distributed-solar incentives have narrowed sharply: the state solar tax credit is long expired, the sales-tax exemption was repealed, and Duke Energy's popular per-watt rebate has ended. What remains is a valuable property-tax exclusion, an active solar-plus-storage pilot (PowerPair), and a net-metering framework transitioning to net billing. This guide covers residential and commercial solar as of mid-2026. Confirm live figures with the administering agency and the Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency (DSIRE) before modeling a project.

For export-compensation mechanics, see Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures. For the federal side, see Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits. For comparisons, see More State Programs and More State Programs (Round 2).

Key correction: Some marketing still cites a "35% NC state solar tax credit" and a "solar sales-tax exemption." Both are outdated — the credit expired end of 2015 and the sales-tax exemption was repealed in 2014. Treat any such claim as inaccurate and verify.

Overview

North Carolina's three investor-owned utilities are Duke Energy Carolinas (DEC), Duke Energy Progress (DEP), and Dominion Energy North Carolina (northeastern NC). Policy is set by the North Carolina Utilities Commission (NCUC), with the Public Staff representing consumers, and the NC Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) State Energy Office on the program side. Cooperatives and municipal utilities are not NCUC-regulated and set their own terms.

Net Metering / Net Billing

Duke Energy (DEC + DEP)

Driven by 2017's HB 589 (N.C.G.S. § 62-126.4) requiring net-metering customers to "pay their full fixed cost of service," Duke is transitioning off legacy 1:1 net metering:

  • Legacy Rider NM — Closed to new residential applicants Sept. 30, 2023. Grandfathered customers can remain until Dec. 31, 2026, then auto-transition to Rider NMB. Retail 1:1 credit with annual true-up.
  • Rider RSC (Residential Solar Choice) — the going-forward standard rider (effective Oct. 1, 2023). Introduces mandatory Time-of-Use with Critical Peak Pricing (TOU-CPP), a monthly minimum bill, non-bypassable charges, and a grid-access fee for systems larger than 15 kW-AC. Net exports are credited monthly at avoided-cost (Net Excess Energy Credit), not retail.
  • Rider NMB (Net Metering Bridge) — same as RSC but without mandatory TOU-CPP; capacity-limited per year (once the annual cap fills, new customers take RSC). Customers may stay on NMB up to 15 years from their interconnection-request date.

Representative values (verify against the current filed tariff before quoting):

Item DEC (approx) DEP (approx)
Grid-access fee (≥15 kW-AC) ~$2.05/kW ~$1.50/kW
Monthly minimum bill ~$22 ~$28
Non-bypassable charges ~$0.44/kW-mo ~$0.36/kW-mo

All figures above are from secondary sources and are periodically updated — verify at Duke's current rates page.

Residential vs. Commercial

Statutory net-metering eligibility caps are 20 kW-AC or less for residential and 1 MW-AC or less for non-residential. Larger commercial DG interconnects but is served under commercial rate schedules with the same fixed-cost-recovery logic (minimum bill / grid access). Verify commercial specifics against current commercial tariff filings.

Dominion Energy North Carolina

Regulated separately; retains traditional 1:1 retail-rate net metering for its NC territory, with monthly carry-forward and annual credit expiration (June 1). Residential cap ~25 kW; application fee ~$200 for systems under 20 kW (verify).

See Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures for how TOU, minimum bills, and avoided-cost exports change project payback.

SREC / RECs

North Carolina's portfolio standard — originally the REPS, now the broadened Clean Energy and Energy Efficiency Portfolio Standard (CEPS) after SB 678 (2023) — is tracked via NC-RETS. In practice the state's REC market functions as an oversupplied voluntary market, and the 2023 CEPS broadening (adding nuclear, large hydro, etc.) further eroded solar-specific demand.

Do not count SRECs as a meaningful NC solar revenue stream — residential SRECs are effectively near-zero value. See SREC Markets Deep-Dive for how real SREC markets differ.

State Tax Credits

None currently active.

  • The 35% NC Renewable Energy Investment Tax Credit (former G.S. § 105-129.16A) expired December 31, 2015 and was not renewed.
  • There was no NC state solar tax credit that expired at end of 2024 — that framing conflates the state credit with the federal §25D credit (which ended Dec. 31, 2025). No NC state ITC was active in 2024 or 2025.
  • Reenactment bills have been introduced but, as of mid-2026, none have been enacted. Monitor the NC General Assembly; verify before assuming any revived credit.

Federal Tax Credits

  • Residential (§25D): The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit ended after December 31, 2025 under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally cannot claim it — and no NC state credit backfills it.
  • Commercial (ITC / §48E): The technology-neutral Clean Electricity Investment Credit generally remains available for commercial and utility-scale projects, subject to phase-out schedules, wage/apprenticeship rules, and bonus adders. See Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Utility Incentives / Rebates

Duke NC Solar Rebate ($0.40/W era) — ENDED

Duke's popular NC Solar Rebate Program (created under HB 589; originally $0.60/W residential, later ~$0.40/W and lower; annual capacity blocks) has ended and is closed to new applicants. Do not quote it as available. (Duke's old rebate page may still exist; verify no residual block.)

PowerPair (solar + battery pilot) — ACTIVE

Duke's NCUC-approved PowerPair pilot pairs new rooftop solar with battery storage:

PowerPair Component Incentive
Solar $0.36/W-DC up to 10 kW-AC → max $3,600
Battery $400/kWh up to 13.5 kWh → max $5,400
Combined max ~$9,000
  • Availability: Duke Energy Progress (DEP) is fully allocated (waitlist only); Duke Energy Carolinas (DEC) has limited capacity remaining. Verify current headroom at Duke's PowerPair page.
  • Participants also select a net-metering rider (RSC/NMB) and specific export/control terms. A commercial PowerPair variant exists — verify separately.

Sales Tax Exemption

Does not currently exist. The former renewable-energy sales/use tax exemption (G.S. § 105-164.13(44)) was repealed effective July 1, 2014. Solar equipment is subject to NC's 4.75% state sales tax plus local sales taxes.

Flag: DSIRE and some installer sites still show a "Sales and Use Tax Exemption for Renewable Energy" entry — this reflects the repealed/historical provision, not current law. Verify against NCDOR (G.S. 105-164.13).

Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

North Carolina's G.S. § 105-275(45) classifies solar energy electric systems as a special class and excludes 80% of appraised value from property tax (only 20% of added value is taxable), for the life of the system.

  • Residential: Systems not used for business/income are typically classified as non-business personal property, which is exempt — so residential solar effectively gets a ~100% exclusion in practice.
  • Commercial / income-producing: The 80% exclusion applies strictly — 20% of appraised value is added to taxable property (e.g., a $100k commercial system → ~$20k taxable).
  • Administrative: Not all counties apply it automatically; file NCDOR Form AV-10 with the county assessor.

Legislative risk: Bills have been introduced proposing to repeal the 80% exclusion for systems placed in service on/after July 1, 2027 (existing systems grandfathered). Not yet law — verify if a project's in-service date is near/after mid-2027.

Permitting & Interconnection Notes

  • Interconnection standards are set by the NCUC (based on FERC small-generator standards) and govern the three IOUs; last major update 2019. Standard fee schedule (verify current amounts): $500 pre-application report; $100 for ≤20 kW; $750 for >20–100 kW; $1,000 for >100 kW–2 MW.
  • SolarAPP+: No confirmed statewide adoption; permitting remains AHJ-by-AHJ. Verify with the specific municipality/county.
  • Electrical code: Most jurisdictions operate under the 2020 NEC with NC amendments as of early 2026; 2023 NEC adoption pending. Verify per AHJ.
  • Cooperatives and municipal utilities set their own interconnection and net-metering terms.

Bottom Line

In North Carolina, the durable distributed-solar value is now the property-tax exclusion (effectively ~100% residential, 80% commercial) plus the PowerPair solar-plus-storage incentive while capacity lasts. The state tax credit and sales-tax exemption are gone, Duke's per-watt rebate has ended, SRECs are effectively worthless, and net metering is transitioning to a TOU / avoided-cost net-billing structure with the legacy 1:1 rider closing Dec. 31, 2026. Model new systems on RSC/NMB terms, not legacy net metering, and confirm PowerPair availability early.
