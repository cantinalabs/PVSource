Residential SRECs for Homeowners

How homeowners in SREC states (NJ, MD, DC, PA, IL, OH, MA, VA) earn and sell solar certificates — registration, aggregators, and the income they add.

1 SREC= 1 MWh
SREC statesNJ · MD · DC…
AggregatorsSell them

In a handful of states, a homeowner's solar array can generate a second stream of income — separate from bill savings — by producing and selling Solar Renewable Energy Certificates (SRECs). This page explains how homeowners in SREC states earn, register, and sell SRECs, the role of aggregators, the income they add, and how SRECs differ from performance-based incentives. For the market mechanics in depth, see the site's SREC Markets Deep-Dive.

SRECs exist only where a state requires them. They come from a state Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) with a solar carve-out — a legal mandate that utilities source a rising share of electricity from solar. Prices are set by supply and demand in that state's market and can be volatile. Verify whether your state has an active residential market on DSIRE.

What an SREC Is

One SREC represents the environmental attributes of 1 megawatt-hour (1,000 kWh) of solar generation. It is separate from the electricity itself — you sell the certificate while still using or exporting the actual energy (which is credited under net metering / net billing).

  1 SREC = 1 MWh (1,000 kWh) of metered solar production

  A typical 7-8 kW home system producing ~9,000-11,000 kWh/yr
  generates roughly 9-11 SRECs per year.

Utilities that fall short of their RPS solar obligation must buy SRECs (or pay an Alternative Compliance Payment, which effectively caps SREC prices). That obligation is what gives SRECs value.

States With Residential SREC (or SREC-like) Markets

As of late 2025, states where homeowners can earn and sell SRECs or fixed solar incentives include the following. Values change frequently — verify current pricing before relying on it.

State Program / market Notes (as commonly cited, late 2025)
Washington D.C. SREC market Historically the highest-value market (SREC-I bids near $383 in 2025)
New Jersey SuSI / SREC-II (ADI) Fixed incentive (~$85 per MWh) for 15 years — see NJ SuSI

Illinois Adjustable Block Program / Illinois Shines Pays for RECs up front over a term; strong LMI carve-out (see Low-Income & Equity Solar Programs)

Note the distinction: New Jersey's SuSI and Massachusetts SMART are fixed / administratively set incentives, not open tradable SREC markets. True tradable SREC markets (D.C., MD, PA, OH, VA, DE) have prices that float with supply and demand.

How a Homeowner Earns and Sells SRECs

  1. Confirm eligibility. Your system must be located in (or eligible to sell into) a qualifying state market. Some out-of-state systems can sell into a neighboring market — verify.
  2. Register the system with the state's program and its generation-tracking system (e.g., PJM-GATS in the Mid-Atlantic). Registration requires system details, interconnection proof, and often meter data.
  3. Meter production. SRECs are minted based on actual metered kWh — every 1,000 kWh yields one SREC.
  4. Sell the SRECs, typically through an aggregator/broker (see below), on either a spot basis or a fixed-price forward contract for several years.
  5. Report income. SREC sales are generally taxable income — keep records and consult a tax professional.

Aggregators and How They Help

Most homeowners don't sell SRECs directly; they use an aggregator that pools many small systems, handles registration, and manages the sales.

Aggregator (examples) Typical service
Xpansiv (formerly SRECTrade) Large national platform; manages many residential accounts
Sol Systems Mid-Atlantic focus (MD, D.C., VA); brokerage plus contract options
Flett Exchange Hands-on D.C./MD platform; per-transaction fee model
Carbon Solutions Group Multi-state (VA, MD, NJ, PA, OH, etc.)
  • Fixed-price contracts trade some upside for predictable income and are easier to finance around.
  • Spot / self-directed sales chase the market price but carry volatility and effort.
  • Aggregators charge a fee or brokerage spread — compare before signing.

The Income SRECs Add — Example

  8 kW system in a market with SRECs at ~$60 each:
  Annual production .............. ~10,000 kWh = 10 SRECs
  SREC income .................... 10 x $60 = ~$600/yr

  Same system in D.C. (~$380/SREC):
  SREC income .................... 10 x $380 = ~$3,800/yr

Volatility warning. SREC prices can fall sharply as a state's solar target fills up and supply outpaces demand — Pennsylvania and Ohio illustrate how markets compress over time. Treat spot SREC income as variable, not guaranteed. New Jersey and Massachusetts addressed this by switching to fixed incentives.

SRECs vs. Performance-Based Incentives (PBIs)

Both pay you based on production, but they differ:

Feature SREC (tradable) Performance-based incentive (PBI)
Price Set by market supply/demand; volatile Fixed / administratively set ($/kWh or $/MWh)
Certainty Lower Higher
Examples D.C., MD, PA, OH, VA, DE markets NJ SuSI, MA SMART, IL Shines block payments
Who bears price risk The homeowner (unless under contract) The program/ratepayers

Many "SREC states" have actually transitioned to PBIs precisely to remove homeowner price risk. When evaluating a system, treat SREC/PBI income as additive to your net-metering savings, state credits, and utility rebates — and remember the federal §25D credit ended after 2025 for homeowner-owned systems.
