Rhode Island Solar Incentives & Policy

Solar in Rhode Island — net metering plus the Renewable Energy Growth tariff, REF grants, and sales- and property-tax exemptions.

REG + NMTariffs
REFGrants
ExemptionsSales & property

For a small state, Rhode Island offers an unusually deep incentive stack: net metering, the tariff-based Renewable Energy Growth (REG) performance program, upfront Renewable Energy Fund (REF) grants, and permanent sales and property tax exemptions. Solar customers choose either net metering or REG (not both), and REF grants pair only with net metering. There is no state income tax credit.

The central decision in Rhode Island is net metering + REF grant vs. REG tariff — you cannot combine REG with net metering, and REF grants only pair with net metering. Model both paths before committing.

Overview

Rhode Island's distributed-solar programs are administered by Rhode Island Energy (RIE, formerly National Grid), with policy oversight from the Office of Energy Resources (OER), the Public Utilities Commission (PUC), and grant administration by Rhode Island Commerce Corporation. The state pairs net metering with two distinctive programs — the REG performance tariff and the REF grant — plus permanent tax exemptions.

Policy in active flux (2026). RIE and OER are conducting a comprehensive review of net metering and REG, and pending FY2027 budget/legislative proposals could change Virtual Net Metering caps and large-system net metering. Residential rooftop solar is reportedly not the target, but verify current terms — especially for large or commercial projects — against enacted law and PUC dockets.

Net Metering / Net Billing

Net metering remains available through RIE:

  • Customers receive bill credits for generation up to 125% of on-site consumption; systems are sized to load based on a 3-year consumption average.
  • Maximum eligible system size: 10 MW.
  • Credit reduction: for projects initiated after April 15, 2023, the renewable net-metering credit is reduced by 20% (subject to a program capacity cap).
  • Excess beyond 100% of consumption is credited at the utility's lower avoided-cost rate.
  • Virtual Net Metering (VNM): allows off-site credits for public entities, educational institutions, hospitals, municipalities, and commercial/industrial customers (up to 10 MW per project site).
  • Community Remote Net Metering (CRNM): requires at least 50% allocation to low/moderate-income or disadvantaged communities; a revised "CRNM v2" with a new cap is expected in 2026–2027 (verify timing/status).

Verify quantified figures. The 20% reduction is the statutory mechanism; any specific cents-per-kWh net-metering value cited by third parties is illustrative. Confirm current caps, the credit-reduction rule, and pending 2026 legislative changes against OER/PUC sources — verify at DSIRE/official site.

For how net metering crediting affects payback, see Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

Renewable Energy Growth (REG) Program

REG is a tariff-based performance program administered by RIE and overseen by the Distributed Generation Contracts Board, revised annually:

  • Participants sell their generation under a long-term, fixed-price tariff (15–20 year contracts), with the rate locked at enrollment. Credits offset the bill; excess is paid out directly.
  • Small-Scale REG covers homeowners and small commercial (projects ≤25 kW); the program year opens April 1 annually, first-come first-served. Payments are taxable income (a W-9 is required).
  • Commercial-Scale REG covers larger solar (and other technologies) during periodic enrollment windows.
  • REG vs. net metering: a customer must choose one — the two cannot be combined.

2026 ceiling prices. Per Commerce RI, RIE will pay either 33.85 cents/kWh or 32.35 cents/kWh in program year 2026 depending on project size and contract term. Third-party sites citing a ~$0.27/kWh residential figure conflict with the official numbers; for exact per-class/per-term rates, consult RIE's current REG tariff documents — verify at DSIRE/official site.

Renewable Energy Fund (REF)

REF is an upfront grant program administered by Rhode Island Commerce Corporation, funded by the electric-bill system benefit charge:

  • The grant discounts the cost of a net-metered system; installers apply on the homeowner's behalf. REF cannot be paired with REG.
  • 2026 grant rounds run in multiple windows (spring, summer, fall) across Small-Scale (residential), Commercial-Scale, Brownfields, and other tracks.
  • A new Energy Storage Adder is offered for both small-scale and commercial applicants.

Grant dollar amounts not officially published on the summary page, and third-party figures conflict widely (e.g., differing per-watt rates and caps). Do not rely on a specific REF dollar amount without confirming via the current REF funding flyer / Request for Projects documents on the Commerce RI site — verify at DSIRE/official site.

SREC / RECs

Rhode Island has a Renewable Energy Standard (RES) but no solar carve-out, so there is no viable standalone SREC market (unlike Massachusetts). Compliance uses general RECs traded in the New England (NEPOOL-GIS) Class I market. Under the REG program, RECs are conveyed to the utility, so REG participants do not separately sell certificates. See the SREC Markets Deep-Dive for how solar certificate markets work elsewhere.

State Tax Credits

Rhode Island offers no state income tax credit for residential or commercial solar. The historic state solar/renewable credit expired years ago; the state relies on REG, REF, net metering, and tax exemptions instead.

Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are separate from state policy:

  • Residential (§25D): The 30% federal residential clean energy credit ended after December 31, 2025 under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally do not qualify.
  • Commercial (ITC / §48E): Business-owned solar may still qualify for the commercial investment tax credit and the technology-neutral §48E credit, subject to current rules.

See Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Utility Incentives / Rebates

  • Rhode Island Energy offers ConnectedSolutions, a battery demand-response program that pays participants for battery dispatch during peak events (per-kW seasonal payments; confirm current rates).
  • OER offers energy storage incentives; the REF Energy Storage Adder also supports batteries.

Sales Tax Exemption

Solar and renewable energy equipment is exempt from Rhode Island's 7% sales and use tax, applied at point of sale by the installer.

The exemption is codified under RI sales/use tax law (R.I. Gen. Laws § 44-18-30). Confirm the exact statutory subsection and whether battery storage and installation labor are covered against the RI General Laws — verify at DSIRE/official site.

Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Residential renewable energy systems are exempt from local property tax under R.I. Gen. Laws § 44-3-21 — the added value of the solar system is excluded from assessment.

Commercial/tangible property treatment varies by municipality; some towns publish their own solar taxation guidance. Commercial developers should confirm treatment with the local tax assessor. Any "20-year" framing seen in third-party guides should be checked against the statute — verify at DSIRE/official site.

Permitting & Interconnection Notes

  • Interconnection applications are submitted electronically via RIE's Interconnection portal (also used for Small-Scale REG). RIE publishes the interconnection standards there.
  • Local building/electrical permits are issued by each city/town building department. A 2026 "instant solar permitting" bill would create a statewide automated permitting platform — verify enactment status.
  • Solar retailer registration: Under R.I. Gen. Laws § 5-93-4 (Residential Solar Energy Disclosure and Homeowners Bill of Rights Act), residential solar retailers must maintain annual registration with the RI Department of Business Regulation — a current requirement worth confirming when choosing an installer.

Bottom Line

Rhode Island punches above its weight: homeowners can pair net metering with a REF grant, or opt into the REG performance tariff (2026 ceilings around 32–34 cents/kWh) — but not both. Permanent sales and property tax exemptions add durable value, while there is no state income tax credit and no SREC market. With the federal residential §25D credit gone after 2025, the REG/REF choice and net metering value become even more central to 2026 residential economics; businesses can still pursue the commercial ITC/§48E. Because several 2026 legislative changes are pending, confirm all program terms — especially REF grant amounts and REG rates — against official sources before signing.

Educational use only. This page is a general reference, not tax, legal, or financial advice. Incentive values, tariff rates, and program rules change frequently. Verify all figures with OER, the PUC, Rhode Island Commerce Corporation, Rhode Island Energy, the RI Division of Taxation, and a qualified professional before making decisions.
