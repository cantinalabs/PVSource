Selling or Buying a Home with Solar

Solar at closing — how owned systems affect home value and appraisal, and how to transfer or buy out a lease or PPA, the most common deal-closing snag.

Home valueOwned systems
Lease transferThe snag
ChecklistsBuyer & seller

Solar can add value to a home — or add friction to a sale — depending almost entirely on how the system was financed. An owned, paid-off system is generally an asset. A leased or PPA system, or one with an outstanding loan and lien, introduces steps that can delay or derail a closing if they are not handled early. This page covers appraised value, lender and appraiser treatment, transferring or buying out third-party contracts, the documents each party needs, disclosures, and UCC-1 liens — with checklists for both sellers and buyers.

Related reading: Homeowner Financing 101, Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection, and Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

Does solar add to home value?

Research on owned systems generally finds a resale premium, but with important caveats.

  • Zillow (2019): Homes with solar sold for about 4.1% more on average than comparable homes without — roughly $9,300 more for the median-valued U.S. home at the time, with wide regional variation (e.g., ~5.4% in the NYC metro, ~2.7% in Riverside, CA).
  • Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory ("Selling Into the Sun," 2015): Across ~22,000 sales in eight states, buyers paid a premium of roughly $4 per watt for homes with host-owned PV — about $15,000 for a typical system of that era.

Big caveats

These studies measure owned systems. The premium is not a fixed number — it varies by region, market, system age, size, and how the appraisal is handled.

  • Leased/PPA systems generally add little or no value premium. Because a third party owns the equipment, it is a contractual obligation transferred to the buyer, not an asset. It can even reduce the buyer pool.
  • Premiums shrink as systems age and as remaining warranty/production declines.
  • A premium only materializes if the appraiser and buyer recognize the value — which requires documentation (below).

Appraiser and lender treatment

  • Owned systems can be included in an appraisal, but the appraiser needs data (system size, age, production, ownership proof) and ideally comparable sales. Appraisers may use tools such as the PV Value® methodology to estimate contributory value. If you give the appraiser nothing, they may assign no added value.
  • Financed (owned but with a loan/lien): The loan is usually paid off at or before closing from the seller's proceeds, and any lien is released so title is clear. The system then conveys free and clear to the buyer.
  • Leased/PPA systems are not counted toward home value by most lenders/appraisers, because the equipment is not owned. Lenders may also scrutinize whether the lease payment affects the buyer's debt-to-income qualification.
  • PACE liens are a special problem: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac generally will not buy mortgages on homes with an outstanding PACE assessment, so a PACE balance often must be paid off at closing or it can block conventional financing entirely. See Homeowner Financing 101.

The most common closing snag: leases and PPAs

When a home has a leased or PPA system, one of two things must happen at sale:

  1. Assumption (transfer): The buyer assumes the remaining contract. The buyer must credit-qualify with the solar provider, and both parties sign a transfer/assumption agreement. Transfer fees commonly run $100–$500+.
  2. Prepayment / buyout: The seller buys out the remaining term (pays a lump-sum buyout figure from the provider) so the system conveys free and clear — or has the provider remove it.

Start this process early — ideally the moment the home is listed. Provider transfer/buyout requests can take weeks, and a stalled solar transfer is one of the most frequent last-minute causes of a delayed solar-home closing. Get the exact buyout amount and transfer requirements in writing from the provider up front.

UCC-1 liens and fixture filings

When a system is financed (solar loan) or third-party owned (lease/PPA), the financier often files a UCC-1 financing statement (sometimes a fixture filing recorded against the property) to protect its interest in the equipment.

  • A UCC-1 is a lien on the solar equipment, generally not on the home itself — but a fixture filing appears in the property records and will surface in a title search.
  • A title company will typically require the UCC-1 to be terminated/released (for a paid-off loan) or subordinated/handled (for a lease that is being assumed) before it will insure clear title.
  • Seller action: If you paid off the loan, confirm the lender filed a UCC-3 termination. Lingering UCC-1 filings after payoff are common and can hold up closing.

Documents buyers and sellers need

Gather these early; missing paperwork is a frequent source of delay:

Document Why it matters
Proof of ownership or the lease/PPA contract Determines whether the system is an asset or an obligation
Loan payoff statement / UCC-3 termination Clears any lien for owned-but-financed systems
Lease/PPA transfer packet and buyout figure Enables assumption or prepayment
Interconnection agreement with the utility Confirms the system is legal and grid-connected; may need re-signing by the buyer
Manufacturer and workmanship warranties Transferable warranties add buyer confidence (see Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection)
Monitoring portal access Lets the buyer verify production and manage the system
Production history / bills Documents actual savings for the appraiser and buyer
Permits and inspection sign-offs Proves the install was permitted and code-compliant
Insurance details So the buyer can arrange coverage (see Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection)

Disclosures

Sellers should disclose the solar arrangement to buyers. Many states require disclosing:

  • Whether the system is owned, financed, leased, or under a PPA
  • Any remaining balance, lien, escalator, or transfer requirement
  • The net-metering/net-billing status — and whether the buyer keeps the same tariff or is moved to a current (often less favorable, e.g., NEM 3.0) tariff. Grandfathered net-metering agreements may or may not transfer; verify with the utility. See Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

Failing to disclose a lease obligation or a lien can create legal liability and blow up a deal at the last minute. When in doubt, over-disclose.

Seller's checklist

  • [ ] Determine your ownership status (cash-owned, loan-owned, lease, PPA, PACE).
  • [ ] If financed/owned: get a loan payoff statement and plan to pay off and terminate the UCC-1 at closing.
  • [ ] If leased/PPA: contact the provider immediately for transfer requirements and the buyout figure.
  • [ ] If PACE: get the assessment payoff; expect it may need to be cleared for the buyer to obtain conventional financing.
  • [ ] Gather warranties, interconnection agreement, permits, monitoring access, and production history.
  • [ ] Confirm the net-metering/tariff transfer rules with the utility.
  • [ ] Disclose the solar arrangement fully to the buyer.

Buyer's checklist

  • [ ] Identify whether the system is owned or third-party (lease/PPA/PACE) — this changes everything.
  • [ ] For leases/PPAs: read the contract for the escalator, remaining term, transfer fee, buyout, and production guarantee; confirm you can credit-qualify to assume it.
  • [ ] Verify the buyout/transfer is completed (or the balance paid off) before closing.
  • [ ] Confirm any UCC-1/fixture filing is being terminated or properly handled by the title company.
  • [ ] Obtain and review all warranties — and confirm they transfer to you.
  • [ ] Get the interconnection agreement and confirm your net-metering/net-billing tariff as the new owner.
  • [ ] Set up monitoring access and add the system to your homeowners insurance (see Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection).
  • [ ] For PACE: confirm your lender will finance the home despite the assessment (many will not).
