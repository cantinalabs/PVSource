Sourcing Materials & Kits

Where to acquire components — distributors and brands by segment, kits vs components, new vs used panels, the certification and warranty risks, and a buyer's checklist.

Kits vs partsHow to buy
New vs usedCertification risk
ChecklistBefore you buy

Acquiring the right components is half the battle in a do-it-yourself solar build. The DIY market has matured: there are solar-specific distributors that cater explicitly to homeowners and off-gridders, manufacturer-direct storefronts, big-box retailers, marketplaces, and a busy secondary market in used and B-grade panels. This guide explains how to buy, where to buy, and what to watch for, then names well-known vendors and brands by segment so you can orient yourself.

This page pairs with Building Your Own System (the project-planning overview) and Solar System Components (what each part actually does). Read those first if you are still deciding what to buy; this page is about acquiring it.

This guide is educational and is not an endorsement of any seller or brand. Vendor names, ownership, and product lines change. Always verify current pricing, stock, seller reputation, warranties, certifications, and return/restocking policies before you purchase. Markets move quickly — treat every price you see as a snapshot, not a fact.

How to Buy: Two Procurement Paths

Component-by-component

You select each part individually — panels, inverter, batteries, racking, wire, connectors, fuses, breakers, and balance-of-system hardware — and assemble the bill of materials yourself.

  • Pros: Maximum control, best price optimization, easy to mix the exact brands you want, and you learn the system deeply.
  • Cons: You own compatibility risk (voltage windows, communications protocols, connector types), shipping logistics, and the chance of forgetting a small-but-required part that stalls inspection.
  • Best for: Builders comfortable reading spec sheets, off-grid/ground-mount projects with non-standard layouts, and anyone optimizing cost.

Pre-engineered kits / bundles

A vendor packages a matched set — typically panels, inverter or all-in-one unit, battery, racking, and wiring — sized to a target output. Many solar-specific distributors sell these.

  • Pros: Components are pre-matched for compatibility; wiring diagrams and parts lists are usually included; fewer ordering mistakes; faster to permit when the kit ships with listed equipment and documentation.
  • Cons: Less flexibility, you may pay for parts you do not need, and "kit" quality varies — confirm the kit is genuinely engineered, not just a cart bundle.
  • Best for: First-time builders, RV/van and tiny-home projects, and grid-tied roof systems where a permit office wants a clean, documented package.

New vs. used / B-grade panels

The secondary market (decommissioned utility panels, cosmetic-second "B-grade," overstock, and surplus) can be dramatically cheaper per watt, but it carries real risks.

Consideration New panels Used / B-grade / surplus
Price per watt Higher Often much lower
Manufacturer warranty Typically 12–25 yr product / 25–30 yr performance Usually void, non-transferable, or expired
Certification paperwork Current UL/IEC listing, datasheet, flash-test data May be missing, outdated, or for a discontinued model
Permit acceptance Generally straightforward Frequently rejected if not currently listed
Insurance acceptance Generally straightforward May be excluded if gear is uncertified/unlisted
Best use Permitted grid-tie and primary off-grid Off-grid/experimental loads where permitting and warranty matter less

Buyer beware on used and uncertified gear. Many jurisdictions require equipment that is currently listed to a recognized standard. A used panel whose model is no longer on the certification list — or whose listing you cannot document — can fail inspection and can give a homeowner's insurer grounds to deny a fire claim. Used panels are best reserved for off-grid or hobby loads where you are not seeking a permit or grid interconnection. Always ask the seller for the model number, datasheet, and any flash-test results, and inspect for microcracks, delamination, and junction-box damage.

What "UL listed" and "IEC certified" mean for you

  • UL listed (e.g., UL 1703 / UL 61730 for modules, UL 1741 for inverters, UL 9540 for energy storage systems) means a recognized testing lab has evaluated the product to a safety standard. Permit offices, utilities, and insurers in North America commonly require it.
  • IEC certified (e.g., IEC 61215 performance, IEC 61730 safety) is the international counterpart, common on imported modules; many products carry both.
  • Why it matters: Listing is frequently the line between a system that passes inspection and gets interconnected versus one that does not. If a product is "self-certified," "for off-grid use only," or its listing cannot be documented, assume your permit office and insurer may reject it. Verify the listing on the product datasheet and, where possible, on the certifying body's directory.

Balance-of-system components

  • Panels: Major Tier-1 module makers (e.g., the large international manufacturers your distributor stocks) for new, currently-listed modules; surplus sellers for used/B-grade (see the warranty and permit caveats above).
  • Connectors and wire: Genuine MC4 (and MC4-compatible) connectors and properly rated PV wire/USE-2 — buy from reputable suppliers, as counterfeit connectors are a known fire risk.
  • Overcurrent protection and combiners: MidNite Solar for DC breakers, combiner/disconnect boxes, and Classic-series MPPT charge controllers (US-made; verify current product line).
  • Fuses, busbars, lugs, grounding hardware, and disconnects: source to the correct DC voltage/current rating; do not substitute AC-only parts on DC circuits.

Buyer's Checklist

  • Certifications: Confirm modules carry current UL 61730/UL 1703 or IEC 61215/61730 listing; inverters UL 1741; storage UL 9540 where applicable. Get the datasheet.
  • Warranty: Verify length, what it covers (product vs. performance), and whether it transfers to you (critical for used/B-grade).
  • Compatibility: Match inverter MPPT voltage/current windows to your string design, and confirm battery-to-inverter communication is on a documented compatibility list.
  • Connectors and wire: Use genuine, correctly rated MC4/PV wire; avoid mismatched-brand connector pairs and unbranded "MC4-like" parts.
  • Shipping and freight: Panels usually ship by freight (LTL) on a pallet — confirm cost, liftgate, residential delivery, and inspect for breakage before signing.
  • Permit and insurance acceptance: Ask your AHJ (authority having jurisdiction) and insurer what equipment listings they require before purchasing, especially for used or imported gear.
  • Seller reputation and returns: Check independent reviews, confirm the return/restocking policy, and keep all invoices, serial numbers, and certification documents for inspection and insurance.
