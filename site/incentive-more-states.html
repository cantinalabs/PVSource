More State Programs

Commercial-solar incentive profiles for more states — Maryland, Minnesota, Connecticut, Colorado and Oregon — with mechanisms, eligibility, status and links to DSIRE.

This guide profiles commercial-solar incentive structures in several additional states beyond the flagship programs covered elsewhere on the site. Each profile is deliberately concise: it names the primary program, explains the incentive mechanism, notes commercial eligibility, flags current status, and links to the authoritative source. Programs change frequently — always confirm live rates, block levels, and enrollment windows with the administering agency or the Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency (DSIRE) before modeling a project.

For federal-plus-state stacking and depreciation interactions, see State Tax Credits & Incentives. For a deeper treatment of certificate markets, see SREC Markets Deep-Dive.

Maryland

Maryland pairs a renewable portfolio standard (RPS) solar carve-out with tax exemptions. The RPS creates a tradable SREC market: 1 SREC = 1 MWh of solar generation, sold to load-serving entities that must meet the solar carve-out or pay the Solar Alternative Compliance Payment (SACP).

  • SREC market — Maryland SRECs trade on a spot/contract basis. The 2025 SACP is approximately $55 (declining on a statutory schedule), which acts as a soft price ceiling. Standard Maryland SRECs have traded in the low-$50s range recently (approximate — verify). Under a Certified SREC multiplier effective January 1, 2025, qualifying in-state projects placed in service between mid-2024 and 2028 can earn up to 1.5× the market rate, so Certified SRECs have traded meaningfully higher. Verify current values at DSIRE — Maryland.
  • Sales & use tax exemption — Solar energy equipment is exempt from Maryland's 6% sales and use tax.
  • Property tax exemption — Solar PV is exempt from state and local real property tax; it may not increase assessed value.
  • Grant status — The Maryland Energy Administration's legacy Commercial Clean Energy Rebate Program (C-CERP) was not offered in FY25 and is not planned to reopen. MEA has directed commercial applicants to successor grant offerings that rotate by fiscal year. Confirm current grant availability at Maryland Energy Administration — Business Incentives.

Commercial takeaway: In Maryland the recurring value is the SREC stream plus tax exemptions, not a fixed upfront grant. Model SRECs conservatively given price volatility and the SACP ceiling.

Minnesota

Minnesota's commercial value comes chiefly from Xcel Energy programs, a value-of-solar / community solar framework, and a property-tax exemption. Minnesota does not run a statewide SREC market.

  • Xcel Solar*Rewards (and Solar*Rewards for Business) — A performance-based incentive paying a per-kWh production payment (historically around $0.03/kWh for a multi-year term, layered on top of net metering) (approximate — verify current block/rate). Availability is capacity-limited and typically opens in annual application windows. See DSIRE — Xcel Solar*Rewards.
  • Community Solar Gardens (Solar*Rewards Community, now the "Melissa Hortman" program) — Commercial subscribers or developers participate via a value-of-solar (VOS) tariff bill credit rather than an SREC. The program was substantially restructured and is administered through the Minnesota Department of Commerce. See MN Commerce — Community Solar Gardens.
  • Property tax exemption — Solar systems are exempt from property tax on the value the panels add.
  • Sales tax exemption — Solar equipment qualifies for exemption from Minnesota sales tax.

Commercial takeaway: A commercial host in Xcel territory typically stacks net metering, a Solar*Rewards production payment (while blocks last), and tax exemptions; larger off-taker or developer roles run through community solar/VOS rather than a certificate market.

Connecticut

Connecticut consolidated its net metering and REC payments into the Non-Residential Renewable Energy Solutions (NRES) tariff, administered by the utilities (Eversource and United Illuminating) under PURA oversight. NRES replaced the older LREC/ZREC program.

  • NRES mechanism — Projects bid into periodic RFPs (roughly February and August) for a fixed tariff rate that then holds for a 20-year contract. Applicants choose a Buy-All (export all generation at the tariff) or Netting (offset on-site load) tariff structure. The program is authorized for a multi-year run selecting up to roughly 100 MW of clean energy annually, with separate carve-outs (e.g., public-school solar). Price caps by project size have been adjusted to reflect federal ITC changes. See PURA — NRES and Eversource — NRES.
  • Sales & use tax exemption — Solar equipment is exempt from Connecticut sales and use tax.
  • Property tax exemption — Class I renewable systems serving eligible loads are exempt from property tax.

Commercial takeaway: NRES is a fixed, long-term, bid-set tariff — closer to a performance-based incentive than a volatile SREC. Bid timing and price-cap category matter; secure an approved rate to lock 20 years of certainty.

Colorado

Colorado combines the investor-owned utility's Solar*Rewards incentive with strong statewide tax treatment. There is no statewide SREC spot market comparable to the Mid-Atlantic.

  • Xcel Solar*Rewards — Xcel Energy's performance/upfront incentive for qualifying customers, with enhanced amounts for income-qualified and disproportionately-impacted communities. Commercial and small-business tracks are capacity-limited. See DSIRE — Colorado.
  • Sales & use tax exemption — Colorado provides a statewide 100% sales and use tax exemption for qualifying solar equipment (local jurisdiction treatment may vary).
  • Property tax exemption — Qualifying renewable systems (commonly systems under 2 MW) receive a 100% property tax exemption on the added value.

Commercial takeaway: The dependable value in Colorado is the tax exemption stack plus utility incentives; verify Xcel commercial block availability before assuming a production payment.

Oregon

Oregon's incentives run primarily through Energy Trust of Oregon (ETO) for customers of Portland General Electric and Pacific Power, complemented by an Oregon Department of Energy (ODOE) rebate program.

  • Energy Trust of Oregon incentives — ETO offers cash incentives for solar and solar-plus-storage. Recent structures included a flat per-system solar incentive and a storage adder around $400/kWh capped near $5,000 (approximate — verify current-year offer). ETO also runs trade-ally and custom paths for larger commercial projects. See Energy Trust — Solar and Energy Trust Insider — Solar Incentives.
  • Oregon Solar + Storage Rebate Program (ODOE) — A separately funded rebate that has cycled through fully-reserved / temporarily-reopened periods; commercial eligibility is limited (residential and low-income service providers are the core categories). Confirm whether a funding round is open at ODOE — Solar + Storage Rebate.
  • Property tax exemption — Oregon exempts the added value of qualifying solar systems from property tax.

Commercial takeaway: For commercial and industrial hosts, ETO custom/production-track incentives plus federal ITC and depreciation typically drive economics; the ODOE rebate is intermittent and residentially focused.
