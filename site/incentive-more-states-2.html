More State Programs (Round 2)

Commercial-solar incentive profiles for more states — Texas, North Carolina, Georgia, Ohio, Wisconsin, Washington, Nevada and Arizona — with mechanisms, eligibility and links.

State policy is where commercial-solar economics are won or lost after the federal stack. This second round of profiles covers eight more states with meaningfully different approaches — from Texas's deregulated, no-statewide-net-metering market to the net-billing regimes of Nevada and Arizona, and the tax-exemption-driven incentives of North Carolina, Georgia, Ohio, Wisconsin, and Washington.

Each profile gives the mechanism, commercial eligibility, current status, and an official link. Programs change frequently, so treat every figure as approximate and cross-check the definitive, continuously-updated database, DSIRE. For prior states, see More State Programs; for federal stacking, see Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits and State Tax Credits & Incentives.

These are state-level frameworks. The most valuable commercial incentives are often utility-specific (rates, standby charges, rebates) and local (property-tax option). Always confirm the terms of the specific utility serving the site.

Texas

  • Mechanism. No statewide net-metering mandate. Texas is largely a deregulated (ERCOT) market; compensation for exported solar depends on the retail provider or utility (some offer buyback/"solar buyback" plans). A 100% property-tax exemption covers the added appraised value from a solar device used for on-site energy. Texas has no state sales tax exemption specific to solar equipment (standard sales tax applies).
  • Commercial eligibility. Broadly available; property-tax exemption applies to on-site generation devices. Large loads may participate directly in ERCOT markets.
  • Current status. Active; competitive-market buyback offers vary by REP and change often. See also the Texas REC program (tracked in ERCOT).
  • Official link. Texas Comptroller — Solar and Wind-Powered Energy Device Exemption

North Carolina

  • Mechanism. 80% property-tax exclusion on the appraised value of a qualifying solar energy electric system (a partial abatement, not full). Utility programs (e.g., Duke Energy) provide net-metering/rider-based compensation and periodic performance or storage offers. No general solar sales-tax exemption (standard sales tax applies).
  • Commercial eligibility. The 80% exclusion is available to business-owned systems via application (Form AV-10); utility program terms vary by rate schedule and interconnection size.
  • Current status. Active; RPS/REPS obligations tracked via NC-RETS. Utility net-metering terms have been revised — confirm current rider.
  • Official link. NCDOR — AV-10 Application for Property Tax Exemption or Exclusion

Georgia

  • Mechanism. No statewide RPS and limited statewide incentives; compensation is driven by utility programs (Georgia Power tariffs, periodic solar/BYOS-type offers) and interconnection rules. Property-tax treatment of solar is limited and utility/local-dependent.
  • Commercial eligibility. Commercial customers rely primarily on utility tariff economics plus the federal stack; program capacity is often capped and can fill quickly.
  • Current status. Active but narrow; watch Georgia Power's Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) and program cycles for current commercial offers.
  • Official link. Georgia Power — Solar and Renewable Programs

Ohio

  • Mechanism. Tradable SREC market tied to Ohio's RPS solar carve-out (historically very low-value, often under $5/SREC). Renewable-energy property-tax exemption (via the Qualified Energy Project exemption for larger facilities, with local payment-in-lieu terms). ECO-PACE / PACE financing enables commercial property owners to finance solar through a long-term special assessment repaid on the property-tax bill.
  • Commercial eligibility. SRECs available to certified systems (tracked in PJM-GATS); the Qualified Energy Project exemption applies to qualifying larger projects with local approval; PACE is available to commercial, industrial, nonprofit, and multifamily owners.
  • Current status. SREC market active but low value; PACE active through regional/special energy improvement districts.
  • Official link. Ohio Dept. of Development — Qualified Energy Project Tax Exemption

Wisconsin

  • Mechanism. Focus on Energy — the statewide efficiency/renewables program — offers rebates/incentives for qualifying business renewable and efficiency projects. A property-tax exemption covers the added value of solar-energy systems (business and residential). Standard sales tax generally applies, though some renewable equipment carries exemptions.
  • Commercial eligibility. Focus on Energy has business/commercial tracks; property-tax exemption applies to the value added by the solar system. Incentive budgets and terms are set annually.
  • Current status. Active; Focus on Energy offerings and budgets change by program year.
  • Official link. Focus on Energy — Business Programs

Washington

  • Mechanism. Net metering at (generally) retail rate for excess generation, subject to utility caps. A sales/use-tax incentive applies to qualifying renewable-energy machinery and equipment (a reduced-rate remittance/exemption for solar systems — terms and system-size thresholds have changed over time). No state income tax.
  • Commercial eligibility. Net metering available to commercial customers subject to system-size and utility-cap limits; sales-tax incentive applies to qualifying equipment purchases — confirm current thresholds and expiration.
  • Current status. Active; the sales-tax incentive terms and net-metering caps are periodically revised — verify current statute.
  • Official link. WA Dept. of Revenue — Renewable Energy / Solar Incentives

Nevada

  • Mechanism. Net billing (successor to full net metering) compensates exports at a set percentage of retail via tiered NEM 2.0-style schedules. A Renewable Energy Property-Tax Abatement offers up to 55% abatement for up to 20 years for qualifying larger facilities (generation facilities generally ≥10 MW), plus a sales/use-tax abatement for qualifying renewable-energy projects.
  • Commercial eligibility. Net billing available to commercial customers (NV Energy); the property/sales-tax abatements target utility-scale/large projects meeting capacity and wage/benefit criteria administered by the Governor's Office of Energy.
  • Current status. Active; abatement application windows and criteria are set by the state — confirm current tier percentages and thresholds.
  • Official link. Nevada Governor's Office of Energy — Renewable Energy Tax Abatements

Arizona

  • Mechanism. Net billing (export credit below full retail; set by utility resource-comparison-proxy/avoided-cost methods). 100% property-tax exemption for the added value of a solar energy device, plus a sales/use-tax exemption on qualifying solar energy equipment.
  • Commercial eligibility. Net billing available to commercial customers (APS, TEP, SRP terms vary); property- and sales-tax exemptions apply to qualifying solar equipment/devices.
  • Current status. Active; export-credit rates are set annually by each utility and decline over time — confirm the current rate rider.
  • Official link. Arizona Dept. of Revenue — Solar Energy Devices
