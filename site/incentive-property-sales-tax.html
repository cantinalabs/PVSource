Property & Sales-Tax Exemptions

The overlooked non-cash incentives — property-tax exemptions and abatements plus sales/use-tax exemptions on equipment, how to claim them, and how much they move project economics.

Property taxExemptions
Sales/use taxOn equipment
LCOEReal impact

The federal ITC and depreciation get the attention, but two state and local incentives quietly move project economics as much as some cash rebates: property-tax exemptions (so a solar system doesn't raise your assessment) and sales/use-tax exemptions (so you don't pay tax on the equipment). Neither writes you a check — they simply remove a cost — which is exactly why they're easy to overlook and easy to leave on the table.

This page explains how each works, how to claim them, how much they affect project economics and LCOE, the wide state-by-state variation, and how they stack with the federal ITC. Because these are highly local, treat specifics as representative and confirm the definitive detail with DSIRE. See also More State Programs (Round 2), State Tax Credits & Incentives, and Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Non-cash, but real. A property-tax exemption can be worth thousands of dollars per year for decades; a sales-tax exemption can cut several percent off installed cost immediately. Both flow straight to the bottom line and improve LCOE.

Property-Tax Exemptions

The Problem They Solve

Adding a solar array increases the value of a property. Absent relief, the local assessor could raise the assessed value by the cost (or added market value) of the system, and you would pay higher property tax every year for the system's life — an annual penalty for improving your property. Property-tax incentives neutralize that.

The Three Common Forms

Form How it works
Exemption / exclusion The solar system's added value is excluded from assessment — either 100% (e.g., AZ, TX on-site devices) or partial (e.g., NC 80% exclusion).
Abatement Property tax on the system is reduced or waived for a set number of years (e.g., NV up to 55% for up to 20 years for qualifying facilities).
Special / alternative assessment The system is assessed under a special methodology (e.g., a fixed $/kW or income-based value) instead of full market value, often for utility-scale.

Key Variables to Check

  • Duration. Some exemptions are permanent; abatements have a term (e.g., 10 or 20 years). After an abatement expires, tax may resume.
  • Full vs. partial. 100% exemptions (AZ, TX device value) vs. partial exclusions (NC 80%).
  • Commercial eligibility. Some programs are residential-only; others cover commercial/industrial or only utility-scale. Confirm your class qualifies.
  • Local-option nuances. In some states the exemption is local-option — counties or municipalities choose whether to adopt it, so treatment can differ within a state. Payments-in-lieu-of-taxes (PILOT) agreements are common for large projects.
  • Application required. Many exemptions are not automatic — you must file (e.g., NC Form AV-10) by a deadline.

Sales/Use-Tax Exemptions

What They Cover

Many states exempt qualifying solar equipment from sales and use tax on purchase. On a large commercial buy, waiving a 4–9% sales tax on equipment is a meaningful upfront saving.

  • What qualifies varies: often modules, inverters, racking, and balance-of-system; sometimes only the generating equipment, not wiring, structural steel, or labor.
  • Some states offer a full exemption, others a reduced rate, a deferral, or a remittance/refund mechanism.

How to Claim It

  1. Confirm the equipment qualifies under the state's definition (read the statute or DOR guidance — line items matter).
  2. Obtain/complete an exemption certificate (the state's resale/exemption form).
  3. Present the certificate to the vendor at purchase so tax is not charged — or, where the program is a refund/remittance, pay the tax and apply for it back.
  4. Retain documentation (certificate, invoices, qualifying-equipment schedule) for audit.

Watch the equipment-vs.-labor and equipment-vs.-real-property lines. Exemptions frequently cover the hardware but not installation labor or structural/civil work — model only what actually qualifies.

Representative State Examples

Illustrative only — confirm current terms with DSIRE and the state DOR.

Notice the cross-cutting variation: a state can exempt property tax but not sales tax (Texas, North Carolina), or vice versa. Always check both taxes separately.

Effect on Project Economics & LCOE

Both incentives improve returns by cutting costs, which lowers the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE):

  • Sales-tax exemption reduces installed capital cost directly — e.g., waiving a 6% sales tax on the equipment portion of a $1,000,000 system can save tens of thousands of dollars at day one, lowering the denominator-driving cost basis.
  • Property-tax exemption removes a recurring operating expense for the system's life — over 20–30 years, avoided property tax can total a large share of a system's cost, and because it's an annual saving it directly improves cash flow and LCOE.

A useful mental model: the sales-tax break lowers the sticker price; the property-tax break lowers the running cost. Both shrink LCOE, and neither shows up as a "rebate" line — so they're easy to forget in a pro forma.

Stacking with the Federal ITC

These state/local exemptions stack cleanly with the federal stack — they operate on different taxes:

  • The federal ITC and MACRS depreciation apply to federal income tax.
  • The sales-tax exemption applies to state/local sales/use tax on the purchase.
  • The property-tax exemption applies to local property tax on the assessed value.

Because they touch separate tax bases, you can generally claim all three simultaneously. One interaction to note: a sales-tax exemption lowers installed cost, which can slightly reduce the ITC-eligible basis (a smaller invoice), but the net effect is still strongly positive — you avoid the tax outright rather than recovering only 30% of it. Confirm basis treatment with your tax advisor. See MACRS Depreciation and Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.
