Connecticut Solar Incentives & Policy

Solar in Connecticut — the net-billing RRES and NRES tariffs, and sales- and property-tax exemptions.

Net billingRRES / NRES
ExemptionsSales & property
UtilitiesEversource · UI

Connecticut has fully transitioned away from traditional retail net metering to a net billing framework built around two tariff programs run by the Public Utilities Regulatory Authority (PURA): the Residential Renewable Energy Solutions (RRES) program for homes and the Non-Residential Renewable Energy Solutions (NRES) tariff for commercial and larger systems. The state pairs these with permanent sales tax and property tax exemptions and a statewide battery storage program, but offers no state income tax credit for solar.

At-a-Glance Summary

SREC market None (RECs conveyed to utility under RRES) None standalone; RECs via NRES

Administering agency PURA / Eversource / United Illuminating PURA / Eversource / United Illuminating

Connecticut's model is a tariff-and-exemption strategy rather than a rebate-and-tax-credit one. Understanding the RRES tariff choice is the single most important decision for a homeowner going solar in the state.

See also: Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures · Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits · SREC Markets Deep-Dive · More State Programs

Overview

Connecticut electricity rates are among the highest in the continental U.S., which makes on-site solar self-consumption economically attractive even without a retail net metering credit. The state retired its legacy net metering and the older Residential Solar Investment Program (RSIP), replacing them under Public Act 19-35 with successor tariff programs administered by PURA and delivered through the two investor-owned utilities, Eversource and United Illuminating (UI).

Solar qualifies as a Class I renewable resource under Connecticut's Renewable Portfolio Standard.

Net Metering / Net Billing

Connecticut no longer offers retail-rate net metering to new customers. Instead:

Residential Renewable Energy Solutions (RRES)

Launched January 2022 (statutory basis CGS § 16-244z), RRES applies to residential systems generally up to 25 kW. Homeowners choose one of two structures, locked for a 20-year term:

  • Netting Tariff — the system offsets on-site load; exported excess is credited. Renewable energy certificates (RECs) are conveyed to the utility.
  • Buy-All Tariff — all generation is sold to the utility at a fixed rate through separate metering, and the home draws all its power from the grid at retail rates.

Which to choose? Netting favors households whose usage aligns well with solar production; Buy-All can favor larger systems or homes with lower daytime load. Run both scenarios with your installer before committing to the 20-year lock.

The program operates in annual "Program Years." Program Year 5 (2026) rates and fees were set by PURA in Docket No. 25-08-02 (decision dated December 2025).

Verify current rates: Specific 2026 Buy-All and Netting per-kWh rates, the on-bill "Solar Energy Adjustment" reportedly applied to new Netting projects, and non-bypassable charges change each Program Year. Confirm the exact figures in the PURA Docket 25-08-02 decision and the current RRES Program Manual before relying on any published number — verify at DSIRE/official site.

Non-Residential Renewable Energy Solutions (NRES)

The commercial counterpart, NRES (launched 2022), replaced the older LREC/ZREC and Virtual Net Metering programs. It offers Buy-All and Netting options through a competitive RFP structure with periodic solicitation windows (typically February and August). Program Year 5 (2026) raised project price caps and set an annual budget ceiling; the bid fee was updated. Commercial developers should track the current NRES RFP schedule and price caps directly through PURA and the utilities.

For a general primer on how netting and net-billing mechanics affect payback, see Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures.

SREC / RECs

Connecticut does not operate a standalone residential SREC market like those in New Jersey, Massachusetts, or Washington, D.C. Solar qualifies as a Class I RPS resource, but for residential systems the RRES program conveys and monetizes the RECs to the utility as part of the 20-year tariff — this effectively replaced separate SREC monetization for homeowners. Commercial RECs are handled through the NRES tariff. For background on how solar certificate markets function elsewhere, see the SREC Markets Deep-Dive.

State Tax Credits

Connecticut offers no state income tax credit for residential or commercial solar. The state's incentive strategy relies on the RRES/NRES tariffs, tax exemptions, and the storage program described below rather than an income tax credit.

Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are governed separately from state policy:

  • Residential (§25D): The 30% federal residential clean energy credit ended after December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally do not qualify for the residential credit.
  • Commercial (ITC / §48E): Business-owned solar may still qualify for the commercial investment tax credit and the technology-neutral §48E clean electricity investment credit, subject to current phase-down schedules, wage/apprenticeship rules, and domestic-content provisions.

For details and current status, see Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Utility Incentives / Rebates

Connecticut does not offer a standalone utility cash rebate for solar panels beyond the RRES/NRES tariff compensation. The primary supplemental incentive is for battery storage:

Energy Storage Solutions (ESS) — a statewide program overseen by PURA and administered by the Connecticut Green Bank with Eversource and UI, covering both residential and commercial/industrial systems.

Program redesign effective April 1, 2026. The prior large upfront incentive model was replaced by a smaller enrollment incentive plus higher long-term performance payments tied to active battery dispatch. Reported residential terms include an enrollment incentive around $30/kWh (higher on grid-constrained circuits) plus multi-year performance payments. Older sources citing large upfront per-kWh rebates are stale for new enrollees — verify current ESS terms at the official program site.

Financing is also available through Smart-E Loans via the Connecticut Green Bank (confirm current rates directly).

Sales Tax Exemption

Connecticut provides a 100% exemption from its 6.35% sales and use tax for solar PV, solar thermal, and geothermal systems, including modules, racking, inverters, storage batteries, and installation services. The exemption is codified at CGS § 12-412 and claimed via Form CERT-140. It is a permanent exemption.

Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Renewable energy systems are exempt from local property tax under CGS § 12-81 (subdivisions 56 and 57) — the added assessed value of the system is excluded:

  • Residential and farm Class I systems installed on or after October 1, 2007 are exempt.
  • Commercial and industrial Class I systems (installed on or after January 1, 2010) are exempt, subject to capacity conditions.

Owners generally file Form M-44 with the town assessor, typically due by November 1 of the assessment year.

Permitting & Interconnection Notes

  • Interconnection runs through the utilities' online portals (Eversource PowerClerk; UI's application-to-connect process). A Permission to Operate must be issued before energizing.
  • 2026 legislation (HB 5036 / HB 5340) directs the state to adopt an automated/instant permitting platform (SolarAPP+ or equivalent) for rooftop systems up to 25 kW including paired storage, extended incentives, and authorized plug-in ("balcony") solar. State deployment and municipal adoption phase in over the following years — treat these as enacted-but-phasing-in and verify effective dates.
  • Local building and electrical permits remain required in addition to interconnection approval.

Bottom Line

Connecticut's high retail rates make solar attractive, but the value proposition hinges on choosing the right RRES tariff (Netting vs. Buy-All) and locking it for 20 years — commercial owners navigate the NRES RFP process instead. There is no state income tax credit, but permanent sales and property tax exemptions and the Energy Storage Solutions battery program add meaningful value. Because the residential federal §25D credit ended after 2025, 2026 economics rest more heavily on state tariffs, exemptions, storage incentives, and (for businesses) the commercial ITC/§48E. Always confirm current-year RRES/NRES rates and ESS terms against PURA and utility sources before signing.

Educational use only. This page is a general reference, not tax, legal, or financial advice. Incentive values, tariff rates, and program rules change frequently. Verify all figures with PURA, the Connecticut Department of Revenue Services, your utility, and a qualified professional before making decisions.
