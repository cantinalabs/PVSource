Community Solar for Households

Solar without a roof — how subscriptions and bill credits work for renters and homeowners, savings vs commitment, who qualifies, contract terms, and consumer cautions.

SubscribeBill credits
RentersNo roof needed
ContractsRead the terms

Community solar lets you benefit from solar power without putting panels on your own roof. Instead of owning hardware, you subscribe to a share of a larger off-site solar farm, and the electricity that array sends to the grid earns credits on your utility bill. It is built for the roughly half of U.S. households that can't or don't want to install rooftop solar: renters, condo and apartment dwellers, people with shaded or north-facing roofs, and homeowners who simply don't want a 20-year roof commitment.

This page is written for the subscriber — the household joining a project. If you are a landowner, developer, business, or municipality looking at building or hosting a project, see the companion page Community Solar (Commercial & Developer).

How a community solar subscription works

A third-party developer builds a solar array somewhere in your utility territory — on a warehouse roof, a capped landfill, or open land. The project is divided into shares, and households subscribe to a portion sized to their annual electricity use (commonly expressed in kilowatts of capacity or in expected annual kWh).

The array doesn't power your home directly. Its electricity flows onto the shared grid. Each month the utility measures your share's production and applies a bill credit to your account, a mechanism called virtual net metering or on-bill crediting. You keep buying electricity from your utility as before; the credits offset part of that cost.

Solar farm produces ──> credits assigned to your share ──> applied to YOUR utility bill
You still receive your normal utility bill, now reduced by the credit.

The two-bill model (most common)

In many programs you receive two bills:

  1. Your utility bill, now showing a solar bill credit (often at or near the retail rate).
  2. A subscription bill from the community solar provider, charging you for the credits you received — but at a discount (commonly 5–15% less than the credit's value).

The gap between the two is your savings. If you earn $100 in utility bill credits and pay the provider $90, you net $10 that month.

Watch the math. Your savings come from the spread between the credit value and the subscription price — not from a flat "X% off your whole electric bill." Some marketing blurs this. Ask the provider to show a sample month with both bills.

How credits are valued

What a credit is worth depends on how your state prices it:

Crediting method What the credit is worth Where it's common
Retail rate Roughly your full bundled per-kWh rate (~12–25¢/kWh in 2026) Most generous; many state programs
Value stack / VDER A stack of separate components (energy, capacity, environmental value), fixed per project New York; coming to California
Avoided cost / wholesale Lower, closer to what the utility pays generators Least generous to subscribers

Savings vs. commitment

Most subscribers save 5–15% on the supply portion of their electricity costs versus the default utility rate. That is real but modest — community solar is a discount on power, not a wealth-building investment the way owning a paid-off rooftop system can be.

In exchange for $0 upfront and no equipment, you give up the larger lifetime savings, the home-value increase, and the federal tax credit that come with owning rooftop solar.

Community solar vs. owning rooftop

Community solar subscription Owning rooftop solar
Upfront cost Usually $0 $15k–$30k (or financed)
Who can use it Renters, condos, shaded roofs Owners with suitable roofs
Typical savings 5–15% on supply Often 50–90%+ of the bill long-term
Federal tax credit No (you don't own it) Yes, if owned (see Residential Incentives)
Equipment / roof risk None You own and maintain it
Portability Move within utility territory; otherwise cancel Stays with the home
Commitment Contract term varies; see below 20–25 year asset

If you can install and afford rooftop solar, owning it almost always saves more over time. Community solar wins when rooftop isn't an option or you want flexibility.

Who qualifies

Eligibility is set by each state program and provider, but the common requirements are:

  • You are a utility customer in the same service territory (sometimes the same load zone) as the project.
  • Your account is in good standing — many providers run a soft credit check or require no recent disconnections.
  • Your annual usage fits the available share size (programs typically cap your subscription so credits don't exceed your consumption).

You generally do not need to own your home, have a good roof, or make any structural changes — which is the entire point.

Low- and moderate-income (LMI) programs

Many states carve out a percentage of every community solar project for low- and moderate-income households and offer deeper, guaranteed discounts. Examples include Illinois Solar for All (ILSFA), Maryland and New Jersey LMI carve-outs, and Colorado's low-income provisions. These programs often feature:

  • Larger guaranteed savings (sometimes 20%+ off).
  • No credit check and no cancellation fees.
  • Income verification via existing benefits (e.g., LIHEAP, SNAP) instead of pay stubs.

If you qualify on income, ask specifically for the LMI or "Solar for All" tier — it is usually the best deal available.

Contract terms & cancellation

Read the subscription agreement carefully. Key terms to check:

  • Length: Older contracts ran 20–25 years; many newer ones are month-to-month or 1-year with easy exit. Prefer short or no-commitment terms.
  • Cancellation: Look for a 30–90 day cancellation notice with no penalty. Avoid contracts with large early-termination fees.
  • Price escalator: Some contracts raise the subscription rate ~2–3% per year. Make sure the discount is guaranteed, not just the rate.
  • Moving: Confirm what happens if you move. Within the same utility territory you can usually transfer; outside it, you should be able to cancel without penalty.
  • Guaranteed savings vs. estimated: A guaranteed-percentage-off contract protects you; an "estimated savings" contract does not.

Consumer-protection cautions

Community solar is legitimate and regulated in most states, but the sales channel can be aggressive. Protect yourself:

  • Be skeptical of door-to-door and robocall pitches claiming a "government solar program" or "your utility's solar discount." Utilities rarely cold-call you, and community solar is not a government handout.
  • Never give your full utility account number, Social Security number, or banking details to an unsolicited caller. Your account number alone can be used to enroll (or slam) you without consent.
  • Confirm enrollment with your utility directly after signing up.
  • Get the savings claim in writing and confirm it's a guaranteed discount, not a projection.
  • Verify the provider against your state public utility commission's list of approved community solar subscriber organizations.
  • Watch for consolidated/single-bill programs where the math is hard to audit — ask to see the underlying credit.

If a pitch pressures you to "sign today," walk away. Reputable programs let you take your time.

Which states have it

As of 2026, roughly 24 states plus Washington, D.C. have enabling legislation that supports subscriber community solar, and most other states have at least limited or utility-pilot offerings. The largest and strongest-economics programs in 2026 are in:

  • Minnesota (Xcel community solar gardens — one of the largest fleets)
  • New York (NY-Sun + Value of Distributed Energy Resources / VDER)
  • Illinois (Adjustable Block Program + Illinois Solar for All)
  • Massachusetts (SMART)
  • New Jersey, Maryland, and Colorado

States without an enabling statute — notably Texas — have no true bill-credit community solar; offerings marketed there are usually retail electricity plans, not virtual net metering. Check your state public utility commission or the U.S. Department of Energy's Community Solar Basics before subscribing.
