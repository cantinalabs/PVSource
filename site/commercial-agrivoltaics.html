Agrivoltaics & Dual-Use Solar

Solar and farming on the same land — configurations from elevated arrays to grazing and pollinator habitat, design trade-offs, research (NREL InSPIRE), and economics.

Dual-useSolar + ag
Grazing · cropsConfigurations
InSPIREResearch

Agrivoltaics (also called dual-use or co-location solar) is the practice of using the same parcel of land for both solar electricity generation and agriculture — growing crops, grazing livestock, or cultivating pollinator habitat in, under, and between the rows of a solar array. Instead of forcing a choice between energy and farming, agrivoltaics aims to produce both from one footprint.

The model has moved quickly from research to deployment. NREL's InSPIRE project (Innovative Solar Practices Integrated with Rural Economies and Ecosystems) documented close to 600 agrivoltaic sites across roughly 60,000 acres in the U.S. as of late 2024, and the first U.S. Solar Grazing Census (NREL with the American Solar Grazing Association, 2024) counted approximately 113,050 sheep grazing about 129,000 acres across 500+ solar sites in 30 states.

This is a commercial-side page for landowners, farmers, and developers. For off-site subscriber programs, see Community Solar (Commercial & Developer); for the federal credits that apply, see Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits.

Configurations

There is no single agrivoltaic design — the right one depends on the agricultural goal, equipment, and climate.

Configuration Description Best for
Standard-height grazing/pollinator Conventional fixed-tilt or tracker array; vegetation managed under and between rows Sheep grazing, pollinator/native habitat — the most common, lowest-cost form
Elevated arrays Panels raised (often ~8–15+ ft) so equipment, animals, or crops fit underneath Crops needing machinery access; cattle; shade-tolerant produce
Spaced / wide rows Increased row spacing and pitch for more ground-level light Row crops and hay between rows
Vertical bifacial Panels mounted vertically (often east-west facing), capturing light on both faces Maximizing arable land between rows; morning/evening generation; minimal shading of crops

The three agricultural pathways

  1. Solar grazing (livestock). Sheep are the dominant choice — they graze vegetation without damaging panels or wiring (unlike goats, which climb and chew, or cattle, which need elevated structures and sturdier mounts). Grazing replaces mowing as vegetation management, often at lower cost, and gives shepherds low-cost forage. This is by far the most scaled form of agrivoltaics in the U.S.
  2. Pollinator habitat. Seeding native flowering plants and grasses under and around arrays supports bees and other pollinators, improves soil and stormwater performance, and can benefit adjacent crops. Several states recognize "pollinator-friendly solar" with scorecards or standards.
  3. Crop production. Growing produce, specialty crops, or forage between/under panels. The most design-intensive pathway — requires elevated or widely spaced arrays and careful matching of shade-tolerant crops to the local light environment.

Benefits & trade-offs

Benefits

  • Land productivity. One parcel yields both energy and an agricultural product, raising total output per acre and helping address "energy vs. farmland" land-use conflicts.
  • Panel cooling. Vegetation and transpiration beneath modules can lower panel temperature; cooler panels are more efficient, modestly boosting energy yield in hot climates.
  • Water benefits. Partial shading reduces soil evaporation and crop water stress; in arid regions some crops use less irrigation under panels.
  • Crop & habitat protection. Panels provide shade and shelter from heat, hail, and wind for certain crops and forage.
  • Vegetation management savings. Grazing can replace mechanical mowing.
  • Farm income diversification & rural economic benefit. Lease income or self-generation plus continued agricultural revenue; grazing contracts for local shepherds.

Trade-offs

  • Higher capital cost for elevated or widely spaced designs (more steel, taller racking, lower density per acre).
  • Reduced panel density / land-use efficiency for energy when rows are spaced for crops or equipment — fewer kW per acre.
  • Operational complexity — coordinating O&M crews with farming/grazing operations, fencing, water, animal welfare, and liability.
  • Crop selection is constrained to shade-tolerant species in many layouts; not every crop performs well.
  • Site-specific results — benefits vary widely by climate, soil, crop, and configuration; what works in arid Colorado may not in the humid Southeast.

Design considerations

Key parameters a developer balances:

  • Panel height / clearance. Higher clearance lets equipment, cattle, or taller crops work underneath but raises racking cost and wind loading. Grazing sheep need far less clearance than tractors.
  • Ground coverage ratio (GCR). GCR is the ratio of module area to land area. Lower GCR (wider spacing) admits more light to the ground for crops but reduces energy per acre; higher GCR maximizes generation but shades the ground.
GCR = total module (collector) area  /  total ground (land) area

  Energy-maximizing array:   higher GCR  (denser rows, more shade below)
  Crop-friendly array:       lower  GCR  (wider rows, more ground light)
  -> Vertical bifacial rows can keep arable strips between panels at modest GCR.
  • Row spacing & orientation. Wider pitch and, for vertical bifacial, east-west orientation preserve usable ground.
  • Equipment clearance & turning radius. Lay out rows so existing farm machinery can pass and turn.
  • Soil, drainage & revegetation. Minimize compaction during construction; establish vegetation/seed mix suited to the agricultural goal.
  • Water & fencing for livestock; mounting protection (cable management out of reach of animals).

Choosing among livestock, crops & pollinators

Pathway Capital/complexity Maturity in U.S. Notes
Sheep grazing Low — standard arrays work High (scaled, 30+ states) Replaces mowing; preferred livestock; cattle/goats problematic
Pollinator habitat Low–moderate Growing; state standards exist Ecosystem + soil/stormwater benefits; complements crops
Crop production High — elevated/spaced arrays Emerging / mostly pilots Highest design effort; match shade-tolerant crops carefully

For most commercial-scale projects today, sheep grazing and pollinator habitat are the lowest-risk, most-proven options; dedicated crop agrivoltaics is advancing but remains more specialized and capital-intensive.

Research, incentives & state programs

  • NREL InSPIRE is the leading U.S. research program, running multi-site field trials on grazing, crops, and pollinator habitat plus modeling tools and the public InSPIRE Agrivoltaics Map. The U.S. Department of Energy has published best-practice reports on combining solar and agriculture.
  • American Solar Grazing Association (ASGA) supports solar-grazing practice and co-ran the 2024 U.S. Solar Grazing Census.
  • State programs. A growing number of states offer dual-use incentives or adders. Massachusetts pioneered a dual-use adder under its SMART program; New Jersey established a Dual-Use Solar pilot; states such as Colorado, Minnesota, and others fund or study agrivoltaics. Pollinator-friendly solar scorecards/standards exist in several states. Program details change frequently — verify current offerings with your state energy office.

Economics

Agrivoltaics economics combine the standard solar revenue stack with agricultural value and avoided costs:

  • Energy revenue — PPA, net metering, community solar subscription revenue, or self-consumption.
  • Federal incentives — the same investment credit and bonus adders that apply to other commercial solar; see Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits. Tax-exempt landowners (e.g., universities, municipalities) may monetize credits via Direct Pay — see Nonprofit, Municipal & School Solar.
  • Agricultural revenue — crop sales, forage, or grazing-contract value.
  • Avoided O&M cost — grazing displacing mechanical mowing.
  • State dual-use adders/grants where available.

Against these, weigh the incremental capital of elevated/spaced designs and the lower energy density of crop-friendly layouts. The strongest economics today are generally standard-height arrays with sheep grazing and/or pollinator habitat, where the incremental cost over a conventional ground-mount is small and the vegetation-management savings are real. Crop-focused designs require a site-specific business case.

Agrivoltaic outcomes are highly site-specific. Use measured local data and pilot results — not generalized claims — to model a given project.
