# Direct Pay (Elective Payment)

**Direct pay** — formally the **elective payment** election under **Internal Revenue Code (IRC) §6417** — lets certain organizations that owe little or no federal income tax collect the value of clean-energy tax credits as a **cash payment from the IRS** instead of as a reduction in tax liability. It was created by the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in 2022 and survives, with new constraints, after the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)** (H.R.1, signed July 4, 2025). For a tax-exempt hospital, a city, a school district, or a tribe, direct pay is the difference between being locked out of the [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md) and being able to use them in full.

This page explains who qualifies, which credits are eligible, the mechanics of getting paid, the **domestic-content phase-in** that can reduce direct-pay for larger projects, how the OBBBA solar deadlines and **foreign-entity (FEOC)** rules interact, and how direct pay compares to [Transferability of Credits (§6418)](incentive-transferability.md).

> Direct pay does **not** create new credits. It changes how you *monetize* a credit you already qualify for — turning a nonrefundable credit into, effectively, a refundable one for eligible entities.

## Who Can Use Direct Pay

The core universe of **"applicable entities"** eligible for direct pay under §6417 is broad but specific:

| Eligible entity | Examples |
|---|---|
| Tax-exempt organizations | 501(c)(3) nonprofits, hospitals, universities, houses of worship |
| State & local governments | Cities, counties, states, school districts, public utilities, agencies |
| Indian tribal governments | And subdivisions thereof |
| Alaska Native Corporations | |
| The Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA) | |
| Rural electric cooperatives | Co-ops serving rural members |
| U.S. territorial governments | Puerto Rico, Guam, etc. |

**For-profit / taxable businesses generally cannot use direct pay**, with three narrow exceptions where §6417 allows any taxpayer to elect it, all outside the core solar ITC/PTC: the **§45Q** carbon oxide sequestration credit, the **§45V** clean hydrogen credit, and the **§45X** advanced manufacturing production credit — each subject to its own limited election period. A commercial solar developer that is a normal taxable business therefore looks to [Transferability](incentive-transferability.md) or tax equity to monetize its ITC, not to direct pay.

## Which Credits Are Eligible

Applicable entities can elect direct pay for the major clean-energy credits, including:

- **§48E** Clean Electricity Investment Tax Credit (and legacy **§48** ITC) — the credit most relevant to solar and paired storage
- **§45Y** Clean Electricity Production Tax Credit (and legacy **§45** PTC)
- **§45X** Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit
- **§45Q** Carbon Oxide Sequestration
- **§45V** Clean Hydrogen; **§30C** alternative-fuel refueling property; and several others

For a solar project owned by a nonprofit or municipality, the relevant elections are almost always the **§48E investment credit** (a percentage of eligible project cost) or the **§45Y production credit** (per-kWh over time).

## How You Get Paid: The Mechanics

Direct pay is claimed on a **tax return**, but for entities that don't otherwise file, it means filing one specifically to make the election. There are four gates, in order:

1. **Place the property in service.** The clean-energy asset must be operational; you cannot register or claim before commissioning.
2. **Complete pre-filing registration.** Using the IRS **Energy Credits Online (ECO)** portal, an authorized representative creates an account and obtains a **registration number for each credit property**. This launched in December 2023.
3. **File the return and make the election.** Include every registration number on the return (e.g., Form 990-T or the appropriate return, with Form 3800). The registration number is mandatory — an election without it is ineffective.
4. **Receive the payment.** The IRS processes the return and pays the credit as a refund/payment, in one **lump sum** (not installments).

> **Timing matters.** The IRS advises completing pre-filing registration **at least 120 days before** you plan to file, because the IRS may take up to 120 days to review a submission and issue registration numbers. Registration is *required but not a guarantee* — the credit is still verified when the return is processed.

Applicable entities generally use a **tax year** that lets them align the election with the year the property was placed in service; many have flexibility to set or change their annual accounting period to optimize timing of the payment.

## Domestic-Content Phase-In (the Direct-Pay "Haircut")

This is the trap most likely to reduce a large project's direct-pay value. A **domestic-content phase-out** applies specifically to elective-pay claims on **larger projects (generally 1 MW AC and above)** that do **not** meet the domestic-content requirement. If the project fails to satisfy domestic content and no exception applies, the amount eligible for direct pay is reduced:

| Construction begins | Direct-pay amount if domestic content NOT met (1 MW+) |
|---|---|
| On/after Jan 1, 2024 | 90% of the credit |
| On/after Jan 1, 2025 | 85% of the credit |
| On/after Jan 1, 2026 | **0%** (no direct pay) |

In other words, for a large project beginning construction in 2026 or later, **failing domestic content eliminates the direct-pay option entirely** for that credit. This is separate from — and stacks conceptually alongside — the domestic-content *bonus* credit.

To satisfy the requirement, a project must meet the domestic-content thresholds for **steel/iron (100% U.S.)** and **manufactured products**, where the manufactured-products threshold itself phases up (roughly 40% for projects starting in 2024, 45% in 2025, 50% in 2026, 55% thereafter). Two statutory exceptions can excuse a project: the **increased-cost exception** (using U.S. materials would raise total project cost by more than 25%) and the **non-availability exception** (the U.S.-made items aren't available in sufficient quantity or quality). Meeting an exception preserves full direct pay even without domestic content.

> **Planning takeaway.** For any 1 MW+ project targeting direct pay, treat domestic content as a **gating design decision made before procurement**, not a bonus to chase later. Under 1 MW, this phase-out generally does not apply.

## Interaction with OBBBA Deadlines & FEOC

Direct pay only monetizes a credit that actually *exists*, so the OBBBA's tightened solar timeline governs eligibility upstream:

- **Solar/wind deadline.** Under OBBBA, §48E/§45Y credits for solar and wind terminate for projects **placed in service after December 31, 2027**, **unless construction begins by July 4, 2026** (projects that begin construction on or before that date follow the older placed-in-service rules). The "beginning of construction" standard has been subject to 2025–2026 IRS guidance (Notice 2025-42) and litigation, so verify the current rule for your project.
- **Prohibited Foreign Entity / FEOC rules.** OBBBA layers in **Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC)** restrictions. An entity that is itself a **prohibited foreign entity** — a "specified foreign entity" or "foreign-influenced entity" — can be denied the underlying credit, and projects with material FEOC-linked content or ownership can lose eligibility. Because direct pay depends on the credit being valid, FEOC problems flow straight through to the direct-pay claim.

Confirm both the beginning-of-construction date and FEOC compliance **before** relying on a direct-pay election.

## Direct Pay vs. Transferability

Both monetize credits, but they solve different problems:

| Feature | Direct Pay (§6417) | Transferability (§6418) |
|---|---|---|
| Who uses it | Applicable entities (tax-exempts, governments, tribes, co-ops) | Taxable businesses that can't use the full credit |
| What you receive | Cash payment from the **IRS** | Cash from a **third-party buyer** |
| Amount | Face value of credit (subject to domestic-content phase-out for 1 MW+) | Face value **minus a market discount** |
| Frequency | Elected on the return | **One-time** transfer of a given credit |
| Best for | Nonprofits, municipalities, tribes, public power | For-profit developers/owners |

A **for-profit** owner generally cannot use direct pay for the solar ITC and turns to transferability. A **nonprofit or government** owner generally *can* use direct pay and often prefers it, because it captures the full credit value rather than accepting a buyer's discount. See [Transferability of Credits (§6418)](incentive-transferability.md) and [The "Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA)](big-beautiful-bill.md).

## Worked Example: A Municipal Solar Project

**Facts.** A city builds a **900 kW AC** rooftop-and-carport solar system on municipal buildings. Total eligible basis is **$1,800,000**. The project qualifies for a **30% §48E investment credit** (assume prevailing-wage/apprenticeship met; no bonus adders modeled).

```
Eligible basis                         $1,800,000
Base ITC rate (§48E)                    x    30%
--------------------------------------------------
Credit amount                          $  540,000
```

**Monetization.** The city is a **local government — an applicable entity** — and owes no federal income tax. Without direct pay, this $540,000 credit would be worthless to it. With **direct pay (§6417)**:

- The system is **under 1 MW AC**, so the **domestic-content phase-out does not apply** — the city can claim the **full $540,000** even if the project does not meet domestic content.
- The city completes **ECO pre-filing registration** (targeting at least 120 days before filing), receives a registration number, files the appropriate return with the election and the registration number, and the IRS pays **$540,000 as a lump-sum payment**.

```
Credit claimed via direct pay          $  540,000
Domestic-content phase-out (<1 MW)          none
--------------------------------------------------
Cash received from IRS                  $  540,000
```

Had this instead been a **2 MW** municipal project (1 MW+) that began construction in 2026 and **failed domestic content**, the direct-pay amount would drop to **0%** — the city would receive **nothing** via direct pay for that credit unless it met domestic content or a statutory exception. That contrast is the entire reason domestic content is a day-one design decision for larger public projects.
