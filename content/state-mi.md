# Michigan Solar Incentives & Policy

Michigan has replaced traditional retail net metering with a **distributed generation (DG) program** that uses an **inflow/outflow tariff** — you pay full retail for grid electricity you consume and receive a lower credit for surplus you export. The state offers **no income tax credit** and **no solar-specific sales tax exemption**, and its property tax treatment is nuanced. Michigan's major story is its 2023 clean energy law, which raised the DG participation cap and set aggressive long-term clean-energy targets.

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Incentive / Policy | Residential | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| Billing structure | DG inflow/outflow tariff (utility-specific) | DG inflow/outflow tariff (up to 550 kW) |
| SREC market | None | None |
| State income tax credit | None | None |
| Sales tax exemption | None (6% applies) | None (6% applies) |

| Utility rebates (solar) | None from DTE/Consumers | None from DTE/Consumers |

> Michigan's solar economics rest primarily on the **DG export credit** plus (for businesses) the federal commercial ITC — not on state tax credits, sales exemptions, or utility rebates, which the state largely does not offer.

## Overview

Michigan closed traditional net metering to new applicants following **PA 341 of 2016** and subsequent MPSC orders. New solar customers of the major investor-owned utilities enroll in a **distributed generation (DG)** program governed by the **Michigan Public Service Commission (MPSC)**. The 2023 clean energy package (notably **PA 235 of 2023**) then substantially expanded who can participate and set a statewide clean-energy trajectory.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Michigan's DG program uses an **inflow/outflow** methodology filed by each utility in its rate cases:

- **Inflow:** electricity you draw from the grid is billed at your **normal full retail rate**.
- **Outflow:** electricity you generate but export is credited at a **lower rate** — generally the power-supply component of the retail rate, excluding distribution/transmission and other charges.

The MPSC's own consumer guidance states plainly that exported electricity "is credited to you at a lower rate than what is done in a traditional net metering program."

**Utility specifics:**

- **DTE Electric — Rider 18.** Credits outflow at the power-supply component (minus transmission); non-time-based customers may be credited at a monthly average real-time locational price.
- **Consumers Energy** operates an equivalent DG tariff.

> **Verify current export credits.** The exact cents-per-kWh outflow credit changes with each utility rate case. Do not rely on a fixed figure — confirm against DTE's and Consumers' current rate books and MPSC dockets.

**The old 1% cap has been lifted.** Under **PA 235 of 2023**, the aggregate DG cap rose from 1% to **10% of a utility's average in-state peak load** (split between systems ≤20 kW and larger systems). PA 235 also raised the individual system size limit to **550 kW** and increased the sizing allowance to **110%** of prior 12-month consumption. Utilities filed updated DG tariffs, all approved by the MPSC in March 2025. Any reference describing a 1% cap is out of date.

For a primer on how inflow/outflow crediting affects payback, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

## SREC / RECs

**Michigan has no SREC market.** There is no solar carve-out in the state's renewable standard, so there is no state SREC trading market for residential systems. See the [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md) for how solar certificate markets work in states that have them.

Michigan's **clean energy standard** (PA 235 of 2023) sets a Renewable Energy Standard of **50% by 2030 and 60% by 2035**, and a Clean Energy Standard of **80% by 2035 and 100% by 2040**. These are utility compliance obligations, not a homeowner incentive.

## State Tax Credits

**Michigan offers no state income tax credit for residential or commercial solar.**

## Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are separate from state policy:

- **Residential (§25D):** The 30% federal residential clean energy credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally do **not** qualify.
- **Commercial (ITC / §48E):** Business-owned solar may still qualify for the commercial investment tax credit and the technology-neutral **§48E** credit, subject to current rules and phase-downs.

See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

Neither **DTE** nor **Consumers Energy** offers an upfront cash rebate for installing residential rooftop solar. Their solar economics rely on the DG export credit plus federal incentives.

- **DTE MIGreenPower** and Consumers' comparable program are **voluntary green-power purchase** programs — they let customers attribute usage to utility renewable projects for a premium. They are **not** rebates for customer-owned solar.
- **EV programs** exist separately (e.g., DTE's Level 2 home charger rebate for EV-owning customers on an EV time-of-use rate; higher amounts for income-qualified customers). Verify current amounts.
- Some **municipal utilities** (e.g., Lansing BWL) have offered their own solar rebates — check locally.

> **Battery storage:** No confirmed standalone residential battery rebate from DTE or Consumers was verifiable. Do not assume a storage incentive exists — verify at DSIRE/official site.

## Sales Tax Exemption

**Michigan has no solar-specific sales tax exemption.** Solar equipment purchases are subject to the standard **6% sales tax**.

> Many third-party solar marketing sites incorrectly claim Michigan exempts solar from sales tax, often confusing it with property tax treatment. This is inaccurate — Michigan Treasury lists no such exemption.

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

This is Michigan's most nuanced area, and residential and commercial treatment differ:

**Residential:** In practice, residential rooftop solar generally does **not** increase a home's assessed/taxable value. However, the statutory exemption certificate program under **MCL 211.7h** is, on its face, closed to devices installed after December 31, 1983. The practical "no assessment increase" outcome rests partly on assessor interpretation.

> **Verify locally.** Do not assume a guaranteed statewide dollar exemption. Confirm treatment with your local city/township assessor.

**Commercial / utility-scale:** The **Solar Energy Facilities Taxation Act (PA 108 of 2023)** lets qualified utility-scale solar facilities obtain a **Solar Energy Facility Exemption Certificate (SEFEC)**, exempting the facility from ad valorem property tax for a **20-year term** and replacing it with a specific per-megawatt Solar Energy Facilities Tax. Absent a SEFEC, commercial/utility-scale solar is generally subject to standard property tax assessment.

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Interconnection follows MPSC interconnection and DG standards, with standardized Level 1–3 agreements for certified projects up to **550 kW** and a statewide standard interconnection agreement (updated under MI Power Grid / PA 235 implementation).
- Local building and electrical permits are separately required.

## Bottom Line

Michigan is a state where **the export credit does most of the work**. Traditional net metering is gone, replaced by an inflow/outflow DG tariff that credits surplus below retail — but the 2023 clean energy law lifted the old 1% cap to 10%, reopening participation. There is **no state income tax credit, no solar sales tax exemption, and no utility solar rebate**, and residential property tax treatment should be confirmed with your local assessor. For 2026 residential buyers, the loss of the federal §25D credit further concentrates value on the DG credit and long-term utility bill savings; businesses can still pursue the commercial ITC/§48E. Verify all current export rates and property tax treatment before signing.

> **Educational use only.** This page is a general reference, not tax, legal, or financial advice. Incentive values, tariff rates, and program rules change frequently. Verify all figures with the MPSC, the Michigan Department of Treasury, your utility, your local assessor, and a qualified professional before making decisions.
