# Third-Party Ownership: Leases vs PPAs

With the homeowner-claimed federal credit (§25D) **terminated for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025**, third-party ownership (TPO) has become one of the main ways the value of a federal solar credit still reaches homeowners. Under a **lease** or a **power purchase agreement (PPA)**, a company owns the system on your roof and claims the **commercial** tax benefits — then prices some of that value into what you pay.

> **Key 2026 point:** You do **not** claim any federal tax credit under a lease or PPA. The **third-party owner** claims the commercial Investment Tax Credit (**§48 / §48E**) and depreciation. Whether that value is worth it to you depends entirely on the contract terms below. See [Residential Solar Credit: Timeline & History](resinc-timeline.md) for why §25D ended and how the commercial credit differs.

## The two structures

| | **Solar lease** | **Power purchase agreement (PPA)** |
|---|---|---|
| What you pay for | Use of the equipment (fixed **monthly rent**) | The **electricity** the system produces (price **per kWh**) |
| Bill basis | Flat monthly amount regardless of output | Varies with how much the system generates |
| Who owns the system | Third-party provider | Third-party provider |
| Who claims federal credit/depreciation | Provider | Provider |
| Typical term | ~20–25 years | ~20–25 years |
| Escalator | Often 0–3%/yr | Often 0–3%/yr |

Both are forms of **not owning** the system. The practical difference: a lease is a fixed payment; a PPA is a per-kWh rate, so your bill tracks production (sunnier months cost more, cloudy months less).

Contrast this with **owning** the system via cash or a loan — covered in [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md).

## Who owns and who claims the tax benefits

Under TPO, the provider (or a tax-equity investor behind it) is the legal owner and:

- Claims the **commercial ITC (§48 / §48E)** on the installed cost, and
- Takes **accelerated depreciation (MACRS)** on the asset.

These benefits are only available to a **business** owner, which is precisely why the model exists: the provider monetizes credits and depreciation you (as a homeowner after 2025) can no longer claim yourself, and passes some of that value through as a below-market rate.

> **Timing risk for the commercial credit (verify before signing):** OBBBA also tightened the **commercial** solar credit. For §48E, widely reported guidance requires projects to **begin construction by July 4, 2026** (or otherwise be placed in service by the end of 2027) to qualify under current rules, with new restrictions on certain leasing arrangements and foreign-entity sourcing. See the IRS [Beginning of Construction notice (Notice 2025-42)](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-42.pdf) and confirm current eligibility with the provider, because the pass-through value of a lease/PPA depends on the provider actually earning the credit.

## How savings are passed through to you

You do not see the credit as a line item; you see it as a **rate**. The provider prices the ITC and depreciation into a monthly lease payment or per-kWh PPA rate that is meant to sit **below your utility's rate**. Your savings are the gap between what you'd have paid the utility and what you pay the provider — over the whole contract, net of escalators.

Independent estimates generally find that **owning saves more over 20–25 years than leasing or a PPA**, because the homeowner-owner keeps 100% of the credit value and pays no financier margin. TPO trades some lifetime savings for **no upfront cost and no maintenance responsibility.**

## Escalators — read this clause carefully

Most leases and PPAs include an **annual escalator** that raises your payment or rate each year, commonly **1–3%**.

- A **1–2% escalator** is often competitive if local utility rates rise faster.
- A **3%+ escalator** can **erode or erase your savings** if utility rates rise slowly, because your payment climbs on a fixed schedule while the utility bill you're being compared against may not.

> **Do the math on year 20, not year 1.** A payment that looks great in the first year can exceed the utility rate late in the term under a high escalator. Ask the provider for the **full payment schedule** across the entire term.

## Buyout

Most TPO contracts include a **buyout option**, often first available around **year 5 or 6** and again at term's end.

- The buyout price is typically the system's **fair market value** at that time, defined by a contract formula.
- Some newer agreements offer a **near-nominal transfer** of ownership after the tax-benefit period (commonly ~year 5–6), which can add value if you later want to own outright.

Get the buyout formula in writing before signing; a vague "market value" clause can produce a surprisingly high number.

## Transfer at home sale

This is where TPO most often causes friction:

- A buyer must usually **assume the remaining lease/PPA** (a credit check and contract transfer), **buy the system out**, or have the seller do so at closing.
- Some buyers are wary of taking on a 15+ year obligation, which can **complicate or slow a sale**.
- By contrast, an **owned, paid-off** system typically transfers with the house and is easier to appraise into value.

If you may sell within the term, understand the **transfer, buyout, and escalator** terms up front — they materially affect resale.

## Pros and cons vs owning

| | **Third-party ownership (lease/PPA)** | **Owning (cash or loan)** |
|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | Typically $0 down | Cash outlay or financed |
| Federal tax credit | Claimed by **provider** (may lower your rate) | For 2026+ systems, **§25D no longer available** to you |
| Maintenance/repairs | Provider's responsibility | Homeowner's responsibility |
| Lifetime savings | Generally **lower** | Generally **higher** |
| Payment growth | Escalator can raise cost yearly | Fixed loan payment or none after payoff |
| Home sale | Must transfer/assume/buy out | Transfers with home |
| Best fit | Little/no tax appetite, wants simplicity, no upfront cost | Wants maximum long-term savings and control |

## How to evaluate a specific offer

1. Get the **full payment or rate schedule** for the entire term, including every escalator.
2. Compare year-by-year against a **realistic utility-rate forecast** — not a rosy one.
3. Confirm the **buyout formula and earliest buyout date** in writing.
4. Read the **home-sale transfer** terms.
5. Compare against an ownership quote (see [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md)), even if you expect to lease.
6. If storage is included, check whether you can enroll in **state/utility battery programs** — see [Residential Storage Incentives](resinc-storage.md) — and who keeps those payments, you or the provider.
