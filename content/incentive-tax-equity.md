# Tax-Equity Structures

A commercial or utility-scale solar project generates two large federal tax benefits — the **Investment Tax Credit (ITC)** and **accelerated (MACRS bonus) depreciation** — but those benefits are only worth cash to an owner with enough **federal tax liability** to absorb them. Many developers are growth-stage companies, funds, or special-purpose entities with little tax appetite. **Tax equity** is the set of financing structures that lets such an owner **sell the tax benefits to an investor who can use them**, in exchange for capital.

This page explains the three classic structures — the **partnership flip**, the **sale-leaseback**, and the **inverted lease** — the roles of sponsor and investor, the capital stack, the 5-year vesting/recapture rule, and how **transferability (§6418)** and **direct pay (§6417)** reshaped and partly relieved the tax-equity market. It stays conceptual. See also [Transferability of Credits (§6418)](incentive-transferability.md), [Direct Pay (Elective Payment)](incentive-direct-pay.md), [MACRS Depreciation](incentive-macrs-depreciation.md), and [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

> **The core problem.** A `30%` ITC plus **`~85%` of basis in accelerated depreciation** is worthless to an entity that owes no tax. Tax equity, transferability, and direct pay are three different answers to the question *"how do I turn tax attributes into cash?"*

## The Two Benefits Being Monetized

| Benefit | What it is | Transferable alone? |
|---|---|---|
| **ITC (§48E / §48)** | `30%` (with PWA) credit against federal tax | **Yes** — via §6418 transfer |
| **MACRS + bonus depreciation** | Deductions recovering ~85% of basis (basis reduced by half the ITC) | **No** — requires a partnership/lease structure |

This split is the key to understanding today's market: **the credit can now be sold with a contract, but the depreciation still needs a structure.** See [MACRS Depreciation](incentive-macrs-depreciation.md).

## The Roles

- **Sponsor / developer.** Builds, operates, and typically retains long-term ownership and upside. Contributes **sponsor equity** and know-how.
- **Tax-equity investor.** A profitable institution (historically large banks and insurers) that contributes capital in exchange for the **bulk of the tax benefits** and a preferred cash return, then steps back once its target return is reached.
- **Lender (optional).** Provides **back-leverage** or project debt, sitting senior to equity in the capital stack.

## The Capital Stack

A typical utility-scale solar project is funded from three sources, roughly:

| Layer | Approx. share of capital | Provided by | Compensated with |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tax equity** | ~`30–40%` | Tax-equity investor | ITC + depreciation + preferred cash |
| **Debt** | ~`40–60%` | Lender | Interest; senior security |
| **Sponsor equity** | Remainder | Developer | Residual cash flow + long-term ownership |

Shares vary widely with project type, credit adders, PPA strength, and interest rates. **Verify against current market terms** — the mix moved sharply after transferability arrived.

## Structure 1 — Partnership Flip

The **most common** tax-equity structure (historically ~80% of solar tax-equity deals). The sponsor and investor form a **partnership** that owns the project.

1. Before the **flip point**, the investor is allocated the large majority of tax benefits — commonly **`99%` of the ITC and depreciation** — plus a preferred cash return.
2. Once the investor reaches its **target after-tax yield** (a *yield-based* flip) or a **fixed date** (a *time-based* flip), allocations **flip** so the sponsor receives most of the cash and residual value.
3. The sponsor often holds an option to **buy out** the investor's residual interest at fair market value.

> **Why it survives §6418:** a partnership flip monetizes **both** the credit **and** depreciation (and passes through cash), which a pure credit sale cannot. Deals increasingly **combine** a flip (for depreciation) with a §6418 **transfer** of the credit.

## Structure 2 — Sale-Leaseback

The developer **sells** the completed project to the tax investor and immediately **leases it back**, operating it under the lease.

- The **investor becomes the owner**, so it claims the **ITC and depreciation**; it must make the ITC election within **3 months** of placing the asset in service.
- The developer pays **lease payments** and retains operational control and the customer/PPA relationship.
- Often achieves the **highest advance rate** because the investor can base the ITC on **fair market value** (a step-up), not just cost.
- A **fixed-price purchase option** at lease end can let the developer reacquire the asset.

## Structure 3 — Inverted Lease (Lease Pass-Through)

Also called a **lease pass-through**. The developer/lessor **retains ownership and the depreciation** but **passes the ITC through** to the investor/lessee via a **§50(d) election**.

- The **investor gets the ITC**; the **sponsor keeps depreciation** — a mirror image of the sale-leaseback split.
- No basis step-up (the ITC is based on cost), and no immediate sale of the asset.
- More complex; historically used when the parties want to divide credit and depreciation between them.

## Structure Comparison

| Feature | Partnership Flip | Sale-Leaseback | Inverted Lease |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who owns the asset | The **partnership** | **Investor** (leases back to developer) | **Developer/lessor** |
| Who gets the **ITC** | Investor (pre-flip) | Investor | Investor (passed through) |
| Who gets **depreciation** | Investor (pre-flip) | Investor | **Developer** |
| Basis step-up to FMV | No | **Yes** (often) | No |
| Relative complexity | High | Moderate | High |
| Prevalence (historic) | **Most common** | Common | Least common |

## How §6418 Transferability Changed the Market

**Transferability** (IRC **§6418**, added by the IRA) lets a taxable owner **sell the ITC/PTC to an unrelated buyer for cash** under a contract — no partnership required. Its effect on tax equity:

- **It commoditized the credit.** A buyer purchases the credit at a **discount** (ITCs have commonly traded around **`90–95` cents on the dollar** — *verify current pricing*). This opened credit-buying to a far wider pool of ordinary profitable corporations, not just a handful of specialized tax-equity investors.
- **It did not replace tax equity.** Transferability **monetizes only the credit**, not the **depreciation** or project cash flows. To capture depreciation, the sponsor still needs a **partnership or lease** structure — so many deals now pair a **flip for depreciation** with a **§6418 sale of the credit**.
- **It shifted recapture risk to the buyer.** Under §6418 the transferee generally bears recapture exposure, driving **indemnities** and **tax insurance** into transfer agreements.

See [Transferability of Credits (§6418)](incentive-transferability.md).

## How §6417 Direct Pay Relieved the Market

**Direct pay** (IRC **§6417**) lets **applicable entities** — tax-exempts, governments, tribes, and rural electric cooperatives — treat the credit as an **overpayment of tax** and receive it as a **cash refund from the IRS**, with **no investor and no discount**.

- For a nonprofit or municipality, direct pay **removes the need for tax equity entirely** for the *credit* — no partner, no flip, no buyer.
- It does **not** monetize depreciation (these entities usually have none), so the structuring problem largely disappears for them.

See [Direct Pay (Elective Payment)](incentive-direct-pay.md).

## Recapture & the 5-Year Vesting Cliff

The **ITC vests over 5 years**, `20%` per year. If the property is **sold, disposed of, or ceases to be investment credit property** during that window, a portion of the credit is **recaptured** (clawed back):

| Year of disqualifying event | ITC recaptured |
|---|---|
| Within year 1 | `100%` |
| Within year 2 | `80%` |
| Within year 3 | `60%` |
| Within year 4 | `40%` |
| Within year 5 | `20%` |
| After 5 full years | `0%` (fully vested) |

Recapture drives structure design: the **investor's exit** (a flip, buyout, or sale) is timed to **avoid triggering recapture**, and change-of-control and disposition are tightly controlled during the 5-year period. See [Recapture considerations](incentive-transferability.md) for how it interacts with credit sales.

## Typical Economics (High Level)

- The tax-equity investor targets an **after-tax internal rate of return** built from the **credit + depreciation + preferred cash**, then flips or exits.
- **Depreciation** alone can be worth roughly **`20–26%`** of eligible basis in present-value tax savings (rate- and timing-dependent) — a large reason structures persist even after transferability.
- **Combined deals** (flip + credit transfer) can raise more capital than either alone, at the cost of more legal and accounting work.

> These are **conceptual ranges**, not quotes. Actual advance rates, flip yields, and discounts depend on the PPA, credit adders, FEOC status, interest rates, and counterparties. **Verify current market terms** with qualified advisors.
