# Ohio Solar Incentives & Policy

Ohio has a **small, oversupplied SREC market**, **net metering** that credits exports below full retail (at the generation/energy rate), **no state income-tax credit**, and the useful **ECO-Link (a PACE-style financing tool)**. Statewide sales/property tax treatment is limited and often misstated. This guide covers residential and commercial systems and where they differ.

> **Note (as of 2026-06-30):** Ohio's RPS/AEPS and net-metering rules have shifted over the years, and SREC prices are low and volatile. Treat figures below as directional and **verify at PUCO, the Ohio Department of Development, and [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/)** before relying on them.

## Overview

Ohio's distributed-solar value is modest at the state level: the **SREC market is small and oversupplied**, net-metering exports are compensated **below full retail**, and there is **no state income-tax credit**. The most concrete state-adjacent tools are **financing** mechanisms — **ECO-Link** (an interest-rate buy-down) for residential and small projects, and **Commercial PACE (C-PACE)** for commercial. Ohio's solar carve-out sits under its **Alternative Energy Portfolio Standard (AEPS)**, which drives limited SREC demand.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

- Ohio requires **investor-owned utilities** to offer **net metering**, commonly for **residential systems up to about 25 kW**.
- **Important:** Ohio does **not** provide full-retail 1:1 credit. Exported energy is generally credited at the **generation (energy) portion** of the rate — often around **4–5 cents/kWh** — **not** the full retail rate that includes delivery. This makes exports worth substantially less than in full-retail states.
- **Commercial systems** and larger installs follow separate tariffs; confirm size limits and credit treatment with the utility and PUCO.

Because export credit is low, **self-consumption** (using generation on-site, especially for commercial daytime loads) is key to Ohio economics. See [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

## SREC / RECs

- Ohio has a **compliance SREC market** tied to the AEPS solar requirement, but it is **small and oversupplied**, so prices are **very low** (recently only a few dollars per MWh). The RPS/solar carve-out was frozen and weakened in the 2010s, undercutting demand.
- **1 SREC = 1 MWh.** Residential SREC revenue is negligible; **commercial** projects generate more volume but should model SREC income **very conservatively (near zero)**.

For how Ohio compares to functioning tradable markets, see [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

## State Tax Credits

Ohio has **no state solar income-tax credit** for residential or commercial systems.

## Federal Tax Credits

- **Residential (§25D):** The 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under **OBBBA**. Systems placed in service on/after **January 1, 2026** do **not** qualify.
- **Commercial (ITC / §48E):** The **Clean Electricity Investment Credit (§48E)** remains available for businesses/TPO systems, subject to **begin-construction** deadlines and phase-outs, and pairs with **MACRS depreciation**.

Details and timelines: [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

- **ECO-Link Program** (administered via the Ohio Treasurer): provides up to a **~3% interest-rate reduction for up to 7 years** on qualifying bank loans that include solar; loan cap around **$50,000** with a minimum share for the qualifying improvement. This is **financing help, not a rebate**, but it can materially lower all-in cost.
- **Commercial PACE (C-PACE):** Ohio has active C-PACE districts enabling long-term financing of commercial solar/efficiency repaid via a property assessment — a common commercial tool (sometimes referenced as "ECO-PACE"-style programs).
- **Utility-specific** rebates/efficiency programs vary; check locally.

## Sales Tax Exemption

Ohio's residential solar **sales-tax treatment is frequently overstated online**. There is **no broad, well-established statewide residential solar sales-tax exemption** comparable to Minnesota's. Certain **commercial/production or qualified-energy-project** exemptions may apply in specific cases. **Verify current treatment** with the Ohio Department of Taxation before assuming an exemption applies.

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

- For **larger/commercial and utility-scale** projects, Ohio offers a **Qualified Energy Project (QEP)** exemption path, under which projects can be exempt from tangible personal-property/real-property tax in exchange for a **payment-in-lieu-of-taxes (PILOT)** and local approval.
- **Residential** rooftop property-tax treatment is limited and depends on local assessment. **Verify** with the county auditor and the Ohio Department of Development/DSIRE.

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Interconnection follows **PUCO / utility interconnection rules**; small residential systems use expedited review.
- Local **AHJ permitting** varies by municipality.
- SREC/AEPS participation requires **system registration** and a **REC tracking** account (PJM-GATS).

## Bottom Line

- **Residential:** Ohio is a **weaker-incentive state** — **below-retail export credit**, a **near-worthless SREC market**, **no state credit**, and (from 2026) **no federal §25D credit**. **ECO-Link financing** and maximizing **self-consumption** are the main levers.
- **Commercial:** **§48E ITC + MACRS**, **C-PACE financing**, and possible **QEP property-tax** treatment drive returns; prioritize **on-site self-consumption** because export credit and SRECs add little.
