# C&I Financing: PPAs, Leases, C-PACE & On-Bill

Commercial and industrial (C&I) solar projects are large enough that the **financing structure** often matters more than the equipment choice. The right structure determines who pays the upfront cost, who owns the system, who claims the **Investment Tax Credit (ITC)** and depreciation, and whether the asset lands on the customer's balance sheet. This guide walks through the main ways C&I solar gets paid for, who claims the tax benefits in each, and how to compare them.

For the incentives referenced throughout, see [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md). For pairing generation with storage to cut demand charges, see [Commercial Storage & Demand-Charge Management](commercial-storage.md). If you cannot host a system on-site at all, see [Community Solar](commercial-community-solar.md).

## The Two Fundamental Choices

Every financing path is a variation on one question: **do you own the system, or does someone else?**

- **Direct ownership** (cash or loan): you own the array, so you claim the ITC and depreciation and keep all the energy savings. You also carry the capital cost, the tax appetite needed to use the credits, and the operations-and-maintenance (O&M) responsibility.
- **Third-party ownership (TPO)**: a developer, investor, or **tax-equity** partner owns the system. They claim the tax benefits and sell you the power or lease you the equipment. You avoid the capital outlay and the need for tax appetite, but you give up the tax benefits and typically save less over the system's life.

> **Tax appetite** is the amount of tax liability a business actually owes. The ITC and depreciation are only valuable to an owner who owes enough tax to absorb them. A non-profit, a municipality, or a company with net operating losses may have little or no appetite — which historically pushed those customers toward TPO structures (though **elective pay**, discussed below, changes this for tax-exempt entities).

## Direct Ownership: Cash and Loans

### Cash purchase

The simplest structure. The business buys the system outright, owns it, and claims:

- The **federal ITC** on eligible basis (see [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md)).
- Accelerated depreciation under **MACRS** (solar is generally 5-year property), plus any bonus depreciation available in the placed-in-service year.

Cash purchase produces the **lowest lifetime cost per kWh** and the highest lifetime return, because there is no financing cost and no third-party margin. The trade-off is the capital tie-up and the requirement to have tax appetite to monetize the incentives.

### Solar loan

A loan preserves ownership — and therefore the ITC and depreciation — while spreading the cost over time. Structures include equipment loans, commercial term loans, and specialized solar loans. Key variables:

- **Term** (often 5-20 years) and interest rate.
- Whether the loan is **secured** by the equipment, the property, or a general lien.
- A common design is a **re-amortizing** or "combo" loan sized so the owner applies the ITC as a lump-sum paydown after year one, dropping the payment thereafter.

Ownership stays with the business, so all savings and tax benefits accrue to it; the lender simply earns interest.

## Monetizing the ITC and Depreciation

For owners without enough tax appetite, the industry developed structures to transfer the tax value to a partner who can use it:

- **Tax-equity partnerships** (e.g., partnership flips, sale-leasebacks, inverted leases): an investor with tax appetite takes an ownership interest primarily to claim the ITC and depreciation, then "flips" a larger share of cash to the developer/host once its target return is met.
- **Transferability**: under current federal law, the ITC (and other clean-energy credits) can be **sold for cash** to an unrelated taxpayer, which has broadened the pool of buyers beyond traditional tax-equity investors.
- **Elective pay ("direct pay")**: tax-exempt entities — governments, schools, non-profits, tribes, co-ops — can receive the credit value as a **direct payment** rather than needing tax liability. This lets many public-sector hosts own their systems instead of relying on TPO.

> These mechanisms are technical and fast-changing. Confirm current eligibility, phase-out timing, and any domestic-content or prevailing-wage conditions with a tax advisor and the [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md) guide before modeling a deal.

## Third-Party Structures

### Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)

Under a **solar PPA**, the developer owns, installs, operates, and maintains the system on the customer's roof or land. The customer buys the **electricity the system produces** at an agreed **price per kWh**, usually set below the current utility rate.

- **Who owns / claims the credit**: the developer (or its tax-equity/transfer partner). The host does not claim the ITC or depreciation.
- **Payment**: `monthly PPA cost = kWh produced x PPA rate`. You pay only for solar delivered.
- **Escalator**: PPA rates commonly include an annual **escalator** (often in the low single digits, e.g., ~1-3%). A flat (0%) escalator front-loads savings less but protects against over-paying if utility rates stagnate.
- **Term**: frequently 15-25 years, often with buyout options at set points and end-of-term renewal or removal.
- **Use case**: customers who want savings with no capital, no O&M, and no tax appetite — retail chains, schools, hospitals, and non-profits.

### Solar lease

A **lease** is similar but you pay a **fixed monthly amount for the equipment** rather than per kWh; you then consume whatever the system produces. Leases split into two accounting flavors:

- **Operating lease**: treated more like a rental. Historically favored for keeping the asset off the balance sheet, though modern lease-accounting standards (ASC 842) now bring most leases onto the balance sheet as a right-of-use asset — confirm treatment with your accountant.
- **Capital / finance lease**: economically closer to a purchase; the lessee may effectively be treated as owner for some purposes.

The lessor generally owns the equipment and claims the tax benefits (unless structured as a finance lease with a bargain purchase option).

### Energy Service Agreement (ESA)

An **ESA**, or "solar/efficiency-as-a-service," is a broader contract where the provider owns the equipment and the customer pays for a **delivered outcome** — energy, savings against a baseline, or a managed-energy service — rather than strictly per-kWh production. ESAs are common when bundling solar with efficiency, storage, or other measures. The provider owns and claims the tax benefits.

## C-PACE (Commercial Property Assessed Clean Energy)

**C-PACE** lets a property owner finance up to **100%** of an eligible project and repay it as a **special assessment on the property tax bill**, secured by a lien on the property.

### How the assessment works

1. A state (or local) **enabling authority** and program administrator authorize C-PACE in the jurisdiction.
2. A private **capital provider** funds the project (energy, water, resilience, and often new-construction measures).
3. Repayment is collected as a **line item on the property tax bill**, typically over **10-30 years** — often matched to the useful life of the equipment.
4. A **PACE lien** attaches to the property. It is generally **senior** to most other debt, which supports the long terms and can lower rates; interest rates are commonly in the mid-to-high single digits.

### Key features

- **Runs with the property**: if the building is sold, the remaining assessment typically **transfers to the new owner** (unless prepaid), since it is tied to the real estate, not the borrower.
- **Ownership stays with the property owner**, so the owner **claims the ITC and MACRS** — combining ownership tax benefits with 100%, off-cash-flow financing.
- **Mortgage-holder consent** is usually required because of the senior lien.

### Availability

C-PACE requires state **enabling legislation** plus an **active local program**. A large and growing majority of states plus D.C. have enabling laws, and roughly 30-plus have active programs, though coverage and program rules vary significantly by county and municipality. Always verify local availability and terms with the state program office. See the [U.S. DOE C-PACE resources](https://www.energy.gov/scep/slsc/articles/commercial-property-assessed-clean-energy-fact-sheet-state-and-local-governments) and [EPA C-PACE overview](https://www.epa.gov/statelocalenergy/commercial-property-assessed-clean-energy).

## On-Bill Financing and On-Bill Repayment

**On-bill financing (OBF)** and **on-bill repayment (OBR)** let a customer repay a solar or efficiency project through a **charge on the utility bill**.

- In **on-bill financing**, the **utility (or a program) provides the capital**.
- In **on-bill repayment**, a **third-party lender** provides capital and the utility acts as the billing/collection channel.

Terms, eligibility, and whether the obligation is tied to the meter or the customer vary widely by utility and state. Many OBF programs target efficiency more than generation, and some are aimed at smaller commercial customers. Availability is **utility- and program-specific**, so confirm with the local utility.

## Balance-Sheet vs. Off-Balance-Sheet

A frequent motivation for TPO (PPA/lease/ESA) is keeping the asset and its financing **off the balance sheet**, preserving borrowing capacity and financial ratios. Two cautions:

- Under **ASC 842**, most leases now appear on the balance sheet as a right-of-use asset and liability, narrowing the historical off-balance-sheet advantage of operating leases.
- **PPAs and service agreements** may still be treated as operating/service contracts in some cases, but classification is fact-specific. Involve your accountant early.

## Comparison Table

| Structure | Who owns | Who claims ITC / depreciation | Upfront cash | Typical term | Escalator? | Balance sheet | Best-fit customer |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash purchase** | Customer | Customer | Full | n/a | n/a | On (asset) | Tax-appetite firms wanting max return |
| **Solar loan** | Customer | Customer | Low/none | 5-20 yr | n/a | On (asset + debt) | Owners wanting ownership, less capital |
| **C-PACE** | Customer | Customer | None (100% financed) | 10-30 yr | n/a | On (assessment) | Property owners; long-hold real estate |
| **On-bill (OBF/OBR)** | Customer (usually) | Customer (usually) | None | Varies | n/a | Varies | Smaller C&I in participating utilities |
| **PPA** | Developer | Developer | None | 15-25 yr | Often ~1-3%/yr | Often off (service) | No tax appetite / no capital / no O&M |
| **Lease (operating)** | Lessor | Lessor | Low/none | 7-20 yr | Often fixed | Usually on under ASC 842 | Wants fixed payment, no ownership |
| **ESA** | Provider | Provider | None | 10-20 yr | Varies | Often off (service) | Bundled solar + efficiency + storage |

> Ranges above are directional and vary by market, credit, and program. Do not treat them as quotes.

## A Simple Cash-Flow Illustration

The example below shows the **shape** of the decision, not real quotes. Assume a **500 kW** rooftop system, roughly **750,000 kWh/year** of production, and a **$0.14/kWh** avoided utility rate — about **$105,000/year** in gross energy value in year one.

```
Ownership (cash / loan):
  Year 1 energy savings ................. ~$105,000
  + ITC (claimed once, per current rules)  large one-time credit
  + MACRS depreciation (years 1-6) ......  additional tax savings
  - O&M, insurance, inverter reserve ....  annual cost
  = Highest lifetime net benefit; customer carries capital & tax-appetite need

PPA (3rd-party owned, e.g. PPA rate < utility rate):
  Year 1 PPA cost = 750,000 kWh x PPA rate
  Savings = (utility rate - PPA rate) x kWh, minus the escalator drift over time
  = No capital, no O&M, no tax appetite needed; developer keeps ITC/depreciation
  = Lower lifetime savings than ownership, but $0 down and predictable

C-PACE (owned, 100% financed):
  Year 1 energy savings ................. ~$105,000
  - annual C-PACE assessment on tax bill   (principal + interest)
  + customer still claims ITC + MACRS
  = Ownership tax benefits with no cash down; assessment runs with the property
```

The consistent pattern: **ownership structures maximize lifetime value but require capital and/or tax appetite; third-party structures trade some lifetime value for zero-down simplicity.** C-PACE and elective-pay/transferability tools increasingly let customers get ownership benefits without the classic barriers.

## Choosing a Structure by Customer Type

| Customer type | Common fit |
|---|---|
| Profitable corporate with tax appetite | Cash or loan (keep ITC + depreciation) |
| Real-estate owner, long hold | C-PACE (runs with property, 100% financed) |
| Non-profit / school / hospital | PPA/ESA historically; **elective pay** now enables ownership |
| Government / municipality | **Elective pay** ownership, or PPA |
| Multi-tenant / triple-net lease building | PPA or C-PACE; watch cost pass-through terms |
| Smaller C&I in supportive utility | On-bill financing/repayment |
