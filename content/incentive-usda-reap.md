# USDA REAP Grants

The **Rural Energy for America Program (REAP)**, administered by **USDA Rural Development**, is one of the most valuable non-tax incentives available for commercial solar in rural America. It provides **grants** (money that does not have to be repaid) and **loan guarantees** to help **agricultural producers** and **rural small businesses** install renewable energy systems — including solar — and make energy-efficiency improvements.

For a qualifying solar project, a REAP grant can cover a meaningful share of installed cost, and it **stacks on top of** the federal [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md) and [MACRS & Bonus Depreciation](incentive-macrs-depreciation.md), dramatically improving project economics.

> **Verification note (as of 2026-06-30):** Program parameters below reflect the FY 2025–2027 Notice of Funding Opportunity and current USDA Rural Development guidance. **REAP funding and application windows are in flux** — USDA delayed/paused the FY 2026 grant application period and, as of recent guidance, was **not accepting new grant applications** at certain points (guaranteed-loan applications continued). Always confirm current funding status, deadlines, and percentages with your **state USDA Rural Development office** before relying on these figures.

See also: [The "Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA)](big-beautiful-bill.md).

## What REAP Is

REAP supports two project types:

- **Renewable Energy Systems (RES)** — including **solar PV**, wind, geothermal, hydro, and biomass.
- **Energy Efficiency Improvement (EEI)** — upgrades such as lighting, HVAC, insulation, refrigeration, and irrigation.

Support comes in three forms, which can be combined:

| Assistance type | What it provides |
|-----------------|------------------|
| **Grant** | Non-repayable funds covering a share of eligible project cost |
| **Loan guarantee** | USDA guarantees a portion of a lender's loan, improving access/terms |
| **Combined grant + loan guarantee** | Both, up to a combined cap of eligible project cost |

> **Key point:** Unlike the ITC and depreciation (which require tax liability to use), a **REAP grant is cash** — valuable even to borrowers with little or no taxable income, and especially powerful when combined with the federal tax benefits.

## Eligibility

To qualify, an applicant must be **either**:

- An **agricultural producer** — a farm or ranch that derives **at least 50% of gross income from agricultural operations** (these may be located in rural *or* non-rural areas), **or**
- A **small business** located in an **eligible rural area** (generally areas with populations of **50,000 or fewer**, outside urbanized areas). Small-business status is defined by SBA size standards.

Additional core requirements:

- The applicant must have **no delinquent federal debt** and demonstrate **financial feasibility**.
- The project must be **technically feasible** (often requiring a technical report or energy assessment).
- The applicant must demonstrate **matching funds** for the non-grant share.
- The system must be **for the applicant's own use** (on-site/behind-the-meter use is typical, though some excess generation rules apply).

> **Key point:** Use the USDA **Rural Development property eligibility map** to confirm a site qualifies as rural. **Agricultural producers can qualify even in non-rural locations**, which is a common point of confusion.

## Grant Share, Loan Guarantees, and Caps

> **Verified (as of 2026-06-30):** Under **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)** funding, the maximum REAP grant share was raised to **up to 50% of total eligible project costs** for qualifying projects — double the historical **25%** Farm Bill cap. The grant share that applies depends on the **funding source** USDA assigns to your application.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Grant share — IRA funds** | Up to **50%** of total eligible project costs |
| **Grant share — Farm Bill / base funds** | Up to **25%** of total eligible project costs |
| **RES grant — minimum / maximum** | **$2,500** min / **$1,000,000** max |
| **EEI grant — minimum / maximum** | **$1,500** min / **$500,000** max |
| **Loan guarantee** | Up to **75%** of eligible cost guaranteed (FY 2025 guarantee terms were enhanced, e.g., an 80% guarantee on qualifying loans) |
| **Combined grant + loan guarantee** | Up to **75%** of total eligible project costs |

> **Key point:** The **50% grant share is not automatic** — it applies to projects funded from the IRA allocation that meet the qualifying criteria; base/Farm Bill funding is capped at **25%**. Confirm which pool funds your award. Enhanced FY 2025 loan-guarantee terms may not carry forward unchanged into later fiscal years.

## Eligible Project Costs

REAP grants apply to **total eligible project costs**, which for a solar installation typically include:

- Solar modules, inverters, racking, and balance-of-system equipment
- Installation labor and electrical work
- Permitting, interconnection, and engineering directly tied to the project
- Required energy assessments / technical reports (within program limits)

Ineligible costs generally include agricultural tillage equipment, used equipment that isn't in good repair, residential portions of mixed-use property, and costs incurred before the application is filed (other than allowed pre-application items).

## How REAP Stacks With the ITC and Depreciation

A critical advantage of REAP is that it **combines** with the federal tax incentives. The key interaction is on **basis**:

- A **REAP grant is generally treated as taxable income** to the recipient (unless a specific exclusion applies) and **typically reduces the depreciable basis** of the property. Treatment can vary — confirm with your tax advisor.
- The **federal ITC** is generally calculated on the project's eligible cost. Whether the grant reduces the ITC-eligible basis depends on the facts; many advisors treat the **ITC as still applying to the full system cost** while the grant affects depreciation basis. **This is a fact-specific area — get professional advice.**

> **Key point:** Because the grant, the ITC, and depreciation interact through **basis**, the order and treatment matter. Do **not** assume you can claim 30% ITC + 50% REAP + full depreciation on 100% of cost without adjustment. A tax professional should model the actual stacked benefit for your project.

## Scoring and Competitive Application Cycles

REAP grants are **competitive**. USDA scores applications on published criteria and funds the highest-scoring projects within each cycle's budget. Scoring factors typically include:

- **Energy generated/saved** relative to cost (energy efficiency of the dollar)
- **Environmental benefits**
- **Applicant's commitment** and matching funds
- **Project readiness** and technical merit
- **Previous grantee status** and small/very-small business priorities

USDA generally runs **multiple application windows (quarterly cycles)** per fiscal year. The FY 2025–2027 NOFO originally set deadlines such as **September 30, 2025; December 31, 2025; and March 31, 2026** for grant rounds.

> **Key point (timing caution):** As of recent guidance, USDA **delayed or paused** the FY 2026 grant application period and at points was **not accepting new grant applications**, citing application backlog. **Guaranteed-loan applications continued to be accepted.** Verify the current open window directly with USDA before planning around a deadline.

## How to Apply

1. **Confirm eligibility** — agricultural producer (50% ag income) or rural small business; verify the site on the USDA eligibility map.
2. **Contact your State USDA Rural Development Energy Coordinator** early — they guide applicants and confirm current deadlines and funding status.
3. **Assemble the package** — application forms, project budget/quotes, technical report or energy assessment, financial statements, and evidence of matching funds.
4. **Submit** by the relevant cycle deadline (paper to the state RD office or via the online portal, per current instructions).
5. **Await scoring and obligation** — if awarded, USDA obligates funds; grant funds are typically **reimbursed after** the project is completed and verified.

> **Key point:** Many applicants work with an experienced REAP grant writer or their installer, because scoring rewards a complete, well-documented application. Engage your **state RD office** at the outset.

## Worked Example: REAP + ITC + Depreciation on a Farm Project

This illustrates the *gross* federal-plus-REAP benefit stack. **Basis interactions are simplified** — see the caveat above and consult a tax professional for exact numbers.

```
ASSUMPTIONS
  Project: 100 kW solar PV on an eligible farm
  Total eligible cost ............. $250,000
  REAP grant share (IRA funds) .... 50%  (qualifying project)
  Federal ITC ..................... 30%
  Depreciation .................... 100% bonus (OBBBA, 2025+)
  Federal tax rate ................ 21% (illustrative)

STEP 1 - REAP grant
  Grant = 50% x $250,000 .......... $125,000  (non-repayable cash)
  (Subject to the $1,000,000 RES grant cap - well within limit.)

STEP 2 - Federal ITC (on system cost)
  ITC = 30% x $250,000 ............ $75,000

STEP 3 - Depreciation (simplified)
  ITC basis haircut = 50% x $75,000 = $37,500
  Grant typically also reduces basis: -$125,000  (treatment varies)
  Illustrative depreciable basis ~= $250,000 - $37,500 - $125,000 = $87,500
  Bonus (100%) Year 1 deduction = $87,500
  Depreciation tax savings = $87,500 x 21% = $18,375

ILLUSTRATIVE FIRST-YEAR BENEFIT STACK
  REAP grant ...................... $125,000
  Federal ITC .....................  $75,000
  Depreciation tax savings ........  $18,375
  ------------------------------------------------
  Total benefit (illustrative) .... $218,375
  Net cost after stack (approx) ...  $31,625  (~13% of $250,000)
```

> **Key point:** With a **50% REAP grant + 30% ITC + bonus depreciation**, a qualifying rural project can offset the large majority of installed cost. The exact figure depends on **grant/basis tax treatment**, your tax rate, and state rules — this example is illustrative only. Model the real numbers with a tax professional.
