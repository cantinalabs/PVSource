# Emergency Response & First Aid

Even on a well-run solar site, incidents happen: a worker contacts an energized conductor, an arc flash burns a technician, a battery enclosure vents toxic gas, or someone falls from a tracker or roof. The minutes immediately after an incident often determine the outcome. A site that has planned its response — trained responders, an **Emergency Action Plan (EAP)**, rescue provisions matched to its actual hazards, and clear reporting — saves lives; a site that improvises loses them. This article covers first-aid fundamentals for solar-specific injuries, incident reporting and OSHA recordkeeping, EAPs, and site-specific rescue for rooftop and confined-space work.

For the program framework see the [Solar Safety Overview](safe-overview.md). The hazards behind these emergencies are detailed in [Electrical Safety & Arc Flash](safe-electrical.md), [Working at Heights & Fall Protection](safe-falls.md), and [Fire Safety & Firefighter Considerations](safe-fire.md).

> **Warning:** In any serious incident, protect yourself first. A rescuer who becomes a second casualty helps no one. Do not touch a shock victim still in contact with a live circuit, do not enter an unventilated confined space, and do not rush under a suspended load or into an unstable trench.

## First Aid for Electrical Shock

Electrical injury can stop the heart, arrest breathing, cause deep internal burns along the current path, and throw the victim, causing secondary trauma. What you see on the skin may vastly understate internal damage.

### Immediate Actions

1. **Do not touch the victim if they may still be in contact with the source.** De-energize first — open the disconnect, initiate rapid shutdown for PV where applicable, or use an approved non-conductive means to separate the victim from the source only if you can do so safely.
2. **Call 911 / activate emergency response.** Treat every meaningful electrical contact as potentially serious even if the victim seems fine.
3. **Check responsiveness and breathing.** If the victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally, begin **CPR** and get an **AED** (see below).
4. **Treat for shock and secondary injuries.** Assume possible spinal injury if the person fell or was thrown; control bleeding; keep the person still and warm.
5. **Monitor.** Cardiac rhythm disturbances can occur after electrical contact, so all significant electrical-injury patients need medical evaluation.

### CPR and AED Basics for Electrical Injury

Electrical shock is a leading cause of cardiac arrest on job sites, and prompt CPR plus early defibrillation dramatically improves survival. Follow current **American Heart Association (AHA)** / American Red Cross guidance and your certified training:

- **Push hard and fast:** Chest compressions at **100–120 per minute**, about **2 inches (5 cm)** deep for an adult, allowing full chest recoil, minimizing interruptions.
- **Use an AED as soon as it arrives.** Turn it on and follow the voice prompts. Ensure the victim is not in contact with a live circuit and is dry before applying pads.
- **Ventilations:** If trained, give rescue breaths at the ratio your certification specifies (commonly 30:2); if untrained or unwilling, hands-only CPR is better than nothing.
- **Continue until** the AED/EMS directs otherwise or the victim recovers.

> **Warning:** Do not apply an AED or perform CPR on a victim who is still in contact with an energized source, or who is lying in water or on a wet, conductive surface, until the hazard is removed. Confirm the scene is safe first.

## First Aid for Arc-Flash Burns

An arc flash can cause thermal burns, blast/pressure injury, hearing damage, eye injury from intense light, and inhalation injury.

- **Stop the burning process:** Remove the victim from the hazard once the circuit is safe; smother any burning clothing.
- **Cool thermal burns** with clean, room-temperature water; do not use ice, which can worsen tissue damage.
- **Do not peel away** clothing stuck to a burn; do not apply creams or ointments to serious burns.
- **Cover** with a clean, dry, non-adherent dressing.
- **Watch for airway/inhalation injury** and treat for shock; call 911 for anything beyond a minor burn.
- **Eyes:** For flash/UV eye exposure, seek medical care; flush eyes if there is chemical or particulate contamination.

## Battery Fire and Off-Gas Emergencies

Lithium-ion energy storage introduces hazards most crews rarely see. In **thermal runaway**, cells self-heat, vent flammable and toxic gas, can jet flame, and may reignite hours later. Vented gases can accumulate and create an **explosion (deflagration) hazard**, and the smoke is toxic.

- **Evacuate and establish a perimeter.** Get people upwind and away; the primary hazard is often the invisible off-gas, not visible flame.
- **Do not enter an enclosure filling with gas.** Vapor clouds can deflagrate. Ventilation and gas detection are firefighter/hazmat functions.
- **Call 911 and inform them it involves a lithium battery/ESS.** Responders need to know to bring the right resources and tactics; provide the manufacturer's emergency response information and any Safety Data Sheets.
- **Expect reignition.** A battery that appears out can reignite; only trained responders should determine when it is safe.
- **Treat exposure victims** for smoke/gas inhalation and burns; move them to fresh air and get medical help.

> **Warning:** Never open, probe, or apply water to a suspected thermal-runaway battery enclosure yourself. Vented lithium-battery gas is flammable, toxic, and can explode. Evacuate, call for trained responders, and provide them the ESS emergency documentation.

## First Aid for Falls

Falls from roofs, trackers, and structures produce trauma to the spine, head, and limbs. See [Working at Heights & Fall Protection](safe-falls.md) for prevention.

- **Do not move the victim** unless there is an immediate life threat; assume spinal injury after any significant fall.
- **Call 911**, control bleeding, keep the person still and warm, and monitor breathing.
- **Suspension trauma:** A worker hanging in a fall-arrest harness can develop life-threatening **suspension trauma (orthostatic intolerance)**. Rescue promptly — do not leave a worker suspended. Have a planned means to reach and lower them quickly.

## Emergency Action Plans (EAPs)

OSHA requires a written **Emergency Action Plan** for most workplaces (see 29 CFR 1910.38 / 1926 requirements). A solar-site EAP should be specific to the actual site, not a generic template.

A solid EAP addresses:

- **Emergency reporting** — how to raise the alarm and call for help; who calls 911.
- **Evacuation** — routes, assembly points, and headcount/accountability procedures.
- **Site address and directions** for EMS, including gate access and staging on remote utility sites.
- **On-site medical/first-aid** resources, trained responders, AED and first-aid kit locations.
- **Rescue plans** for the site's specific hazards (rooftop, confined space, trench, suspended worker).
- **Hazard-specific procedures** — electrical, ESS/battery, fire, severe weather, heat illness.
- **Roles and contacts** — emergency coordinator, competent/qualified persons, utility and fire contacts.
- **Training and drills** — workers must be trained on the plan; drills verify it works.

## Site-Specific Rescue

Generic first aid is not enough where the victim cannot simply walk out.

### Rooftop Rescue

- Plan how to reach and lower an injured or suspended worker before work begins.
- Coordinate with the fire service for technical/high-angle rescue where needed.
- Address rapid retrieval of a worker in suspension trauma as a time-critical task.

### Confined-Space Rescue

Some solar tasks (vaults, certain enclosures, deep excavations acting as confined spaces) fall under **OSHA confined-space** rules (29 CFR 1926 Subpart AA for construction; 1910.146 for general industry).

- **Do not enter to rescue without training, equipment, and atmospheric monitoring.** Would-be rescuers are frequently the fatalities in confined-space incidents.
- Provide **non-entry (retrieval) rescue** where feasible — harness, retrieval line, and mechanical retrieval device.
- Arrange and confirm the **rescue service** capability before permit-space entry; a plan on paper is not a rescue.

> **Warning:** Confined-space rescue attempts by untrained bystanders are a documented cause of multiple-fatality events. Never enter a vault, pit, or gas-filled enclosure to pull someone out without proper training, monitoring, ventilation, and equipment. Call trained rescuers.

## Incident Reporting and OSHA Recordkeeping

Reporting is both a legal obligation and a safety tool — it drives corrective action.

### OSHA Reporting Timeframes

| Event | Report to OSHA within |
|---|---|
| Work-related **fatality** | **8 hours** |
| Work-related **in-patient hospitalization, amputation, or loss of an eye** | **24 hours** |

Report by phone to the OSHA area office, the 24-hour hotline (**1-800-321-OSHA**), or the online reporting form.

### Recordkeeping (29 CFR 1904)

- Covered employers must record recordable injuries and illnesses on the **OSHA 300 Log**, complete an **OSHA 301** incident report (or equivalent) for each, and post the **OSHA 300A** summary annually (Feb 1–Apr 30).
- **Recordable** cases generally include those involving death, days away from work, restricted duty or job transfer, medical treatment beyond first aid, or loss of consciousness.
- **Internal reporting:** Report all incidents *and* near-misses internally, preserve the scene where safe, investigate root causes, and implement corrective actions.

> **Note:** Preserve evidence after a serious incident (equipment position, PPE, meter settings) where it is safe to do so — it supports both the OSHA investigation and your own root-cause analysis so the event does not repeat.

> **Safety Disclaimer:** This article is a general educational reference, not medical advice, professional rescue instruction, or a substitute for certified training. First-aid, CPR, and AED steps are summarized from general public guidance (e.g., AHA/Red Cross) and current at the time of writing; always follow your current hands-on certification and local EMS protocols, which take precedence. Electrical, battery, confined-space, and fall rescues are hazardous technical operations that must be performed only by trained, equipped personnel — never improvise a rescue that endangers you. OSHA reporting and recordkeeping rules (29 CFR 1904, 1910, 1926) change and have exemptions; confirm current requirements. When lives or compliance are at stake, rely on certified responders, your safety professional, and licensed medical care.
