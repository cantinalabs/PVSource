# Residential Solar Credit: Timeline & History

The federal tax credit for home solar has been the single most important incentive in the history of U.S. residential solar. It was created in 2005, nearly expired several times, was cut and then restored, and finally **ended for most homeowners at the close of 2025**. Understanding that history helps you read older quotes, contracts, and marketing claims accurately — and understand why the market looks the way it does today.

> **Bottom line (dated 2026):** The residential credit under **Internal Revenue Code §25D** — the "Residential Clean Energy Credit" — was **terminated for expenditures made after December 31, 2025** by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025. If your system was **placed in service on or before December 31, 2025**, you may still claim 30% on the appropriate tax return. Systems placed in service in 2026 or later do **not** qualify for §25D. This article is educational history, not tax advice — see the disclaimer at the end.

For the mechanics of how the credit worked while it existed, see [Residential Federal Solar Tax Credit (§25D)](resinc-federal.md). For the paths that remain after the sunset, see [Third-Party Ownership: Leases vs PPAs](resinc-tpo.md).

## Two different credits, one confusing name

Homeowners often conflate two things:

- **§25D — the residential credit.** Claimed by the **homeowner who buys and owns** the system. This is the "30% off your solar" credit most people mean. It is the credit that ended after 2025.
- **§48 / §48E — the commercial (business) ITC.** Claimed by a **business that owns** the system, including a company that leases a system to you or sells you power under a PPA. This is a separate credit on a separate timeline. It did **not** end on the same date — see [Third-Party Ownership](resinc-tpo.md).

Much of the "solar is still 30% in 2026" messaging you may see refers to the **commercial** credit reaching homeowners through leases and PPAs, not to §25D.

## Timeline at a glance

| Year(s) | Credit rate for homeowner-owned systems | What drove it |
|---|---|---|
| Before 2006 | No dedicated federal residential solar credit | — |
| 2006–2008 | 30%, but **capped at $2,000** for residential | Energy Policy Act of 2005 created the credit; the $2,000 cap limited its value |
| 2009–2019 | **30%**, cap removed | 2008 legislation removed the residential dollar cap |
| 2020 | **26%** | Original scheduled step-down began |
| 2021 | **26%** (not 22%) | Dec. 2020 COVID-relief law delayed the step-down and held the rate at 26% |
| 2022 | **30% (restored)** | Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) restored 30% and made it retroactive for 2022 |
| 2023–2032 | **30%** | IRA schedule (originally through 2032) |
| 2033 | *(was scheduled 26%)* | Never took effect for most — superseded by OBBBA |
| 2034 | *(was scheduled 22%)* | Never took effect — superseded by OBBBA |
| **After Dec. 31, 2025** | **0% — §25D terminated** | **OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025)** |

The exact statutory rates and the placed-in-service rule are set out in [26 U.S. Code §25D](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/25D) and the IRS's [Residential Clean Energy Credit](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/residential-clean-energy-credit) page.

## What each era meant for homeowners

### 2006–2008: a capped start
The credit existed but was worth at most **$2,000** for a residential system. On a typical installation this covered only a small fraction of cost, so adoption stayed modest.

### 2009–2019: the uncapped 30% era
Removing the dollar cap turned the credit into a true **30% of system cost** benefit. Combined with falling panel prices, this decade is when residential solar scaled nationally.

### 2020–2021: the delayed step-down
The credit was scheduled to step down to **26% in 2020** and **22% in 2021**. In December 2020, Congress — as part of COVID-relief legislation — **held the rate at 26% for both 2020 and 2021** instead of letting it fall to 22%. So a 2021 install earned 26%, not 22%.

### 2022: restoration to 30%
The **Inflation Reduction Act of 2022** restored the credit to **30%** and applied it retroactively to systems placed in service during 2022. It also **added standalone battery storage** (batteries not charged by solar) as qualifying property beginning in 2023, and set a long runway originally reaching to 2034.

### 2023–2025: the 30% + storage window
For three years the credit sat at a flat 30% and clearly covered **home battery storage** of at least 3 kWh, whether or not paired with solar. This is the era most current homeowners installed under.

### After 2025: the OBBBA sunset
OBBBA **terminated §25D for expenditures made after December 31, 2025**. The IRS applies a **"placed in service"** test: the expenditure is treated as made when the **original installation is completed**. Signing a contract or paying a deposit in 2025 is not enough on its own — the system generally needed to be installed and operational by the deadline. Grant Thornton and other firms summarized the change shortly after enactment ([Grant Thornton overview](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/alerts/tax/2025/insights/energy-incentives-under-obbba-what-you-need-to-know)).

## Carryforward of prior-year credits

The residential credit was **nonrefundable**: it could reduce your federal tax liability to zero but did not pay out cash beyond that. If your credit exceeded your tax for the year, §25D allowed the **unused portion to carry forward** to future tax years.

> **Why this still matters in 2026 and beyond:** If you placed a qualifying system in service by the end of 2025 but could not use the full 30% credit that year, the **carryforward of the unused amount to later years generally survives the termination.** The Congressional Research Service discusses these expiration-and-carryforward mechanics in [Expiration and Carryforward Rules for the Residential Clean Energy Credit](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12611).

Whether and how much you can carry forward depends on your individual tax situation. Confirm with a tax professional before relying on a carryforward.

## Reading older sources correctly

Because the rate changed so many times, dates matter when you read anything about "the solar tax credit":

- A pre-2022 article may quote **26%** — correct for its era, wrong for 2023–2025.
- A 2023–2025 article quoting a flat **30% through 2032/2034** reflects the **IRA schedule that OBBBA later cut short**.
- Any 2026-and-later claim of "30% federal credit for buying your own system" is **outdated for §25D.** If a company still advertises 30%, ask whether they mean a **lease/PPA passing through the commercial credit** — see [Third-Party Ownership: Leases vs PPAs](resinc-tpo.md).
