# South Carolina Solar Incentives & Policy

South Carolina is one of the stronger Southeastern solar states, anchored by a **25% state income tax credit** for residential solar that — contrary to a widespread misconception — **remains active with no expiration date**, plus a **100% residential property tax exemption**. Under the **Energy Freedom Act (Act 62)**, the state transitioned from retail net metering to utility **Solar Choice** tariffs.

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Incentive / Policy | Residential | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| State income tax credit | **25%**, up to $3,500/yr, 10-yr carryforward (§ 12-6-3587) — active | Not the residential credit; § 12-6-3775 brownfield credit repealed |
| Billing structure | Solar Choice net billing (Act 62); legacy NEM grandfathered | Solar Choice / time-of-use tariffs |
| SREC market | None (no RPS) | None |
| Sales tax exemption | None (general) | Utility-scale via manufacturing exemption |
| Property tax exemption | 100% for ≤20 kW residential (§ 12-37-220) | General assessment above 20 kW (verify) |
| Utility solar rebate | None current | None current |
| Administering agency | SC PSC / ORS / utilities | SC PSC / ORS / utilities |

> **Key correction:** The residential 25% credit under **§ 12-6-3587 has no repeal date**. The credit that expired at the end of 2024 was the *separate* non-residential **§ 12-6-3775** brownfield/EPA-site credit — not the credit homeowners use.

## Overview

South Carolina combines a generous **state income tax credit**, a **residential property tax exemption**, and — following the 2019 **Energy Freedom Act (Act 62)** — a transition from retail net metering to **Solar Choice** tariffs administered through Duke Energy and Dominion Energy South Carolina under the SC Public Service Commission (PSC) and the Office of Regulatory Staff (ORS). There is **no RPS and no SREC market**, and no general solar sales tax exemption.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Act 62 directed the PSC to replace full-retail net metering with **Solar Choice Metering** tariffs. Per the ORS timeline:

- Customers who applied **before May 16, 2019** kept full-retail net metering through **December 31, 2025** (this grandfathering window has now closed).
- Customers who applied **May 16, 2019 – May 31, 2021** keep full-retail net metering through **May 31, 2029** (still grandfathered).
- **June 1, 2021:** Solar Choice interim tariffs began.
- **January 1, 2022:** Solar Choice **permanent tariffs** apply to all new applicants.

**Utility structures:**

- **Duke Energy (Carolinas and Progress):** Time-of-use "Solar Choice" — roughly 1-to-1 credit on surplus but at time-varying rates (peak/off-peak), with a monthly minimum bill and non-bypassable riders.
- **Dominion Energy South Carolina (DESC):** New DER customers are served on a time-of-use rate retaining 1-to-1 credit value, with an annual November true-up (excess paid at a lower avoided-cost rate). DESC's prior net metering framework reportedly expired at the end of 2025, with those customers rolling to Solar Choice in 2026.

> **Verify tariff specifics.** Monthly minimum-bill amounts and non-bypassable charges differ by utility and change over time (some widely cited figures are actually from Duke's North Carolina program). Confirm against the current SC Solar Choice tariff sheets and PSC dockets — verify at DSIRE/official site.

For how net billing and time-of-use crediting affect payback, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

## SREC / RECs

South Carolina has **no Renewable Portfolio Standard and no active SREC market**. RECs exist only under voluntary utility green-power programs; there is no tradeable residential SREC value. See the [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md) for how these markets function in states that have them.

## State Tax Credits

South Carolina offers a **25% state income tax credit** for residential solar under **SC Code § 12-6-3587**:

- **25%** of purchase-and-installation cost.
- Annual credit capped at the lesser of **$3,500 per facility** or **50% of tax liability**.
- Unused credit carries forward **up to 10 years**; nonrefundable.
- Claimed on **Schedule TC-38**. You must **own** the system (leased systems and community solar do not qualify).

> **This credit has no repeal date** (per SC DOR Revenue Ruling #24-2). Only the geothermal portion of § 12-6-3587 is scheduled to sunset (January 2032); solar is unaffected. The credit that expired December 31, 2024 was the **separate** non-residential **§ 12-6-3775** brownfield credit, which does not apply to homeowners installing panels at their residence.

## Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are separate from state policy:

- **Residential (§25D):** The 30% federal residential clean energy credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally do **not** qualify for the federal residential credit — but South Carolina's **state** 25% credit remains available.
- **Commercial (ITC / §48E):** Business-owned solar may still qualify for the commercial investment tax credit and the technology-neutral **§48E** credit, subject to current rules.

See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

There are **no current utility solar rebates in South Carolina.** Neither Duke Energy SC nor Dominion Energy SC offers an active residential solar rebate.

> The often-cited **$0.60/watt** residential rebate and the **PowerPair** solar-plus-battery incentive are **North Carolina** programs, not South Carolina. Do not treat them as SC incentives.

## Sales Tax Exemption

**South Carolina offers no general residential solar sales tax exemption.** Standard state sales tax (plus local option) applies to residential solar equipment — in fact, SC DOR guidance treats sales tax paid as part of the cost basis for the 25% income tax credit. The only solar-related sales/use tax relief applies to large **utility-scale** generating facilities treated as manufacturers, which does not help homeowners.

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Under **SC Code § 12-37-220**, residential-use renewable energy property with a nameplate capacity of **≤20 kW (AC)** is **100% exempt** from property tax — the added home value is not assessed.

> **Commercial/larger systems:** Systems above the 20 kW residential threshold do not get this blanket exemption; commercial and utility-scale solar is generally handled under standard property-assessment rules. The exact treatment above 20 kW is nuanced — verify against the current § 12-37-220 text.

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Interconnection follows the **South Carolina Generator Interconnection Procedures (SCGIP)** approved by the PSC, applying to Dominion, Duke, and Lockhart Power. Small residential systems (generally ≤20 kW) get expedited/fast-track review; larger systems require an engineering study. The utility must issue **Permission to Operate** before energizing.
- Installations must meet the National Electrical Code as adopted by the SC Building Codes Council; local governments issue building/electrical permits.
- Note: SC DOR accepts the utility interconnection agreement as certification that a grid-tied system qualifies for the state income tax credit.

## Bottom Line

South Carolina remains attractive despite the end of full-retail net metering. The centerpiece is the **25% state income tax credit** (up to $3,500/year, active with no sunset), reinforced by a **100% residential property tax exemption** for systems ≤20 kW. New customers are on **Solar Choice** time-of-use tariffs, and there is **no SREC market, no general sales tax exemption, and no utility rebate**. Critically for 2026: even though the federal residential §25D credit ended after 2025, South Carolina's **state** 25% credit continues — a meaningful differentiator. Businesses can also pursue the commercial ITC/§48E. Confirm current tariff terms and credit rules before proceeding.

Verify all figures with the SC PSC, the Office of Regulatory Staff, the SC Department of Revenue, your utility, and a qualified professional before making decisions.
