# Residential Storage Incentives

Home batteries have their own incentive landscape, largely separate from solar panels. Some incentives pay you **upfront** to install storage; others pay you **over time** to let the utility use your battery during grid stress (a "virtual power plant," or **VPP**). This article maps the major residential storage programs by state and explains how the now-ended federal credit treated batteries.

> **Federal note (dated 2026):** The federal **Residential Clean Energy Credit (§25D)** covered qualified home battery storage of at least **3 kWh** — including standalone batteries not charged by solar, starting in 2023. Like the solar portion, §25D **ended for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025** under the OBBBA. Storage installed in 2026 or later does **not** qualify for §25D. See [Residential Solar Credit: Timeline & History](resinc-timeline.md).

Because the federal credit is gone for new installs, **state and utility storage programs are now the primary incentives** for home batteries. For the physical backup capability these programs sit on top of, see [Residential Battery & Whole-Home Backup](residential-battery-backup.md).

## Two kinds of storage incentive

1. **Upfront rebates** — a one-time payment (often per kWh of capacity or per kW of power) that lowers install cost. Example: California SGIP.
2. **Performance / VPP payments** — recurring payments for enrolling your battery so the utility can dispatch it during peak events. Example: ConnectedSolutions. These are often paid **per kW of demonstrated performance** during a season.

Some states offer both; some batteries can stack an upfront rebate with ongoing VPP income. Program terms change frequently — **verify current amounts and open/closed status before you buy.**

|---|---|---|---|---|

| Connecticut | **Energy Storage Solutions (ESS)** | Upfront + performance | As of **April 1, 2026**: reduced upfront (~**$30/kWh** standard, up to ~**$130/kWh** on grid-constrained circuits) plus annual performance payments (~**$300–$550/kW**, years 1–5) | Program was restructured to a performance-based model in 2026 |
| Hawaii | **Battery Bonus** → **BYOD Plus** | Upfront + export | **Battery Bonus closed to new participants July 1, 2024.** Successor **BYOD Plus** reported at ~**$400/kW** committed (higher, ~**$800/kW**, for low/moderate income) | Do not quote the old Battery Bonus for new installs |
| Vermont | **GMP Bring Your Own Device (BYOD)** | Upfront + VPP | Reported up to ~**$950 per kW** of usable power for enrolling an eligible battery; GMP also offers a leased-battery option | GMP dispatches battery during peaks |
| Nevada | **NV Energy** residential storage | Upfront (status varies) | Historically a per-watt-hour credit for TOU customers (~**$0.19/Wh = $190/kWh**); enrollment status has been intermittently paused | Confirm the program is open before relying on it |

> **All amounts above are representative and change often.** Programs open, close, refund, and restructure — the Hawaii Battery Bonus closure and the April 2026 Connecticut restructuring are both examples. Always confirm the **current** rate and whether enrollment is open with the program administrator or your installer.

## How the ended federal credit treated storage

While §25D was in effect through 2025:

- Batteries with capacity of **3 kWh or more** qualified for the **30%** credit.
- From **2023**, **standalone** storage qualified even if **not charged by solar** — a change from earlier years when batteries generally had to be solar-charged.
- The credit was **nonrefundable** with a **carryforward** of unused amounts (see [Residential Solar Credit: Timeline & History](resinc-timeline.md)).

For any storage **placed in service after December 31, 2025**, this federal credit is **no longer available.** State/utility programs and financing are the remaining levers.

## VPP enrollment income — how to think about it

VPP programs (ConnectedSolutions, GMP BYOD, Hawaii export programs, and similar) pay you to share stored energy during grid peaks. Points to weigh:

- **How it's paid.** Performance programs usually pay **per kW of measured performance** during peak events over a season, not per kWh of capacity you own.
- **How much control you give up.** The utility may discharge your battery during events; understand any **reserve** you can keep for your own backup.
- **Rate lock.** Programs like ConnectedSolutions commonly **lock your per-kW rate for the first several summers** of enrollment.
- **Stacking.** In some states you can combine an **upfront rebate** with **ongoing VPP income** — confirm eligibility rules.
- **Taxability.** VPP payments may be **taxable income** depending on your situation. This is not tax advice — ask a tax professional.
- **Who keeps the money under a lease/PPA.** If your system is third-party-owned, the **provider** may capture VPP payments, not you. See [Third-Party Ownership: Leases vs PPAs](resinc-tpo.md).

## Stacking storage incentives with everything else

- **Utility rate design** (time-of-use, net billing) often determines whether a battery pays off at all — see [Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md).
- **Financing** for storage works much like solar financing — see [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md).
- If you also drive electric, a battery can shift **EV charging** into cheaper hours — see [EV Charging + Solar at Home](residential-ev-charging.md) and [EV Charging + Solar Incentives at Home](resinc-ev.md).

## Quick checklist before buying storage

1. Confirm your state/utility program is **open** and get the **current** rate in writing.
2. Check whether you can **stack** an upfront rebate with a VPP program.
3. Understand how much **battery reserve** you keep for backup vs. what the utility can dispatch.
4. Ask whether payments are **taxable** in your case.
5. If leasing/PPA, confirm **who receives** any VPP income.
6. Size the battery for your actual **backup and rate-arbitrage** goals, not just the incentive.
