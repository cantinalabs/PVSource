# Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection

A residential solar system is a 25-plus-year investment bolted to your roof. Protecting it means understanding three things: how your **homeowners insurance** treats it, what the layered **warranties** actually cover, and the **consumer-protection** basics that keep you from being trapped by a bad contract. This page covers all three, with a warranty comparison table and documentation checklist.

For how financing choices interact with these protections, see [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md); for resale implications, see [Selling or Buying a Home with Solar](residential-selling-home.md).

## Homeowners insurance and solar

### Are the panels covered?

For an **owned, roof-mounted** system, panels are usually treated as **part of the dwelling** — the same coverage that protects your roof and walls. That means covered perils (fire, wind, hail, falling objects, etc., subject to your policy) generally extend to the panels. Key nuances:

- **Ground-mounted systems** often fall under **"other structures" (Coverage B)**, which is typically capped at **about 10% of your dwelling limit**. A $300,000 dwelling limit implies roughly $30,000 for ground-mount — potentially not enough for a large array. Ask for a higher limit or endorsement.
- **Battery storage** may need to be specifically listed; confirm it is covered for both damage and, ideally, liability.

### Raise your dwelling coverage limit

Adding a system increases your home's **replacement cost**. If you do not raise your **dwelling coverage (Coverage A)** limit to include the system, you could be **underinsured** after a total loss. Notify your insurer when you install and confirm the new limit reflects the system's replacement cost.

### Premiums, wind, and hail

- Adding solar often **raises your premium modestly** (higher insured value), though amounts vary widely.
- In **hail- and wind-prone regions**, some insurers in 2025 have begun **excluding or limiting wind/hail coverage** for solar, or requiring a **separate endorsement** and higher deductible. If you live in such an area, confirm in writing that panels are covered for wind/hail, and check the **percentage deductible** (often applied to the whole dwelling value, which can be large).

> Before installing, call your insurer. Ask: (1) Will the panels be covered under my dwelling? (2) Do I need to raise my Coverage A limit? (3) Is there any wind/hail exclusion or separate deductible? (4) Do you need to inspect or approve the installer?

### Leased / PPA systems

If a third party **owns** the system (lease or PPA), **their** insurance typically covers the equipment itself — but gaps can appear:

- **Roof damage** caused by the mounting, or **water intrusion**, may fall into a gray zone between your policy and theirs.
- Confirm **liability** coverage (who is responsible if the system injures someone or damages a neighbor's property).
- Read the lease/PPA to see what the provider insures and what remains your responsibility. Ask your own insurer whether the third-party equipment affects your policy.

## The warranties involved

A residential solar installation is covered by **several separate warranties** from **different parties** (module maker, inverter maker, installer). Knowing who backs each — and for how long — is essential, because a company that goes out of business cannot honor a warranty.

### Module warranties (two kinds)

- **Product (materials/workmanship) warranty** — covers manufacturing defects in the panel. Typically **12–25 years** (many premium modules now 25 years).
- **Performance (power output) warranty** — guarantees the panel still produces at least a stated percentage of rated output over time (e.g., ~87%–92% at year 25, reflecting normal **degradation**). Typically **25–30 years**.

### Inverter warranty

- **String inverters:** often **10–12 years**, sometimes extendable.
- **Microinverters / power optimizers:** often **25 years**, matching module life.

### Workmanship / labor warranty (installer)

Covers the quality of the **installation** — wiring, mounting, and labor to fix defects. Typically **5–25 years** depending on the installer. This is the warranty most dependent on the **installer still being in business**.

### Roof penetration / weatherproofing warranty

Covers **leaks** where mounts penetrate the roof. Sometimes bundled into workmanship, sometimes separate. Confirm the term and whether it covers **water damage** to the home interior, not just re-sealing.

### Battery warranty

Batteries carry their own warranty, usually **10 years** and/or a **throughput/cycle** guarantee (e.g., a minimum retained capacity like ~70% after 10 years or a stated total energy throughput). See [Residential Battery & Whole-Home Backup](residential-battery-backup.md).

### Warranty comparison table

| Component | Warranty type | Typical term | What it covers | Backed by |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Solar module | Product/materials | 12–25 yrs | Manufacturing defects | Module manufacturer |
| Solar module | Performance/output | 25–30 yrs | Guaranteed min. output vs. degradation | Module manufacturer |
| String inverter | Product | 10–12 yrs | Defects/failure | Inverter manufacturer |
| Microinverter/optimizer | Product | ~25 yrs | Defects/failure | Manufacturer |
| Installation | Workmanship/labor | 5–25 yrs | Install quality, labor to repair | Installer |
| Roof penetrations | Weatherproofing | 5–25 yrs | Leaks at mounts | Installer (sometimes roofer) |
| Battery | Product + capacity/throughput | ~10 yrs | Defects and min. retained capacity | Battery manufacturer |

> **Labor is the catch.** A 25-year panel warranty may cover the **part** but not the **labor** to remove and reinstall it. Ask whether labor and shipping are included, and for how long.

## Monitoring and production guarantees

- **Monitoring** — nearly all modern systems include an app/portal showing production. Use it: catching an underperforming string early is how you actually enforce a performance warranty.
- **Production guarantee** — some installers (especially with leases/PPAs) promise a minimum annual kWh and **pay you** if the system underproduces. Read the fine print: guarantees often exclude weather variance, exclude the first year, or require you to file within a window.

## Consumer-protection basics

Solar sales have attracted high-pressure and deceptive tactics. Protect yourself:

### Verify before you sign

- **Check the installer's license** with your state contractor licensing board and confirm they are bonded/insured.
- **Check reviews and complaints** (BBB, state consumer-protection office, online reviews).
- **Confirm the manufacturers** of your panels/inverter/battery are established (warranties are only as good as the company behind them).

### Read the contract

- Ensure **all promises are in writing** — production estimates, price, warranty terms, escalators (for leases/PPAs), and who is responsible for permits and interconnection.
- Understand the **total price** (cash vs. financed) and any **dealer fees** — see [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md).
- Know your **right of rescission / cooling-off period** (many states give 3 days to cancel a home-solicitation sale).

### Avoid the classic scams

> Red flags: "free solar," "government is paying for it," urgency/"today only" pricing, requests for large upfront deposits before permits, blank fields in the contract, or refusal to provide a written cash price. Walk away and get another quote.

### Disclosures

Reputable installers provide a **solar disclosure document** (some states mandate one) summarizing price, financing, production estimate, and consumer rights in plain language. If you do not get one, ask.

## What to keep and document

Store these in one folder (digital and paper) for the life of the system — you will need them for warranty claims, insurance, and resale:

- Signed **installation contract** and any **financing/lease/PPA agreement**
- **Manufacturer warranties** for panels, inverter, and battery
- **Workmanship/roof-penetration warranty** from the installer
- **Permits, inspection sign-offs, and the interconnection agreement** with the utility
- **System design/site plan**, equipment model and serial numbers
- **Monitoring login** credentials and portal link
- Records of any **insurance endorsement** or dwelling-limit change
- Any **production guarantee** terms and claim procedures
