# Working at Heights & Fall Protection

Falls are the single deadliest hazard in solar work. Most PV installation happens on roofs, and roof edges, skylights, ladders, and fragile surfaces put workers at height for hours at a time. OSHA fall-protection violations are perennially the most-cited construction standard, and in solar they are the leading cause of fatalities. This article covers the fall hazards specific to solar, OSHA's requirements, the components of a fall-arrest system, and the rescue planning that too many crews forget.

> **A harness that stops your fall can still kill you.** A worker left hanging in a harness after arrest can suffer **suspension trauma** (orthostatic intolerance) in minutes as blood pools in the legs. Fall protection without a **rehearsed rescue plan** is only half a system.

## Rooftop Fall Hazards in Solar

Solar crews face fall hazards that ordinary roofers may not, because they linger near edges, carry awkward loads, and route cables across the whole roof plane.

- **Roof edges and eaves** — the primary fall path; workers back toward edges while positioning modules.
- **Low-slope (flat) commercial roofs** — deceptively safe-feeling; a large open field with edges and roof openings on every side.
- **Steep-slope residential roofs** — footing and load-carrying hazards compound the edge hazard.
- **Skylights and roof openings** — often unmarked, sometimes painted over, and frequently mistaken for solid surface.
- **Fragile surfaces** — aged metal, fiber-cement, and some membrane roofs will not bear a worker's weight.
- **Ladders** — the transition point on and off the roof; a leading source of both falls and struck-by injuries.
- **Weather** — wind lifts modules like sails; wet or frosty surfaces destroy footing; heat drives fatigue and mistakes.

## OSHA Fall-Protection Thresholds

The applicable threshold depends on whether the work is construction or general industry — a distinction covered in [Solar Safety Overview](safe-overview.md).

| Work type | Standard | Fall-protection required at |
|---|---|---|
| **Construction** (installing a new array, re-roof) | 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M | **6 feet** to a lower level |
| **General industry** (routine O&M on an existing system) | 29 CFR 1910 Subpart D | generally **4 feet** to a lower level |

Under construction rules, each employee on a surface with an unprotected side or edge **6 feet (1.8 m) or more** above a lower level must be protected by a **guardrail system, safety net system, or personal fall arrest system** — OSHA [29 CFR 1926.501](https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1926/1926.501). Residential construction activities carry the same 6-foot trigger unless a specific alternative in the standard applies.

> **The 6 ft vs. 4 ft trap.** A crew that installs under construction rules (6 ft) and returns months later to service the system under general-industry rules may face the lower **4 ft** trigger. Know which hat you are wearing before you step onto a roof.

## Personal Fall Arrest Systems (PFAS)

A **Personal Fall Arrest System** catches a worker who has already fallen. Its three components must all be present and compatible — the system is only as good as its weakest link.

| Component | Function | Key requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchorage** | The fixed point the system attaches to | Capable of supporting **5,000 lb** per worker, or designed by a qualified person with a **2:1** safety factor |
| **Body support** | Distributes arrest forces | **Full-body harness only** (body belts are prohibited for fall arrest) |
| **Connector** | Links harness to anchor | Lanyard with shock absorber, or self-retracting lifeline (SRL); locking snaphooks/carabiners |

Criteria from OSHA [29 CFR 1926.502](https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1926/1926.502) that installers must respect:

- Arrest forces on the body must not exceed **1,800 lb** with a full-body harness.
- The system must **limit free fall to 6 feet** and prevent the worker from contacting a lower level.
- Account for **total fall clearance**: free-fall distance + deceleration distance + harness stretch + worker height + safety margin. On a low structure, the worker can hit the ground before the system arrests — the reason SRLs (which limit fall distance) are often preferred at lower heights.
- Inspect all components before each use; remove from service any equipment that has arrested a fall.

## Guardrails, Warning Lines, and Other Systems

PFAS is not the only option, and often not the best. Higher on the hierarchy of controls (see [Solar Safety Overview](safe-overview.md)) are passive systems that do not depend on a worker clipping in.

- **Guardrail systems** — a passive engineering control. Top rail at roughly **42 inches (±3 in)**, able to withstand a **200 lb** force; midrails and toeboards as required. Preferred where feasible because they protect everyone in the area continuously.
- **Warning-line systems** — used on low-slope roofs to demarcate a zone where work may proceed without conventional fall protection, combined with a safety monitor or other measures. For roofing operations, the warning line is generally erected **not less than 6 feet from the roof edge** when mechanical equipment is not in use (greater distances apply along the direction of equipment operation).
- **Safety net systems** — installed below the work surface to catch a falling worker; more common on large commercial and utility structures.
- **Controlled access zones / safety monitor** — administrative controls used in limited residential-construction scenarios; the least protective option and subject to strict conditions.

## Ladders

Ladders are where most roof access goes right or wrong.

- Set an extension ladder at roughly a **4:1 pitch** (1 foot out for every 4 feet of height).
- Extend the ladder **at least 3 feet above** the landing surface and secure/tie it off at the top.
- Maintain **three points of contact** when climbing; do not carry loads that prevent it — hoist materials separately.
- Inspect for damage; keep the area at the base clear; never place a ladder in front of an unlocked door or a driveway without controls.

## Skylights and Fragile Surfaces

Skylights are a notorious solar killer because they look like part of the roof.

> **Treat every skylight and roof opening as a hole in the roof.** OSHA requires holes and skylights to be guarded. Cover them with a labeled, load-rated cover, install a guardrail/screen around them, or protect exposed workers with PFAS. Never assume a skylight will bear weight — many are only glazing.

The same caution applies to **fragile roof surfaces**: aged translucent panels, corroded metal, and some membrane assemblies. Verify load-bearing capacity before stepping, use walkway boards or crawling boards where needed, and stay on structural members.

## Rescue Planning

OSHA requires employers to provide **prompt rescue** of employees in the event of a fall, or to ensure workers can rescue themselves. A rescue plan is not optional and cannot be "call 911 and hope."

A workable rescue plan addresses:

- **Suspension-trauma awareness** — a suspended worker can deteriorate within minutes; act fast.
- **Retrieval method** — pre-positioned rescue devices, controlled-descent equipment, aerial lift, or ladder rescue as appropriate to the site.
- **Trained rescuers on site** — not merely PFAS users, but people trained and equipped to perform the rescue.
- **Suspension-trauma relief straps** on harnesses so a suspended worker can stand in the harness and relieve leg pressure while awaiting rescue.
- **Communication** — how the alarm is raised, who responds, and how emergency services are summoned for remote utility sites where response is slow.

Rehearse the plan. A rescue improvised for the first time during a real fall is the plan most likely to fail.

## A Roof-Access Fall-Protection Checklist

```
Before roof access:
[ ] Fall-protection method selected (guardrail > warning line > PFAS)
[ ] Anchorage identified and rated (5,000 lb or engineered)
[ ] Harness + connector inspected, compatible, correct fit
[ ] Fall clearance calculated for the actual structure height
[ ] Skylights / openings covered or guarded
[ ] Fragile surfaces identified and marked
[ ] Ladder set 4:1, extended 3 ft, tied off
[ ] Weather checked (wind, wet, ice, heat)
[ ] Rescue plan briefed; rescuers and equipment on site
[ ] Suspension-trauma relief straps present on harnesses
```

> **Safety disclaimer.** This article is educational only and is not a substitute for hands-on fall-protection training by a competent person or a site-specific fall-protection plan. Falls from height cause severe injury and death. Fall-protection systems must be selected, installed, inspected, and used in accordance with current OSHA standards (29 CFR 1926 Subpart M for construction; 29 CFR 1910 Subpart D for general industry) and manufacturer instructions, and every job requires a rehearsed rescue plan. Do not work at height without proper training, equipment, and authorization. When in doubt, stop work and consult a competent fall-protection professional.
