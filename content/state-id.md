# Idaho Solar Incentives & Policy

Idaho has **no statewide net-metering law** — compensation is set **utility by utility** — but it offers a genuinely useful **state income-tax deduction** for residential alternative-energy systems. There is **no statewide sales-tax or property-tax exemption** for solar. This guide covers both **residential** and **commercial** solar where the rules diverge.

> **Educational use only.** Program terms, rates, and statutes change frequently. Every figure below should be re-verified against the official Idaho agency pages, your utility's tariff, and [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) before you rely on it. Today's date for this guide is **2026-06-30**.

## Overview

Idaho's solar policy is defined more by what it lacks (a statewide net-metering mandate, RPS, and tax exemptions) than by rich incentives. The two things that do help:

- A **residential alternative-energy income-tax deduction** worth up to **100% of cost** spread over four years (subject to annual and lifetime caps).
- **Utility net-billing / net-metering tariffs** — the compensation value depends heavily on **which utility serves you**.

The major regulated utilities are **Idaho Power**, **Rocky Mountain Power (PacifiCorp)**, and **Avista**, each with its own on-site-generation schedule approved by the **Idaho Public Utilities Commission (IPUC)**.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Idaho has **no statewide net-metering statute.** Compensation is set in each utility's IPUC-approved tariff, and several utilities have moved from retail net metering toward **net billing** (crediting exports at lower, avoided-cost-type rates). See [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md) for the mechanics.

> **Verify at the IPUC and your utility — these tariffs are actively changing.**

| Utility | Reported structure (verify) | Grandfathering / notes |
|---------|-----------------------------|------------------------|
| **Idaho Power** | Transitioned to **net billing** (Jan 2024); exports valued near **avoided cost**, not retail | Systems in service by **Dec 20, 2019** reportedly grandfathered on net metering until **Dec 20, 2045** |
| **Rocky Mountain Power** | Net billing since ~Nov 2020; small residential systems reportedly still near retail, but **Schedule 136 proposals (2025)** would cut export credit toward **~$0.04/kWh** and limit rollover — **pending IPUC** | Confirm current approved schedule |
| **Avista** | Net metering reported for systems up to **~100 kW** | Verify current schedule |

> **Because Idaho compensation is utility-specific and mid-transition, do not model at retail rate without confirming the exact export credit and any pending IPUC rate case for your utility.**

**Commercial note:** System-size ceilings, demand-charge interactions, and export rates for commercial customers differ from residential schedules. Pull the specific commercial/large-general-service on-site-generation tariff for your utility.

## SREC / RECs

Idaho has **no Renewable Portfolio Standard** and **no retail SREC market.** Systems may technically generate RECs, but there is no state-mandated buyer, so **do not count on SREC revenue.** For how tradable-certificate markets work elsewhere, see [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

## State Tax Credits

Idaho's headline incentive is a **residential income-tax deduction** (a deduction, not a credit) for alternative-energy devices, including solar.

> **Idaho Residential Alternative Energy Deduction (verify current caps):**
> - Deduct **40% of the system cost in the year of installation**, then **20% per year for the next three years** — up to **100% of cost total** over four years.
> - **Annual cap** commonly reported at **$5,000/year**; **total cap** commonly reported at **$20,000**.
> - Applies to owner-occupied **residences**; confirm eligibility, forms, and current caps with the **Idaho State Tax Commission**.

There is **no comparable state solar credit for commercial** taxpayers — commercial value comes from federal incentives and depreciation (below).

## Federal Tax Credits

- **Residential (§25D):** The residential clean-energy credit **ended for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025** under the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**. **2026 residential buyers receive no federal §25D credit** — but the Idaho state deduction above still applies.
- **Commercial (ITC §48 / tech-neutral §48E):** Businesses may still claim the **commercial ITC** for qualifying projects, subject to current begin-construction / placed-in-service and phase-out rules, with potential bonus adders and depreciation.

See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

- No standing statewide upfront PV rebate.
- Utilities occasionally run **demand-response, EV, or storage** programs; check **Idaho Power**, **Rocky Mountain Power**, and **Avista** program pages for current offerings.
- The **Idaho Office of Energy and Mineral Resources (OEMR)** maintains a list of financing/incentive resources — verify current programs.

## Sales Tax Exemption

**Idaho has no statewide sales-tax exemption for solar equipment.** Idaho's general sales tax applies to PV equipment purchases for both residential and commercial buyers. Budget accordingly and **verify current treatment** with the Idaho State Tax Commission.

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

**Idaho has no statewide property-tax exemption** specifically for residential solar. Assessment practices can vary locally; **verify with your county assessor** whether and how a PV system affects assessed value. (Certain **commercial/utility-scale** generation may fall under separate state property-tax provisions — verify at DSIRE/official site for large projects.)

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- **Permitting:** Local building/electrical permits via city or county; Idaho follows adopted building/electrical codes. Confirm AHJ requirements.
- **Interconnection:** No single statewide standard — each utility files interconnection procedures with the **IPUC**. Small residential systems generally use a simplified application; larger systems trigger studies.
- **Timing:** Because export rates hinge on which tariff you interconnect under (and grandfathering dates), confirm the **effective schedule** at the time your system energizes.

## Bottom Line

- **Residential:** The **state alternative-energy deduction (up to 100% over 4 years)** is the standout benefit. Net-billing (not retail net metering) is the norm at the major utilities and is **actively being reduced** — model exports conservatively. No sales/property exemption; no federal §25D for 2026 buyers.
- **Commercial:** No state credit or exemptions; value is the **federal commercial ITC/§48E + depreciation**. Export compensation depends on your utility's commercial on-site-generation tariff — verify with the IPUC.
