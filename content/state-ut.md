# Utah Solar Incentives & Policy

Utah has moved decisively away from retail-rate net metering toward **net billing with a low fixed export credit**, and its once-popular **residential state solar tax credit has fully phased out**. Commercial projects still have a state credit, and Rocky Mountain Power runs an active battery incentive. This guide summarizes the current landscape for both residential and commercial solar as of mid-2026. Programs change frequently — confirm live figures with the administering agency and the **Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency ([DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/))** before modeling a project.

For background on how export compensation works, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md). For the federal side, see [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md). For neighboring-state comparisons, see [More State Programs](incentive-more-states.md) and the [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Policy Lever | Residential | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| **Export compensation** | **Net billing** (Sched. 137) — fixed low export credit | Same; large C&I on separate schedules |
| **SREC / REC market** | None (voluntary RPS; no carve-out) | None tradable at retail |
| **State income-tax credit** | **$0 — expired** for installs after 12/31/2023 | **10% up to $50,000** (RESTC), expires after 1/1/2028 |
| **Utility rebate** | RMP **Wattsmart Battery** ($1,000–$2,000) | Wattsmart Battery ($200/kW) |
| **Sales-tax exemption** | Generally **taxable** (exemption is production-facility oriented) *(verify)* | Alternative-energy exemption (sunset 6/30/2027) |
| **Property tax** | No confirmed statewide residential exemption *(verify with county)* | N/A rooftop |

> **Framing:** The residential Utah solar tax credit is **gone** (it hit $0 for installs after 2023 — do not rely on the old "$400" figure). And Utah's export credit is a **fixed net-billing rate well below retail**, reset every March 1. Homeowner economics depend heavily on self-consumption.

## Overview

Utah's dominant investor-owned utility is **Rocky Mountain Power (RMP)**. State policy is set by the **Utah Public Service Commission (PSC)**, with tax-credit programs administered by the **Utah Office of Energy Development (OED)** and the **Utah State Tax Commission**. Utah's headline change is the shift from net metering to **net billing** with a low, annually reset export credit — and the full expiration of the residential state solar tax credit.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Utah transitioned from retail-rate net metering to **net billing** under RMP. Three grandfathered tiers exist:

| Program | Applies to (interconnection filed) | Export value | Expires |
|---|---|---|---|
| Schedule 135 (Net Metering) | before Nov 15, 2017 | full retail rate | Jan 1, 2036 |
| Schedule 136 (Transition) | Nov 15, 2017 – Oct 30, 2020 | ~90–92.5% of retail | Jan 1, 2033 |
| **Schedule 137 (Net Billing)** | **after Oct 30, 2020 (all new customers)** | **fixed export credit, reset each Mar 1** | no expiration |

**Current Schedule 137 export credit rate (effective March 1, 2026):**

- **June–September: 4.855¢/kWh**
- **October–May: 4.033¢/kWh**

For context, residential retail is roughly **10.2¢/kWh**, so the export credit is well below retail (~40–48%). The rate is **recalculated every March 1**.

> **Accuracy note:** Some third-party sites still cite the prior year's rates (~5.6¢ summer / 4.7¢ winter). Those are **stale**. Use the figures above from the official RMP Schedule 137 tariff, and **verify** the current-year rate before modeling. See [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

## SREC / RECs

**Utah has no viable SREC market.** Its **Renewable Portfolio Goal** is **voluntary** — 20% of adjusted retail sales by 2025, with a cost-effectiveness limitation and no interim mandates — so it creates little compliance demand. There is **no solar carve-out**. RECs are tracked via **WREGIS**, but there is no meaningful SREC revenue stream for Utah homeowners. Contrast with the compliance markets in our [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

## State Tax Credits

**Residential — expired.** The residential solar PV credit under the **Renewable Energy Systems Tax Credit (RESTC)** was 25% of cost up to a cap that stepped down $400 per year:

| Install year | Max residential solar PV credit |
|---|---|
| 2018–2020 | $1,600 |
| 2021 | $1,200 |
| 2022 | $800 |
| 2023 | $400 |
| **2024 and later** | **$0 — no credit** |

The OED confirms residential solar PV installed **in 2024 and beyond is not eligible**. Home batteries are also ineligible. **There is no Utah residential solar income-tax credit in 2026** — the widely repeated "$400" figure was the 2023-only value and is now stale.

**Commercial — still active.** The commercial RESTC remains available: a **refundable credit of 10% of eligible system cost or $50,000, whichever is less**, for solar and other renewables. A **$15 OED application fee** applies. Per 2025 legislation (HB 264), **all RESTC expires for systems placed in service after January 1, 2028** — a timing deadline for commercial projects. *Verify current terms at OED.*

## Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are national, not Utah-specific, but they drive project economics:

- **Residential (§25D):** The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally **cannot claim §25D**. Confirm with a tax professional.
- **Commercial (ITC / §48E):** The technology-neutral **Clean Electricity Investment Credit (§48E)** generally remains available for commercial and utility-scale projects, subject to phase-out schedules, wage/apprenticeship rules, and bonus adders. See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

- **RMP Wattsmart Battery Program (updated June 15, 2026):** An active dispatchable-battery incentive.
  - **Residential single battery <10 kW:** up to **$1,000** per household.
  - **Residential ≥10 kW:** cap **$2,000** per household.
  - **Commercial:** **$200/kW** (systems >30 kW require pre-approval / custom offer).
  - Terms changed as of June 15, 2026 — **verify current dollar amounts** on the RMP page.
- **Solar rebates:** RMP does **not** offer a general residential solar-panel cash rebate.

## Sales Tax Exemption

Utah has an **Alternative Energy sales-and-use tax exemption**, but it is oriented toward **alternative-energy production facilities / commercial-scale projects** (leases must be ≥7 years), with a scheduled **sunset of June 30, 2027**.

> For **typical residential rooftop purchases**, treat equipment as **generally taxable** unless it qualifies as an alternative-energy production facility. Consumer guides that broadly claim "Utah exempts solar panels from sales tax" overstate this. **Verify** at the Utah State Tax Commission and DSIRE (program #1070).

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

**No statewide residential property-tax exemption specific to rooftop solar was confirmed.** Utah does not broadly exempt the added home value from rooftop solar. Treatment is handled at the **county assessor** level. **Verify with your local county assessor and at DSIRE.** (Separate treatment applies to utility-scale/production property.)

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- New RMP customers interconnect under **Schedule 137 (Net Billing)**; residential systems are generally capped at **25 kW** (2 MW non-residential).
- **Interconnection review fees (from the tariff):** Level 1 = **$60**; Level 2 = **$75 + $1.50/kW**; Level 3 = **$150 + $3.00/kW**.
- **Systems ≤10 kW (inverter-based)** generally need **no external disconnect switch** and no one-line diagram; larger systems require a lockable manual disconnect near the meter.
- Local **building and electrical permits and inspection** are required in addition to utility interconnection.

## Bottom Line

For **Utah homeowners**, the story has tightened: **no state solar tax credit** (expired after 2023), a **fixed net-billing export credit well below retail** (~4.0–4.9¢/kWh, reset each March), and **§25D federal credit ended after 2025**. Self-consumption and a possible **Wattsmart Battery** incentive ($1,000–$2,000) now carry more weight. For **commercial projects**, the **10%/$50,000 RESTC remains available through systems placed in service before January 1, 2028**, alongside the federal §48E ITC and depreciation, plus the $200/kW commercial battery incentive. Confirm the **current March-1 export rate** and the **2027/2028 sunset dates** before committing.
