# Florida Solar Incentives & Policy

Florida — the "Sunshine State" — combines an excellent solar resource with two of the most favorable state-level tax provisions in the country: a **100% property-tax exemption** and a **sales-tax exemption** for solar equipment. This guide covers the current landscape for **residential and commercial** systems, with pointers to verify time-sensitive values.

> **Educational reference only — not tax or legal advice.** Programs change frequently. Verify every value below with the relevant agency, your utility, and [DSIRE](https://programs.dsireusa.org/) before making decisions.

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Policy / Incentive | Status | Notes |
|--------------------|--------|-------|
| Statewide net metering | Yes | IOUs required to offer retail-rate net metering |
| SREC market / RPS solar carve-out | No SREC market | Florida has **no** mandatory statewide RPS |
| State personal income-tax credit | No | Florida has **no state income tax** |
| State corporate income-tax credit | No | No solar-specific corporate income-tax credit |
| Federal residential credit (§25D) | Ended | Expired after Dec 31, 2025 (OBBBA) |
| Federal commercial ITC (§48/§48E) | Yes | See federal pages; phase-out schedule applies |
| Utility rebates / storage incentives | Limited | Mostly community-solar subscription programs |
| Sales-tax exemption | Yes | Solar equipment exempt (FSEC-certified components) |
| Property-tax exemption | Yes — **100%** | Added value of residential & commercial systems exempt |
| Utility structure | Regulated IOUs + munis/co-ops | FPL, Duke FL, TECO, FPUC |

## Overview

Florida enjoys a strong solar resource — roughly **5.0 to 5.5 peak sun-hours per day** statewide, fairly uniform across the peninsula. Summer cloud cover and afternoon thunderstorms temper output somewhat, but year-round sun and rising electricity demand make Florida one of the largest residential solar markets in the U.S.

Utility structure:

- **Investor-owned utilities (IOUs):** **Florida Power & Light (FPL)** (the largest), **Duke Energy Florida**, **Tampa Electric (TECO)**, and **Florida Public Utilities (FPUC)** — all regulated by the **Florida Public Service Commission (PSC)**.
- **Municipal utilities** (e.g., JEA, OUC, Lakeland Electric, Gainesville Regional Utilities) and **electric cooperatives** set their own net-metering and program rules.

Key regulators/agencies: the **Florida PSC** regulates IOU rates and net metering; the **Florida Solar Energy Center (FSEC)** at UCF certifies qualifying solar equipment (relevant to the sales-tax exemption); the **Florida Department of Revenue** administers tax exemptions.

> **Key point:** Florida's IOUs must offer net metering, but **municipal and co-op customers should confirm their utility's specific policy.**

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Florida has **statewide net metering for investor-owned utilities**, established by PSC rule.

- IOUs (**FPL, Duke Energy Florida, TECO, FPUC**) are **required to offer net metering** to customer-generators, crediting excess generation.
- Excess monthly generation typically **rolls over as kWh credits**, and any remaining credit balance is commonly **trued-up annually** — often paid out as a credit (frequently on the January bill) at the utility's avoided-cost rate. Confirm current true-up terms with your utility.
- A prior legislative effort to phase down Florida net metering was **vetoed in 2022**, so **retail-rate net metering has remained in place for IOU customers** — but net-metering policy is periodically revisited, so **verify current rules.**
- **Municipal utilities and co-ops** set their own net-metering (or net-billing) terms, which can differ from the IOU standard.

**Commercial** customers of the IOUs are likewise eligible for net metering, typically with size tiers and interconnection tiers based on system capacity.

For the general mechanics, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

> **Key point:** Florida IOU net metering has historically been **retail-rate and favorable**, but it is a **PSC-rule / legislative matter that has faced repeated change attempts.** Verify current status with the [Florida PSC](https://www.floridapsc.com/) and DSIRE.

## SREC / RECs

Florida **does not have a mandatory statewide Renewables Portfolio Standard**, and therefore **no SREC market or distributed-solar carve-out.**

- **No homeowner-sellable SRECs** in Florida.
- Some utilities offer **voluntary green-power / REC programs**, but these do not create a retail SREC income stream for rooftop owners.

For how SREC markets work where they exist, see [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

> **Key point:** **No SREC income in Florida.** Value comes from net-metering bill credits plus the tax exemptions.

## State Tax Credits

**Florida has no state income tax**, so there is **no state personal or corporate income-tax credit for solar** — and none is needed at the income-tax level.

- **Personal:** no state income tax; no solar income-tax credit.
- **Corporate:** Florida levies a corporate income tax on businesses but offers **no solar-specific corporate income-tax credit.**

Florida's headline value is instead the **combined sales-tax and 100% property-tax exemptions** plus favorable IOU net metering. See [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md).

> Verify at [DSIRE Florida](https://programs.dsireusa.org/system/program?state=FL).

---

## Federal Tax Credits

- **Residential (§25D Residential Clean Energy Credit):** **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Homeowner purchases placed in service in 2026 or later cannot claim the 30% residential credit. See [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md).
- **Commercial (§48 / §48E ITC and MACRS depreciation):** remains available in 2026, subject to OBBBA's phase-out timeline and begin-construction / placed-in-service deadlines. Domestic-content, energy-community, and low-income adders may apply. See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

> Federal rules changed materially under OBBBA and are date-sensitive. Confirm with a tax professional and the [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

Florida has **few direct cash rebates** for solar; most utility "solar" offerings are **community-solar subscription programs** rather than rooftop rebates:

| Utility | Program (verify current) | Notes |
|---------|--------------------------|-------|
| **FPL** | SolarTogether (community/shared solar subscription) | Subscription program, not a rooftop rebate |
| **Duke Energy Florida** | Clean Energy Connection / Shared Solar | Subscription-based |
| **TECO** | Sun Select | Subscription-based |

- These subscription programs let customers support solar without a rooftop system; **they are not rooftop rebates** and pricing/availability varies (some have been fully subscribed or cost more than standard service).
- **Direct residential storage rebates are limited** in Florida; check your specific utility for any current battery or demand-response programs.

> **Key point:** Don't expect large upfront cash rebates in Florida — **the state's economics come from the tax exemptions and net metering, not utility rebates.**

For community-solar mechanics, see [Community Solar](residential-community-solar.md); for storage, see [Battery Storage](battery-storage.md).

## Sales Tax Exemption

Florida offers a **sales-and-use tax exemption for solar energy systems and components.**

- **Solar energy systems and their components are exempt from Florida sales tax**, provided the equipment is on the list of **qualifying components certified by the Florida Solar Energy Center (FSEC)**.
- This exemption **lowers upfront installed cost** and applies to both residential and, generally, commercial equipment.

> **Key point:** Florida's **solar sales-tax exemption is real and valuable.** Confirm current scope and the FSEC-certified component list with the [Florida Department of Revenue](https://floridarevenue.com/) and [DSIRE](https://programs.dsireusa.org/).

---

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Florida provides a **100% property-tax exemption for the value added by a solar energy system.**

- **Residential:** the added value of a residential solar (and certain renewable) system is **100% exempt from property taxes** — installing solar **does not increase your assessed value.**
- **Commercial:** Florida also provides an exemption (an **80% exemption** has historically applied to the assessed value of qualifying renewable-energy source devices on **non-residential/commercial** property, distinct from the 100% residential exemption). **Confirm the current residential vs. commercial percentages**, as these are statutory and can change.

> **Key point:** Residential added value is **100% exempt**; commercial treatment differs (historically an 80% exemption). Verify current percentages and any application requirements with your county property appraiser and [DSIRE](https://programs.dsireusa.org/).

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Florida has **many AHJs** — cities and counties — with varying permitting processes, though the state has pushed toward faster, standardized residential solar permitting.
- Florida is a growing adopter of **SolarAPP+** automated residential permitting in participating jurisdictions.
- **Interconnection** for IOU customers follows **PSC-standard interconnection tiers** (based on system size), which streamline the process for small residential systems; munis and co-ops have their own rules.
- Florida's building code emphasizes **wind-load / hurricane requirements** — rooftop solar mounting must meet stringent wind-uplift standards, an important design consideration statewide (especially in High-Velocity Hurricane Zones such as Miami-Dade and Broward).

See [Residential Permitting](residential-permitting.md) and [Commercial Permitting](commercial-permitting.md).

---

## Bottom Line

Florida pairs a strong solar resource with **statewide IOU net metering, a solar sales-tax exemption, and a 100% residential property-tax exemption** — an unusually favorable combination — even though it has **no state income-tax credit and no SREC market**. Utility cash rebates are limited (mostly community-solar subscriptions), so value comes from **net-metering bill credits and the tax exemptions**, plus (for commercial) the federal ITC and depreciation. Because net-metering policy has faced repeated legislative attention and exemption percentages are statutory, **verify current rules with the Florida PSC, Department of Revenue, your utility, and DSIRE before committing.**
