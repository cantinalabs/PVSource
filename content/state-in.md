# Indiana Solar Incentives & Policy

Indiana has phased out retail net metering entirely, replacing it with an **Excess Distributed Generation (EDG)** buyback that credits exports at roughly **125% of the utility's avoided wholesale cost** — well below retail. The state's most durable benefit is a **property tax deduction** that removes the added assessed value of a solar system, though that deduction carries a reported sunset. There is **no state income tax credit** and **no SREC market**.

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Incentive / Policy | Residential | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| Billing structure | EDG buyback (~125% of avoided cost); legacy NEM grandfathered | EDG buyback |
| SREC market | None (no RPS; voluntary CHOICE) | None |
| State income tax credit | None | None |
| Sales tax exemption | Generally not for typical homeowners | Production/manufacturing exemption (for-sale) |
| Property tax deduction | 100% of added value (IC 6-1.1-12-26.1) — sunset reported | 100% of added value |
| Utility solar rebate | None statewide | None statewide |
| Administering agency | IURC / utilities | IURC / utilities |

> Indiana uses **instantaneous netting** — only power exported at each moment earns the low EDG rate, while power you consume on-site offsets full retail. This makes **self-consumption far more valuable than exports**, so battery storage and load-shifting materially improve economics.

## Overview

Under **Senate Enrolled Act 309 (2017)**, Indiana phased out retail-rate net metering. New customers of the five large investor-owned utilities (IOUs) could enroll in net metering only until the earlier of July 1, 2022, or a utility reaching a cap equal to 1.5% of peak load. All five hit the cutoff, so **net metering is closed to new applicants statewide**. New solar customers now receive the **Excess Distributed Generation (EDG)** credit, governed by the Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission (**IURC**).

## Net Metering / Net Billing

**Net metering is closed to new applicants.** Grandfathering applies to earlier adopters:

- Systems interconnected **before December 31, 2017** keep full-retail net metering until **July 1, 2047**.
- Systems interconnected **January 1, 2018 – July 1, 2022** keep full-retail net metering until **July 1, 2032**.
- After those dates, grandfathered customers roll onto EDG.

**How EDG works:** Under IC 8-1-40, the EDG rate equals the utility's prior-year average wholesale/marginal price **× 1.25** (i.e., **125% of avoided cost**), updated annually. Crucially, Indiana uses **instantaneous netting** — you earn the low EDG rate only on power exported at each instant, while on-site consumption offsets full retail. (The Indiana Supreme Court upheld instantaneous netting in early 2023.)

**Representative 2026 EDG rates by utility** (approximate; recalculated annually):

| Utility | EDG rate (2026, approx.) |
|---|---|
| NIPSCO | ~$0.0497/kWh |
| AES Indiana (IPL) | ~$0.0543/kWh |
| Indiana Michigan Power (I&M / AEP) | ~$0.0547/kWh |
| Duke Energy Indiana | ~$0.0551/kWh |
| CenterPoint (Vectren) | ~$0.0556/kWh |

> **Verify current rates.** EDG rates are recalculated each spring through IURC compliance filings. Confirm against each utility's current tariff before relying on a figure. Municipal utilities and rural electric co-ops (REMCs) are **not** covered by the EDG statute and set their own buyback rates — verify at DSIRE/official site.

For how below-retail export crediting affects payback (and why self-consumption matters), see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

## SREC / RECs

Indiana has **no mandatory Renewable Portfolio Standard and no SREC market.** The only relevant program is the **voluntary** Clean Energy Portfolio Standard ("CHOICE" program), which creates no market demand for solar RECs. See the [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md) for how solar certificate markets work in states that have them.

## State Tax Credits

**Indiana offers no state income tax credit for residential or commercial solar.**

## Federal Tax Credits

Federal incentives are separate from state policy:

- **Residential (§25D):** The 30% federal residential clean energy credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Systems placed in service in 2026 and later generally do **not** qualify.
- **Commercial (ITC / §48E):** Business-owned solar may still qualify for the commercial investment tax credit and the technology-neutral **§48E** credit, subject to current rules.

See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

There is **no statewide solar rebate program**, and the five large IOUs offer no active solar-purchase rebates. A few rural co-ops offer minor efficiency-oriented rebates (not solar-panel rebates), and co-op programs change frequently — verify current terms directly with each cooperative.

## Sales Tax Exemption

Indiana has **no blanket residential solar sales tax exemption.** The relevant exemption is the **industrial production / manufacturing exemption** (e.g., IC 6-2.5-5-10.7), which applies to equipment used to produce electricity **for sale** — primarily benefiting commercial and utility-scale projects.

> A typical homeowner purchase generally does **not** qualify, because home-generated electricity (even with excess exported) is not treated as produced "for sale." Do not assume a residential exemption — verify with the Indiana DOR.

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Under **IC 6-1.1-12-26.1**, a **"solar power device" deduction** removes the added assessed value of a solar installation — effectively a **100% exemption** of the value the system adds. It applies to **both residential and commercial** real property (systems installed after December 31, 2011) and is claimed via **State Form 18865** filed with the county auditor (no annual re-filing once approved).

> **Sunset flag.** Multiple 2026 sources report the solar power device deduction carries a statutory sunset around **January 1, 2027**. As of mid-2026 it remains available, but confirm the current status against the text of IC 6-1.1-12-26.1 and Indiana DLGF guidance — verify at DSIRE/official site.

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Interconnection with IOUs follows Indiana's standards at **170 IAC 4-4.3**; an executed interconnection agreement is required before energizing, and the utility installs a bidirectional meter.
- There is no unified statewide solar permit — local building/electrical permits are handled by the municipal or county Authority Having Jurisdiction, and each utility (Duke, AES, NIPSCO, I&M, CenterPoint) has its own DG application process.

## Bottom Line

Indiana is a challenging retail-credit environment: net metering is gone, and **EDG credits exports at roughly 125% of avoided cost** with **instantaneous netting**, so maximizing **self-consumption** (right-sizing systems, adding storage) is essential to good economics. There is **no state income tax credit, no SREC market, no residential sales tax exemption, and no utility rebate** — the main state benefit is the **property tax deduction** removing added assessed value, which carries a reported 2027 sunset worth verifying. With the residential federal §25D credit ended after 2025, 2026 residential buyers lean on the property tax deduction and self-consumption savings; businesses can still pursue the commercial ITC/§48E. Confirm current EDG rates and the property tax deduction status before proceeding.

> **Educational use only.** This page is a general reference, not tax, legal, or financial advice. Incentive values, tariff rates, and program rules change frequently. Verify all figures with the IURC, the Indiana Department of Revenue, the DLGF, your utility, and a qualified professional before making decisions.
