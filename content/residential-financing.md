# Homeowner Financing 101

How you pay for a home solar system can change your lifetime savings by tens of thousands of dollars — often more than the choice of panels. This page walks through the main ways homeowners finance residential solar, who owns the system in each, who claims (or claimed) the tax credit, and the trade-offs, red flags, and dealer-fee pitfalls to watch for.

A critical change frames everything below: **the federal residential solar tax credit (Section 25D) expired for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025** under the 2025 budget law (OBBBA). Homeowners who buy a system in 2026 and beyond generally **cannot** claim the 30% federal credit themselves. See [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md) for the full picture, including how third-party-owned systems may still capture a business credit.

## The five main financing paths

| Method | Who owns the system | Who gets/claimed the tax credit | You take on debt? | Typical term |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash purchase** | You | You (25D — only if placed in service by 12/31/2025) | No | n/a |
| **Solar loan** | You | You (25D — same deadline caveat) | Yes | 10–25 yrs |
| **Lease** | Third party | The lessor (business credit, e.g. 48E) | No (monthly payment) | 20–25 yrs |
| **PPA** | Third party | The provider (business credit) | No (pay per kWh) | 20–25 yrs |
| **Residential PACE** | You | You (25D — same deadline caveat) | Yes (via property tax) | 5–30 yrs |

### 1. Cash purchase

You pay the full cost upfront and own the system outright.

- **Pros:** Lowest lifetime cost (no interest, no dealer fees, no escalators). Highest home-value benefit at resale for owned systems (see [Selling or Buying a Home with Solar](residential-selling-home.md)). You captured any incentives directly.
- **Cons:** Large upfront outlay. Opportunity cost of the capital. You bear maintenance and warranty-claim responsibility.
- **Payback:** Shortest of all options because there is no financing cost layered on top.

### 2. Solar loan

A loan lets you own the system while spreading payments over time. Loans come in two flavors:

- **Secured** (e.g., home equity loan/HELOC) — backed by your home, usually lower interest.
- **Unsecured (solar-specific) loans** — offered through installers/dealers, no home lien but often carry **dealer fees**.

**"$0 down" and the dealer-fee trap.** Many "$0 down" solar loans advertise a low interest rate (sometimes near 0%). To offer that rate, the lender charges the installer a **dealer fee** — commonly **15%–30%** of system cost — which the installer then bakes into a **higher sticker price**. So the same system might be quoted at, say, $25,000 cash but $32,000 on a "0.99%" loan.

```
Cash price:                     $25,000
Same system on a "low-rate" loan (25% dealer fee):
   Grossed-up price = $25,000 / (1 - 0.25) = $33,333
```

> The dealer fee is often invisible on the contract. Always ask for the **cash price** and the **financed price** side by side, and compare the total amount you will actually repay (principal + interest) against the cash price.

- **Pros:** Ownership; you keep incentives; no escalator (payment is fixed).
- **Cons:** Interest and dealer fees raise total cost; a lien (often a UCC-1 fixture filing) may complicate a home sale until paid off — see [Selling or Buying a Home with Solar](residential-selling-home.md).

### 3. Lease

A third party owns the system on your roof; you pay a **fixed monthly lease payment** to use it, regardless of production.

- **Ownership:** The leasing company owns the equipment.
- **Tax credit:** The lessor claims any applicable **business** credit — not you. This is why leases/PPAs remain marketed in 2026 even though the homeowner 25D credit has ended.
- **Escalator:** Most leases include an **annual escalator** (commonly **1%–3%/year**) that raises your payment every year — sometimes faster than utility rates rise.
- **Pros:** Little to no upfront cost; provider handles maintenance/monitoring; predictable payment.
- **Cons:** You do not own the system, so you generally get **little or no home-value premium** at resale; escalators can erode savings; transferring the contract at sale can be a closing headache.

### 4. Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)

Similar to a lease, but instead of a fixed rent you pay a **per-kWh rate** for the electricity the system produces.

- **Ownership:** Third party owns the system and claims any business credit.
- **Escalator:** PPA rates also commonly escalate (often 1%–3%/year).
- **Pros:** Pay only for energy produced; no upfront cost; maintenance included.
- **Cons:** If production is high (sunny years) your bill is high; savings depend on staying below utility rates as both escalate; same resale/transfer complications as a lease. Under **net-billing tariffs (NEM 3.0)**, PPA value math is more complex because exports are worth less — see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

### 5. Residential PACE (where available)

**Property Assessed Clean Energy** financing repays the cost as a **line item on your property tax bill**, secured by a lien on the property (senior in some structures).

- **Availability:** Residential PACE is **very limited** — as of 2025 primarily **California and Florida** (Missouri's residential program ended in 2024). Availability changes; verify locally.
- **Ownership:** You own the system.
- **Pros:** No FICO-driven approval in some programs (based on home equity); ties repayment to the property.
- **Cons:** Interest rates often **6%–9%**, and long terms mean you may pay **thousands more** than a conventional solar loan. Critically, **Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac generally will not purchase mortgages** on homes with a PACE lien, which can shrink your buyer pool or force a payoff at sale. New federal rules now apply mortgage-style disclosure standards to residential PACE.

> PACE has a history of aggressive door-to-door sales and consumer complaints. Treat any PACE pitch with the same scrutiny as a high-pressure loan.

## Effect on payback and lifetime savings

- **Cash** almost always wins on total lifetime savings and shortest payback.
- **Loans** preserve ownership and incentives but add interest/dealer-fee cost; a fair-priced loan can still beat leasing over 25 years.
- **Leases/PPAs** lower upfront cost and remove maintenance worry but typically deliver the **lowest lifetime savings** and **least resale benefit**, especially once escalators compound.

```
Escalator effect (3%/year lease starting at $120/month):
   Year 1:  $120/mo   = $1,440/yr
   Year 10: $157/mo   = $1,879/yr
   Year 20: $211/mo   = $2,527/yr
Compare against your utility rate trajectory before assuming savings.
```

## Transferability at home sale

- **Owned (cash/loan/PACE):** If financed, the loan or PACE lien usually must be **paid off at or before closing** (or, rarely, assumed). A UCC-1 fixture filing must be **terminated** so title is clear.
- **Lease/PPA:** The contract must be **transferred (assumed) by the buyer**, who must **credit-qualify** with the provider, or the seller must **buy out** the remaining term (a prepayment/buyout figure). Transfer fees commonly run **$100–$500+**. This is one of the most common last-minute snags in solar-home closings — covered in [Selling or Buying a Home with Solar](residential-selling-home.md).

## Red flags and dealer-fee pitfalls

> Be cautious if you see any of these:

- **No cash price offered.** If the salesperson will only quote a monthly payment, you cannot see the dealer fee.
- **"It pays for itself / it's free."** Nothing is free; the cost is inside the loan, lease, or PPA (often plus an escalator).
- **High-pressure "today only" pricing** or urgency around expiring incentives.
- **Production estimates that seem too high** or ignore shading, roof orientation, or degradation.
- **Escalators glossed over.** Ask for the escalator rate and the payment in years 1, 10, and 20.
- **Blank contract fields, verbal promises not in writing, or unlicensed installers.** Verify licensing (see [Insurance, Warranties & Consumer Protection](residential-insurance-warranties.md)).
- **Long-term liens (PACE)** without disclosure of the resale/mortgage impact.

**Always get 3 independent quotes**, insist on the cash price, read the full contract (including escalators, transfer terms, and warranty obligations), and use any state **right-of-rescission** window if you have second thoughts.
