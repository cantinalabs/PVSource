# Nonprofit, Municipal & School Solar

Tax-exempt organizations — **nonprofits, houses of worship, municipalities, public K-12 schools, water districts, and Tribal governments** — own enormous amounts of building stock and pay large electricity bills, yet for decades they were largely shut out of the single most valuable solar incentive: the federal **Investment Tax Credit (ITC)**. Because they owe no federal income tax, they had **no tax liability to offset**, so the credit was worthless to them directly.

That changed with **Direct Pay (elective payment)** under **IRC §6417**, created by the Inflation Reduction Act and preserved under the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Direct Pay lets a tax-exempt entity claim the clean-energy ITC and receive its value as a **cash payment from the IRS** rather than a tax offset. This page explains how that works, the procurement models available, how to finance projects, and the wage/content rules that affect the credit amount.

This is a commercial-side page. For the underlying credit mechanics that apply to taxable businesses, see [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md). For resilience and backup, see [Commercial Microgrids & Resilience](commercial-microgrids.md); for demand savings, [Commercial Storage & Demand-Charge Management](commercial-storage.md).

## The historic "tax appetite" problem

The ITC is a **nonrefundable tax credit** — historically it could only reduce taxes owed. A church, city, or school district owes no federal income tax, so it had no "appetite" for the credit.

The old workaround was a **third-party Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)** or lease: a taxable developer would own the system, claim the ITC, and sell the electricity to the tax-exempt host. The tax benefit reached the nonprofit only indirectly, passed through as a lower PPA price — and the developer kept a share. Direct Pay removes that middleman where the organization wants to **own** its system.

## Direct Pay / elective payment under IRC §6417

Under §6417, an **applicable entity** can make an election to treat the amount of an eligible clean-energy credit as a **payment against tax**. Because the entity owes little or no tax, the IRS refunds the credit as **cash**.

**Who qualifies as an applicable entity:**

- Tax-exempt organizations under §501(c) (nonprofits, charities, houses of worship)
- State, local, and territorial governments and their political subdivisions (cities, counties, school districts, water/utility districts)
- Tribal governments and Alaska Native corporations
- Rural electric cooperatives, the TVA, and certain public utilities

**Eligible credits include** the clean electricity investment credit (§48E / legacy §48 ITC) and the production credit (§45Y), among others.

### How much can be monetized

The base credit is **30%** of eligible project cost when wage/apprenticeship requirements are met (or the project is under 1 MW). Stackable **bonus adders** can raise the effective rate to roughly **40–50%+**:

| Component | Credit value |
|---|---|
| Base ITC (meets prevailing wage & apprenticeship, or < 1 MW AC) | 30% |
| **Domestic content** adder | +10% |
| **Energy community** adder | +10% |
| **Low-income** adder (§48E(h) allocation, where available) | +10% or +20% |

> Without meeting prevailing-wage and apprenticeship rules, projects **1 MW AC and larger** fall to a **6% base** rate — a steep penalty. Projects under 1 MW AC get the full 30% automatically.

### The elective-payment process

1. **Place the project in service** and confirm it meets all credit requirements.
2. Complete **pre-filing registration** through the IRS IRA/CHIPS portal to obtain a **registration number** for each property — this is mandatory and must be done before filing.
3. **File an annual return** (e.g., Form 990-T or 990 for tax-exempts; the relevant return for governments) with **Form 3800** and the source credit form, making the §6417 election.
4. Receive the **cash payment** from the IRS after processing.

### Critical 2026 deadlines

OBBBA preserved elective pay but **accelerated the sunset** of the underlying solar credits. As of mid-2026, projects generally must **begin construction by July 4, 2026** (with a four-year placed-in-service window), and projects beginning construction after that date must be **placed in service by December 31, 2027**. New **foreign-entity-of-concern (FEOC) / material-assistance** restrictions also limit credits for projects built with significant Chinese-manufactured components.

> These deadlines are time-sensitive and were tightened in 2025. Confirm the current "begin construction" and placed-in-service rules with a tax professional before committing capital.

## Worked Direct Pay example

A school district installs a **750 kW** rooftop and carport array.

```
System size:                          750 kW AC  (under 1 MW -> full 30% base, no wage rules)
Total eligible project cost:          $1,800,000
Base ITC rate:                        30%
Domestic content adder:               +10%
Energy community adder (qualifies):   +10%
-------------------------------------------------
Effective credit rate:                50%
Direct Pay (cash from IRS):           0.50 x $1,800,000 = $900,000
-------------------------------------------------
Net cost after Direct Pay:            $1,800,000 - $900,000 = $900,000
```

The district owns the array outright and receives a **`$900,000` cash refund** roughly a year after placing it in service. If it pairs Direct Pay with a financing bridge (below), it can avoid laying out the full amount upfront.

> The IRS payment arrives **after** the project is in service and the return is filed — not at installation. Districts and nonprofits typically need **bridge financing** to cover the gap between paying the contractor and receiving the refund.

## Procurement models

### 1. Direct ownership + Direct Pay (now usually best)

The entity buys and owns the system and claims the credit as cash via §6417. It captures **100% of the energy savings and the full credit**, with no developer margin skimmed off. Best when the organization can finance construction and float the refund timing.

### 2. Third-party PPA or lease (still useful)

A taxable developer owns the system, claims the credit, and sells power to the host under a long-term PPA at a fixed `$/kWh`, or leases the equipment. Advantages:

- **No upfront capital** and no IRS-refund timing risk.
- Developer handles O&M and performance risk.
- Useful where the entity **can't access affordable financing** or wants to avoid asset ownership.

The trade-off is that the developer keeps part of the tax value. Compare the lifetime PPA cost against owning with Direct Pay.

| | Direct ownership + Direct Pay | Third-party PPA / lease |
|---|---|---|
| Who claims credit | The entity (as cash) | The developer |
| Upfront capital | Needed (or financed) | None |
| Savings captured | Maximum | Shared with developer |
| O&M responsibility | Owner | Developer (typically) |
| Best when | Financing available | Capital-constrained |

## Municipal & nonprofit financing

Owning a system requires capital up front. Common public-sector tools:

- **General-obligation or revenue bonds** — municipalities finance solar like other capital projects; energy savings help service the debt.
- **Lease-purchase / tax-exempt lease-purchase (TELP)** agreements — the entity pays over time and owns the asset at the end; widely used by schools and cities because it avoids a large upfront outlay and can fit within capital budgets.
- **Energy Savings Performance Contracts (ESPCs)** — an ESCO designs, installs, and guarantees savings that repay the project.
- **Qualified energy conservation bonds and state green banks / revolving loan funds.**
- **Direct Pay bridge loans** — short-term financing sized to the expected IRS refund.

## Prevailing wage & domestic content interactions

- **Prevailing wage & apprenticeship (PWA):** Projects **1 MW AC and larger** must pay Davis-Bacon-style prevailing wages and meet apprenticeship-hour requirements to earn the full 30% (otherwise 6%). Build PWA recordkeeping into procurement contracts from day one. Projects **under 1 MW AC are exempt** and get 30% automatically.
- **Domestic content:** Meeting steel/iron and manufactured-product thresholds earns the **+10% adder**. Document supplier certifications.
- **Energy community:** Sites on brownfields, in fossil-fuel-employment areas, or in qualifying census tracts earn **+10%**. Check the location against current Treasury/DOE maps.
- **FEOC / material assistance:** New OBBBA rules can disqualify credits for projects with significant prohibited-foreign-entity content — vet the supply chain early.

## School-specific considerations

K-12 schools are among the strongest candidates for solar and deserve special attention:

- **Carports and shade structures** add covered parking, EV-charging hosting, and storm protection alongside generation.
- **Pair with storage and microgrid controls** so a school can serve as a community **resilience hub** during outages — see [Commercial Microgrids & Resilience](commercial-microgrids.md).
- **Curriculum integration:** rooftop arrays double as STEM teaching tools (real-time generation dashboards).
- **Budget cycles:** align procurement with bond elections and capital-improvement plans; lease-purchase fits annual operating budgets.
- **Roof condition:** re-roof before installing — don't mount on a roof near end of life.
- **Demand charges:** schools with high summer demand benefit from pairing solar with storage; see [Commercial Storage & Demand-Charge Management](commercial-storage.md).
