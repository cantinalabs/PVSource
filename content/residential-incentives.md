# Residential Incentives

Residential solar economics are driven by a stack of incentives: the **federal tax credit**, the **billing arrangement** for exported energy (net metering vs. net billing), **state and utility rebates/credits**, **SREC** markets, **tax exemptions**, **low-income programs**, and how the chosen **financing** structure determines who actually captures the federal benefit. This page covers the residential-specific picture for 1–2 family dwellings — kept separate from the site's commercial incentive guides.

> **Most important 2026 change:** The **federal Section 25D Residential Clean Energy Credit (30%)** was **terminated early** by the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, H.R.1, signed July 4, 2025)**. As verified June 30, 2026, **25D applies only to expenditures made — i.e., installations completed/placed in service — on or before December 31, 2025.** A homeowner who **buys** a system (cash or loan) and completes installation **after** that date generally gets **no federal residential credit**. See the worked example below, and verify your situation with a tax professional and the IRS.

## Federal Section 25D — and Its OBBBA Sunset (Verified)

| Item | Status (verified June 30, 2026) |
|---|---|
| Statute | **IRC Section 25D**, Residential Clean Energy Credit |
| Credit rate | **30%** of qualified expenditures (no overall dollar cap for solar PV) |
| What changed | **OBBBA (H.R.1)** amended 25D(h): the credit terminates for **expenditures made after December 31, 2025** (previously the credit ran through 2034 with a later step-down) |
| "Expenditure made" | Under 25D, an expenditure is treated as made **when the original installation is completed** — so **placed-in-service / completion date** controls, not contract or payment date |
| Cutoff | Installation **completed on or before Dec 31, 2025** can claim 25D on the 2025 return (filed in 2026); completed **after** that date **cannot** |
| Carryforward | Unused 25D credit from a **qualifying** (pre-2026) installation may still be **carried forward** to later tax years |

> **Warning:** Marketing claiming "you can still get 30% in 2026" for a **homeowner-owned** purchase is generally inaccurate for the **Section 25D** credit. The only common path to federal value on a residential install completed in 2026 is **third-party ownership (lease/PPA)**, where the *provider* — not the homeowner — claims the separate **commercial** credit (Section 48E) and may pass savings through. That commercial path has its own deadlines (broadly tied to construction beginning by July 4, 2026 or placed-in-service by end of 2027). Verify specifics with the provider and a tax professional.

## Net Metering vs. Net Billing (NEM 3.0)

How your utility compensates **exported** energy is often the largest ongoing driver of residential value.

| Feature | **Net metering (NEM)** | **Net billing** |
|---|---|---|
| Export credit | At or near **full retail** rate | Lower **avoided-cost / export** rate |
| Effect of self-consumption | Offsets retail import | Offsets retail import |
| Trend | Being scaled back in many states | The growing national model |
| Battery value | Lower | **Higher** (store midday, use/export at peak) |

> **California NEM 3.0 / Net Billing (verified June 2026):** California's IOUs operate under the **Net Billing Tariff**, effective April 15, 2023. Exports are credited via the **Avoided Cost Calculator** — roughly **75% below retail** (often ~$0.05–$0.08/kWh). Rates are locked to your PTO **vintage year** for ~9 years. A California court **upheld NEM 3.0 in March 2026**. As a result, **adding a battery** is now central to residential payback in California (storing midday production for expensive evening hours). NEM 2.0 grandfathering generally required pre-April-15-2023 applications and PTO before April 15, 2026.

Net-metering rules vary enormously by state and utility — full 1:1 retail in some, near-zero in others. Confirm your specific tariff with your utility and check [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) and [SEIA's net metering overview](https://seia.org/net-metering/).

## State Residential Tax Credits & Rebates

Many states and utilities offer their own residential incentives on top of (or now, in place of) the lapsed federal credit. These vary widely, change frequently, and may be capped or first-come-first-served.

| Incentive type | What it does | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **State income-tax credit** | Reduces state tax liability by a % or fixed amount (e.g., historically NY, SC, and others) | Caps and rates differ; some have sunset dates |
| **Upfront rebate** | Cash/$-per-watt rebate from a state agency or utility | Often budget-limited; may require approved equipment/installers |
| **Performance/production incentive** | Pays per kWh produced over a term | Less common for residential than commercial |
| **Storage incentives** | Rebates for adding batteries (e.g., California SGIP-type programs) | Especially relevant under net billing |

> Because state and utility programs change constantly and differ by location, do not rely on a static list. Look up your exact state, county, and utility on the **[DSIRE database](https://www.dsireusa.org/)** — the authoritative, regularly updated source for U.S. residential incentives.

## Residential SREC Participation

In states with **Renewable Portfolio Standards** that include a **solar carve-out**, residential systems can earn **Solar Renewable Energy Certificates (SRECs)** — typically **one SREC per 1,000 kWh** (1 MWh) generated. SRECs are sold (often through aggregators) to utilities that must meet solar obligations.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Where it applies | Active markets in select states (e.g., historically NJ/SREC-II, PA, MD, DC, MA, OH) |
| Value | Set by **market supply/demand**; can be volatile, from a few dollars to hundreds per SREC depending on the market |
| How homeowners participate | Register the system with the state program/tracking system; sell via an **aggregator** or broker |
| Risk | Prices can decline as markets fill; not guaranteed long-term |

SREC income is separate from net metering and from any tax credit. Verify whether your state has an active residential market and the current pricing/registration process via [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) and your state program.

## Property- & Sales-Tax Exemptions

Many states reduce the tax burden of going solar:

| Exemption | Effect for homeowners |
|---|---|
| **Property-tax exemption** | The added home value from solar is **excluded from property-tax assessment**, so your taxes don't rise because of the system |
| **Sales-tax exemption** | Solar equipment purchase is **exempt from state/local sales tax**, lowering upfront cost |

Both are common but **not universal**, and the structure (full vs. partial, permanent vs. term-limited) varies. Confirm availability for your state and locality on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/).

## Low-Income Residential Programs

Several program types aim to extend solar to **low- and moderate-income (LMI)** households:

- **State/utility LMI solar programs** offering enhanced rebates or no-cost installations for income-qualified homeowners.
- **Community solar** subscriptions (where allowed) that provide bill credits without rooftop ownership — useful for renters and homes with poor roofs.
- **Weatherization / energy-assistance** programs sometimes bundled with solar or efficiency upgrades.

Eligibility, funding, and availability are highly local. Check your **state energy office**, your **utility**, and [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) for current LMI offerings.

## Financing Options & Who Claims the Credit

The financing structure determines **ownership**, which determines **who can claim any federal credit**. This is the most-misunderstood part of residential solar economics — especially after the 25D sunset.

| Option | Who owns the system | Who claims federal credit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash purchase** | Homeowner | Homeowner (via **25D**) — **only if completed by 12/31/2025**; otherwise **none** | Lowest lifetime cost when 25D applied; maximum control |
| **Solar loan** | Homeowner | Homeowner (via **25D**) — same 12/31/2025 limit | $0-down options common; watch for "dealer fees" baked into rate |
| **Lease** | **Third-party provider** | **Provider** (commercial credit, e.g., **48E**) | Homeowner pays fixed monthly lease; provider's tax benefit may lower the payment |
| **PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)** | **Third-party provider** | **Provider** (commercial credit) | Homeowner buys the **kWh produced** at an agreed rate; no system ownership |

> **Key point (post-2025):** For a system **owned by the homeowner** and completed **after Dec 31, 2025**, there is **no Section 25D credit**. Federal incentive value on a 2026 install typically only flows through **third-party-owned** lease/PPA structures, where the **provider** claims the commercial credit and may pass part of the savings into a lower lease/PPA price. Whether that net result beats ownership depends entirely on the contract terms — read them carefully.

## Worked Example: How the Credit Works for a Homeowner

Assume a **$24,000** residential PV system, homeowner-owned (cash or loan).

**Scenario A — installation completed on or before Dec 31, 2025 (25D applies):**

```
  System cost ........................ $24,000
  Section 25D credit (30%) ........... -$7,200   (nonrefundable; reduces
                                                  federal tax liability)
  Net federal cost ................... $16,800

  - If your 2025 federal tax liability is < $7,200, the unused
    portion CARRIES FORWARD to later tax years.
  - State/utility rebates, SRECs, and net-metering savings are
    ADDITIONAL and stack on top of this.
```

**Scenario B — same system, installation completed in 2026 (homeowner-owned):**

```
  System cost ........................ $24,000
  Section 25D credit ................. $0   (TERMINATED for expenditures
                                            made after 12/31/2025)
  Net federal cost ................... $24,000

  Possible federal value path: a LEASE/PPA instead, where the
  PROVIDER claims the commercial credit and may reflect part of it
  in your monthly price. Homeowner does NOT claim a federal credit.
```

> **Reminder:** The 25D credit is **nonrefundable** — it offsets tax owed, not a cash refund — but a qualifying pre-2026 system's unused credit **carries forward**. State credits, exemptions, SRECs, and your net-metering/net-billing savings are evaluated **separately and in addition**.
