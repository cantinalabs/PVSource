# State Residential Tax Credits & Rebates

With the **federal §25D credit ended** for homeowner-owned systems placed in service after Dec 31, 2025 (see [Residential Federal Solar Tax Credit (§25D)](resinc-federal.md)), **state-level** incentives have become relatively more important to residential solar economics. Many states offer their own **personal income-tax credits** or **rebates** for homeowners. This page surveys the common types, shows how they interacted with the (now-ended) federal credit, and explains how to find your state's current programs.

> **Programs change constantly — do not rely on a static list.** State credits and rebates are frequently amended, capped, and sunset. The figures below are illustrative and were commonly cited as of the site's writing; **verify current values for your state on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/)** before relying on any number.

## Types of State Residential Incentives

| Incentive type | What it does | Typical structure |
|---|---|---|
| **State income-tax credit** | Reduces your **state** income tax by a % of system cost (often capped) | Nonrefundable; may carry forward |
| **Upfront rebate** | Cash or $/watt paid toward installation | Budget-limited; first-come, first-served |
| **Performance-based incentive (PBI)** | Pays per kWh produced over a term | More common for commercial; some residential |
| **Sales-tax exemption** | Removes state/local sales tax on equipment | See [Property & Sales-Tax Exemptions](resinc-tax-exempt.md) |
| **Property-tax exemption** | Excludes added home value from assessment | See [Property & Sales-Tax Exemptions](resinc-tax-exempt.md) |

## Survey of State Personal Income-Tax Credits

The following states have (or recently had) **residential income-tax credits** for solar. **Verify each on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) — rates, caps, and availability change.**

| State | Credit (as commonly cited) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **New York** | **25%** of cost, up to **$5,000** | One of the most valuable residential credits; nonrefundable, carryforward. See [NY-Sun & VDER](incentive-ny-sun-vder.md) |
| **Hawaii** | **35%** of cost, cap **~$5,000** per 5 kW system | High-value; multiple systems can each qualify |
| **Arizona** | **25%** of cost, up to **$1,000** | Modest cap; long-standing program |
| **Massachusetts** | **15%** of cost, up to **$1,000** | Nonrefundable, carryforward; stacks with SMART. See [MA SMART](incentive-ma-smart.md) |
| **South Carolina** | **25%** of cost | Generous rate; per-year usage limits with carryforward |
| **New Mexico** | **10%** of cost (New Solar Market Development credit), cap **~$6,000** | Program has had funding caps |
| **Iowa** | Historically **15%**, cap **~$5,000** | Program has faced funding sunsets — verify status |
| **Montana** | Modest fixed-amount residential credit historically | Verify current availability |

> These figures are **illustrative snapshots**, not current guarantees. Some of these credits have expiration dates, waitlists, or annual funding caps that can pause new claims. Always confirm the **current** rate, cap, and program status on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) and your **state department of revenue / energy office**.

Many states with **no** income-tax credit still offer strong value through **net metering** ([Residential Net Metering & Net Billing](resinc-net-metering.md)), **SRECs** ([Residential SRECs for Homeowners](resinc-srec.md)), **tax exemptions** ([Property & Sales-Tax Exemptions](resinc-tax-exempt.md)), or **utility rebates** ([Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md)).

## How State Credits Stacked With the Federal Credit

While §25D was in force (through 2025), state credits generally **stacked on top of** the federal credit — but the interaction mattered:

```
  Example (system completed in 2025, in a hypothetical 25% state
  credit state, $24,000 system):

  Federal 25D (30%) ...................... -$7,200
  State income-tax credit (25%) .......... -$6,000
  Combined tax credits ................... -$13,200
  Net cost after credits ................. $10,800
```

Two important nuances that still apply to state credits today:

- **Rebate-first ordering.** A **state or utility rebate** paid to the homeowner generally **reduced the cost basis** used to compute the (federal) credit — you couldn't claim a percentage credit on money someone else paid. State income-tax credits, by contrast, were computed on the system cost and did not reduce the federal basis the same way. Rules vary — confirm with a tax professional.
- **Nonrefundable + carryforward.** Most state credits, like §25D, are **nonrefundable** (offset tax owed, not a cash payment) and often allow **carryforward** of unused amounts. A household with low state tax liability may take several years to fully use a large credit.

> **Post-2025 reality:** With §25D gone for homeowner-owned 2026 installs, state credits now stack with **utility rebates, SRECs, exemptions, and net-metering value** — but no longer with a federal residential credit. See [Residential Federal Solar Tax Credit (§25D)](resinc-federal.md).

## How to Find Your State's Programs

1. **Start with [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/)** — the authoritative, regularly updated database of U.S. incentives. Search by ZIP and filter for "Residential" and "Financial Incentive."
2. **Check your state energy office / department of revenue** for the exact form, cap, carryforward rules, and any waitlist.
3. **Check your utility** separately — utility rebates ([Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md)) are distinct from state tax credits and often more time-sensitive.
4. **Confirm current-year status** — some credits have annual funding that runs out mid-year.
5. **Consult a tax professional** before assuming any credit applies to your situation.

For a state-by-state orientation, use the site's state guides (linked from the U.S. State Solar Map), and see program-specific deep-dives like [NY-Sun & VDER](incentive-ny-sun-vder.md), [MA SMART](incentive-ma-smart.md), and [NJ SuSI](incentive-nj-susi.md).
