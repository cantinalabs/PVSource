# Transferability of Credits (§6418)

**Transferability** under **Internal Revenue Code (IRC) §6418** lets a taxable business that earns a clean-energy tax credit **sell that credit to an unrelated party for cash**, rather than using it against its own tax bill or raising complex tax-equity financing. Introduced by the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in 2022, transferability did more than any other provision to broaden the market for clean-energy credits — it turned a lawyer-intensive partnership structure into something closer to a commodity trade. It survives the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)** (H.R.1, signed July 4, 2025), with a significant new restriction: credits can no longer be transferred to prohibited foreign entities.

This page covers what can be sold and by whom, the tax treatment, the one-time-transfer rule, registration and documentation, market pricing and discounts, recapture and buyer diligence, the OBBBA/FEOC transfer prohibition, and how transferability compares to traditional tax equity and to [Direct Pay (Elective Payment)](incentive-direct-pay.md). See also [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md) and [The "Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA)](big-beautiful-bill.md).

> Transferability is for **taxable** businesses. If your organization is tax-exempt or governmental, you generally use [direct pay](incentive-direct-pay.md) instead — you don't need to sell a credit you can already cash out from the IRS.

## What Can Be Sold, and By Whom

An **eligible taxpayer** — broadly, any taxpayer that is **not** an applicable entity eligible for direct pay — can elect to transfer all or a portion of an eligible credit. Transferable credits include the ones central to solar and storage:

- **§48E** Clean Electricity Investment Tax Credit (and legacy **§48** ITC)
- **§45Y** Clean Electricity Production Tax Credit (and legacy **§45** PTC)
- **§45X** Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit
- **§45Q**, **§45V**, **§30C**, and others

The credit is sold to an **unrelated third party** (a "transferee") for **cash**. The classic case: a solar developer with a large ITC but insufficient tax liability sells the credit to a profitable corporation that wants to reduce its federal tax bill.

## Tax Treatment: Tax-Free In, Non-Deductible Out

The economics are engineered to be clean on both sides:

- **To the seller (transferor):** the cash received for the credit is **excluded from gross income** — it is **not taxable**. The seller gives up a credit and receives tax-free cash.
- **To the buyer (transferee):** the amount **paid for the credit is not deductible**. The buyer pays, say, 92 cents and uses a $1.00 credit against its tax — the 8-cent spread is its economic return, and it can't also deduct the 92 cents.

This symmetry is what makes the discount the buyer's yield and keeps the transaction from generating a second layer of tax benefit.

## One-Time Transfer

A given credit can be transferred **only once**. The buyer who acquires a credit **cannot re-sell it** to yet another party. A seller *may* split a single credit and sell portions to multiple buyers, but each portion, once transferred, is final. This one-time rule shapes the market: buyers are end-users of the credit, not intermediaries flipping paper.

## Registration & Documentation

Like direct pay, transferability runs through the IRS **Energy Credits Online (ECO)** pre-filing registration portal:

1. **Pre-filing registration.** The *seller* registers each credit property and obtains a **registration number** — required before the transfer can be reported. Plan for the IRS review window (up to ~120 days); register well before the filing deadline.
2. **Transfer election on the return.** Both parties reflect the transfer on their tax returns; the seller includes the registration number and a **transfer election statement**.
3. **Transfer agreement.** A written agreement between the parties documenting the credit, amount, price, and required representations. It typically allocates recapture risk and includes indemnities.

Registration is required for the transfer to be valid, but it is **not** IRS pre-approval of the credit itself — the credit's validity is still subject to examination.

## Market Pricing & Discounts

Buyers pay **less than face value** — the discount is their return and their compensation for risk and timing. Pricing depends on credit type, deal size, the seller's credit quality, the strength of the tax insurance and indemnity package, and market supply/demand.

| Factor | Effect on price |
|---|---|
| Large, well-documented ITC with tax insurance | Higher price (smaller discount) |
| PTC (paid over 10 years, more diligence) | Often larger discount than ITC |
| Small deal, thin documentation | Larger discount |
| Recapture exposure, weak indemnity | Larger discount |
| Strong buyer demand / scarce credits | Smaller discount |

As a rough market frame, ITCs have commonly traded at roughly **90–95 cents on the dollar**, with PTCs and riskier or smaller deals seeing deeper discounts. **Verify current pricing** — the market moves with supply, interest rates, and post-OBBBA sentiment.

## Recapture & Buyer Diligence

The single most important risk for a buyer is **recapture**. If, within the **five-year ITC recapture period**, the underlying property is sold, ceases to be qualifying, or otherwise triggers recapture, the tax benefit can be clawed back — and under §6418 the **recapture liability generally falls on the buyer** (the transferee), not the seller, unless the parties contractually shift and indemnify it.

Because of this, buyers conduct real diligence and de-risk:

- **Diligence** on the project's credit qualification, cost basis, prevailing-wage/apprenticeship compliance, and domestic-content and FEOC status.
- **Indemnity agreements** in which the seller agrees to make the buyer whole for a recapture or disallowance.
- **Tax insurance** covering credit validity and recapture — now a standard feature of larger deals.

## OBBBA / FEOC: No Transfers to Prohibited Foreign Entities

OBBBA left the §6418 transfer mechanism largely intact but added a hard restriction. For tax years **beginning after July 4, 2025**, a credit **cannot be transferred to a "specified foreign entity"** (an SFE) — part of the broader **Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) / prohibited foreign entity** regime. In practice:

- **Buyers must not be prohibited foreign entities.** Sellers and their advisors screen transferees for FEOC status, and transfer agreements now include FEOC representations.
- **The underlying credit must itself survive FEOC rules.** OBBBA can deny credits to projects with disqualifying foreign ownership or material assistance from prohibited entities; a transfer cannot rescue a credit that FEOC rules have already killed.
- **Solar timing still governs eligibility.** Because §48E/§45Y solar credits phase out under OBBBA (generally: placed in service after Dec. 31, 2027 unless construction begins by July 4, 2026), transferability only matters for credits that still exist. Confirm the beginning-of-construction position, which has been subject to 2025–2026 IRS guidance and litigation.

## Transferability vs. Tax Equity vs. Direct Pay

| Feature | Transferability (§6418) | Traditional Tax Equity | Direct Pay (§6417) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who monetizes | Taxable business selling to a buyer | Developer partnering with a tax-equity investor | Tax-exempts/governments/tribes/co-ops |
| Structure | Simple credit sale for cash | Complex partnership (e.g., partnership flip) | Election on a return; cash from IRS |
| Cost/complexity | Low — days/weeks, contract-based | High — months, heavy legal/accounting | Moderate — registration + return |
| What's monetized | The tax credit only | Credit **plus** depreciation and cash flows | The tax credit only |
| Proceeds | Face value minus discount | Larger nominal value, but high transaction cost | Face value (subject to domestic-content phase-out for 1 MW+) |
| FEOC limit | **No transfer to prohibited foreign entities (OBBBA)** | FEOC rules apply to the credit | FEOC rules apply to the credit |

**How it broadened the market.** Before transferability, monetizing a credit essentially required a **tax-equity partnership** — expensive, slow, and available only to developers large enough to attract a handful of specialized investors. Transferability let a much wider pool of ordinary profitable corporations buy credits with a contract instead of a partnership, dramatically increasing the number of both sellers and buyers, and shrinking deal timelines and legal cost. Tax equity still exists (and still captures depreciation and other benefits that a pure credit sale does not), and many deals now combine the two.

## Worked Example: A Credit Sale

**Facts.** A for-profit developer builds a **5 MW AC** commercial solar project. Eligible basis is **$8,000,000**; the project qualifies for a **30% §48E ITC** (prevailing-wage/apprenticeship met; no adders modeled). The developer is a growth-stage company with little federal tax liability, so it cannot use the credit itself and is **not** eligible for direct pay.

```
Eligible basis                         $8,000,000
Base ITC rate (§48E)                    x    30%
--------------------------------------------------
Credit generated                       $2,400,000
```

**The sale.** The developer registers the property in the IRS **ECO portal**, obtains a registration number, and sells the entire $2,400,000 credit to a profitable manufacturer at a market price of **$0.93 per dollar** of credit, under a transfer agreement with a seller **recapture indemnity** and **tax insurance**.

```
Credit face value                      $2,400,000
Transfer price (93 cents/$)             x   0.93
--------------------------------------------------
Cash to seller (tax-free)              $2,232,000
Discount retained by buyer             $  168,000
```

**Result for each side:**

- **Seller:** receives **$2,232,000 in cash, tax-free**, without partnership structuring — usable immediately for the next project.
- **Buyer (transferee):** applies the full **$2,400,000 credit** against its federal tax, having paid **$2,232,000** (non-deductible). Its **$168,000** spread is the economic return, in exchange for taking recapture risk for the 5-year period (mitigated by indemnity and insurance).

The buyer must **not** be a prohibited foreign entity, and the transfer must occur only **once**. Compare this to a nonprofit owner, which would instead use [Direct Pay](incentive-direct-pay.md) and collect the full **$2,400,000** from the IRS with no buyer discount.
