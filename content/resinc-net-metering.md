# Residential Net Metering & Net Billing

For a homeowner with rooftop solar, the way your utility **compensates the energy you export** to the grid is often the **single largest ongoing driver of savings** — frequently more important over 20 years than any one-time rebate. This page explains net metering, the shift to net billing, California's NEM 3.0, true-ups, non-bypassable charges, grandfathering, and how all of it shapes home-solar payback.

> **The big trend (verified June 2026):** Traditional **full-retail net metering is being scaled back** across much of the country in favor of **net billing** tariffs that pay **less than retail** for exported energy. According to the NC Clean Energy Technology Center's *50 States of Solar* tracking, roughly **one-third of states** are now offering alternatives to, or significantly revising, traditional net metering. Rules vary enormously by state and utility — always confirm yours on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/) and with your utility.

## How Net Metering Credits Work

**Net metering (NEM)** lets your meter effectively run backward. When your panels produce more than your home is using, the excess flows to the grid and you receive a **credit**; when you draw from the grid (at night, on cloudy days), you use up those credits.

Under **full-retail net metering**, each exported kilowatt-hour is credited at **the same retail rate** you pay for imported energy. That 1:1 relationship is what makes net metering so valuable — a kWh you export in the afternoon offsets a kWh you import in the evening, dollar for dollar.

```
  monthly bill = (kWh imported - kWh exported) x retail rate
                 + fixed charges + non-bypassable charges
```

> **Self-consumption is always worth the most.** Energy your home uses **the instant it's produced** avoids the retail rate entirely and never touches the export-credit question. This is why load timing (running the dishwasher, EV charger, or pool pump midday) and batteries matter so much under weaker export rates.

## Net Billing vs. Full-Retail Net Metering

The industry is moving from **net metering** toward **net billing**, where exports are credited at a **lower "avoided-cost" or export rate** rather than full retail.

| Feature | **Full-retail net metering** | **Net billing** |
|---|---|---|
| Export credit rate | At/near **full retail** | **Avoided cost** (often well below retail) |
| Value of self-consumption | High | High (relatively even more important) |
| Value of a battery | Lower | **Higher** — store midday, use/export at peak |
| National direction | Being phased down | The **growing** model |
| Payback sensitivity | Less sensitive to timing | Very sensitive to timing and storage |

Under net billing, the gap between the **retail rate you pay** and the **export rate you receive** means every exported kWh is "worth less" than a kWh you consume directly. That reshapes system design: homeowners increasingly **right-size** the array to their own consumption and **add storage** rather than oversizing for export.

## California NEM 3.0 (Net Billing Tariff)

California is the clearest example of the net-billing shift.

> **NEM 3.0 / Net Billing Tariff (verified June 2026):** California's investor-owned utilities (PG&E, SCE, SDG&E) operate under the **Net Billing Tariff (NBT)**, effective **April 15, 2023**. Exports are valued using the state's **Avoided Cost Calculator (ACC)** — a schedule of export rates that vary by **month, hour, and weekday/weekend**. Compared with the old NEM 2.0 full-retail crediting, average export compensation fell **roughly 75%**. A California court **upheld NEM 3.0 in March 2026.** Sources: [CPUC](https://www.cpuc.ca.gov/), [Lawrence Berkeley National Lab NEM 3.0 study](https://eta.lbl.gov/publications/one-year-tracking-impacts-nem-30).

The practical result in California has been a **surge in battery attachment** — from roughly 11% of new residential solar to **over half** by 2024 — because storing cheap midday production for expensive evening hours is now central to the economics. See [Battery Storage](battery-storage.md) and [Residential Battery Backup](residential-battery-backup.md).

## True-Ups: Monthly vs. Annual Settlement

Net-metering and net-billing programs differ in **how often** credits are reconciled against charges.

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **True-up** | The periodic settlement where accumulated export credits are netted against charges |
| **Monthly netting** | Credits and charges reconciled each month; leftover credits may roll or be paid at a low rate |
| **Annual true-up** | A once-a-year reconciliation (common in California NBT); credits banked across seasons offset winter usage |
| **Netting interval** | How finely production and consumption are matched — from monthly down to **15-minute or instantaneous** intervals (finer intervals reduce credit value) |

> **Netting intervals are getting shorter.** Several utilities have moved to **hourly, 15-minute, or instantaneous** netting (e.g., Nevada, West Virginia). Shorter intervals mean brief production spikes that once offset later usage now export at the low rate instead — another factor pushing homeowners toward storage.

## Non-Bypassable Charges and Fixed Fees

Even with generous export credits, most tariffs include charges you **cannot offset** with solar:

- **Non-bypassable charges (NBCs):** Per-kWh fees (for public-purpose programs, wildfire funds, etc.) applied to imported energy regardless of solar. In California these run a few cents per kWh.
- **Fixed monthly charges / minimum bills:** Flat customer charges that solar cannot erase.
- **Demand charges (residential):** Less common but spreading — a charge based on your **peak** kW draw in a period, which solar alone may not reduce without storage.

> The *50 States of Solar* report found dozens of proposed **residential fixed-charge or minimum-bill increases** in recent quarters — a quieter but real erosion of solar savings. Read your tariff's fixed-charge section, not just the export rate.

## Grandfathering

When a utility switches to a less favorable successor tariff, existing solar customers are usually **grandfathered** onto the old rules for a defined period.

- California **NEM 2.0** customers were generally grandfathered for **~20 years** from their interconnection date, provided they applied before the NEM 3.0 cutoff.
- Grandfathering terms (length, whether it survives a home sale, whether adding panels triggers the new tariff) **vary by state and utility.**

> **Check before you expand or move.** Adding capacity, changing meters, or in some cases selling the home can **forfeit** grandfathered status and move the system onto the current (usually weaker) tariff. Confirm the rules before making changes.

## How Export Rules Drive Payback — Examples

**Example 1 — Full-retail net metering (retail = $0.16/kWh):**

```
  10,000 kWh/yr produced, 4,000 kWh self-consumed, 6,000 kWh exported
  Self-consumption saved: 4,000 x $0.16 = $640
  Export credit:          6,000 x $0.16 = $960
  Annual bill benefit ...................... ~$1,600
```

**Example 2 — Net billing (retail = $0.16/kWh, export = $0.05/kWh):**

```
  Same 10,000 kWh, same 4,000 self-consumed, 6,000 exported
  Self-consumption saved: 4,000 x $0.16 = $640
  Export credit:          6,000 x $0.05 = $300
  Annual bill benefit ...................... ~$940
```

**Example 3 — Net billing + battery (shift 3,000 exported kWh to self-use):**

```
  Now 7,000 kWh self-consumed, 3,000 kWh exported
  Self-consumption saved: 7,000 x $0.16 = $1,120
  Export credit:          3,000 x $0.05 = $150
  Annual bill benefit ...................... ~$1,270
```

The battery recovers much of the value net billing removed — which is exactly why storage attachment has climbed in net-billing states. Combine this with any [State Residential Tax Credits & Rebates](resinc-state.md) and [Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md); note the [federal §25D credit ended for post-2025 homeowner-owned systems](resinc-federal.md). For households without a suitable roof, [Community Solar for Households](residential-community-solar.md) is an alternative.
