# Construction & Site Safety

Ground-mount and utility-scale solar construction is heavy civil work as much as it is electrical work. Before a single module is bolted down, crews drive thousands of steel piles, dig miles of cable trench, swing loads with cranes and telehandlers, and move earthmoving equipment across active work zones — often in extreme heat and open, exposed terrain with no natural shade. The dominant fatal hazards on these sites are **struck-by**, **caught-between**, **trenching cave-ins**, **crane/rigging failures**, and **heat illness**, not electric shock. This article covers the site-safety disciplines that keep a solar construction workforce alive, anchored in OSHA's Construction standards (29 CFR 1926).

For program context see the [Solar Safety Overview](safe-overview.md). Energized-work hazards are covered under [Electrical Safety & Arc Flash](safe-electrical.md); elevated work (racking, trackers, structures) under [Working at Heights & Fall Protection](safe-falls.md); and incident response under [Emergency Response & First Aid](safe-emergency.md).

> **Warning:** Struck-by and caught-between hazards — the "Focus Four" mobile-equipment and trench dangers — kill more solar construction workers than electricity. Never let familiarity with a routine task lower your guard around moving equipment and open excavations.

## Heavy Equipment and Pile Drivers

Utility solar depends on **pile-driving rigs** that hammer or vibrate steel piles into the ground at high production rates, plus excavators, dozers, graders, telehandlers, water trucks, and utility vehicles constantly circulating the site.

### Controlling Struck-By and Caught-Between Hazards

- **Establish and enforce exclusion zones** around operating pile drivers and swing radii. No one enters the drop/impact zone of a pile hammer.
- **Positive communication:** Spotters, hand signals, and radios for all blind-side movement. Make eye contact and get acknowledgment before approaching operating equipment.
- **High-visibility apparel:** Class 2 or Class 3 hi-vis is standard on active earthwork sites.
- **Backing and internal traffic:** Back-up alarms, cameras, and spotters; minimize backing by planning circulation and using pull-through routes.
- **Pinch points:** Keep hands, feet, and bodies out of the space between piles, racking, and the machine — caught-between injuries happen in an instant during alignment.
- **Suspended piles:** Never position yourself under a suspended or partially driven pile.

> **Warning:** Operators cannot see everything. Assume you are invisible to an equipment operator until they signal that they see you. Pile-driver leads and hammers are massive suspended loads — a dropped or misaligned pile is a fatal struck-by hazard.

## Trenching and Excavation (OSHA Subpart P)

Cable trenching for collection circuits is routine on solar sites, and trench collapse is one of the deadliest hazards in all of construction. **OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart P** governs excavation safety.

### Core Requirements

- **Protective systems at 5 feet:** A trench **5 feet (1.5 m) deep or greater** requires a protective system — **sloping, benching, shoring, or shielding (trench box)** — unless the excavation is made entirely in stable rock.
- **Under 5 feet:** A competent person may waive a protective system only if inspection shows no indication of potential cave-in.
- **Competent person:** A designated **competent person** must classify the soil, select the protective system, and inspect the excavation daily (and after every rainstorm or hazard-increasing event) with the authority to stop work and remove workers.
- **Soil classification:** Soils are classified (Stable Rock, Type A, Type B, Type C) using field methods such as visual and manual tests, a pocket penetrometer, or thumb penetration, per Subpart P Appendix A.
- **Spoil piles:** Keep excavated soil and materials at least **2 feet (0.6 m)** from the edge of the trench.
- **Access/egress:** For trenches 4 feet or deeper, provide a ladder, ramp, or steps within **25 feet (7.6 m)** of every worker.
- **Atmosphere and water:** Test for hazardous atmospheres where they may exist; control accumulating water; never work under a trench that is not adequately protected.
- **Underground utilities:** Locate and mark utilities (call 811) before digging; support or protect exposed utilities.

| Trench depth | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Less than 5 ft | Protective system at competent person's discretion (unless cave-in indicators present) |
| 5 ft and deeper | Protective system required unless in stable rock |
| Deeper than 20 ft | Protective system must be designed by a registered professional engineer |
| 4 ft and deeper | Means of egress within 25 ft of workers |

> **Warning:** A cubic yard of soil weighs roughly 3,000 lb (about a small car). Cave-ins bury and crush workers in seconds, with no time to react. Never enter an unprotected trench 5 feet or deeper — no "quick" exception is worth a life.

## Cranes and Rigging (OSHA Subpart CC)

Cranes set transformers, inverter skids, tracker components, and pre-assembled structures. **OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC** governs cranes and derricks in construction.

- **Qualified/certified operators:** Crane operators must be certified per Subpart CC; riggers and signal persons must be qualified.
- **Ground conditions and setup:** Assess ground bearing capacity, use adequate mats/outriggers, and level the crane. Poor ground and inadequate cribbing cause tip-overs.
- **Lift planning:** Confirm load weight, radius, and rated capacity from the load chart; plan critical lifts in writing.
- **Power lines:** Maintain OSHA clearance distances from energized power lines; use a dedicated spotter/proximity plan.
- **Rigging inspection:** Inspect slings, shackles, and hardware before each use; rig to the load's center of gravity; use tag lines to control loads.
- **Exclusion zones:** Keep workers out from under suspended loads and out of the swing radius of the rotating superstructure.

> **Warning:** Environmental conditions can stop crane work. High wind, poor visibility, and lightning are grounds to halt lifts. Do not exceed the manufacturer's wind-speed limits for the load and configuration.

## Heat Illness Prevention

Solar sites are open, treeless, and reflective — a near-worst case for heat exposure. Heat illness ranges from heat rash and cramps to **heat exhaustion** and **heat stroke**, which is a life-threatening medical emergency. OSHA enforces heat hazards under the **General Duty Clause** and runs a **Heat National Emphasis Program (NEP)**; a federal heat standard has been proposed. Some states (e.g., California, under Cal/OSHA) have enforceable heat-illness rules.

### Water, Rest, Shade — and Acclimatization

- **Water:** Provide cool drinking water and encourage frequent small drinks (roughly one cup every 15–20 minutes during heat).
- **Rest:** Schedule work/rest cycles that lengthen as the heat index rises; adjust the pace during heat waves.
- **Shade:** Provide accessible shade for breaks and for recovering workers — critical on shadeless solar fields, so bring engineered shade structures.
- **Acclimatization:** New and returning workers must be gradually built up over **7–14 days**. A large share of heat fatalities occur in a worker's first few days on the job. Follow a "20% rule" style ramp-up for new workers.
- **Monitoring:** Use a buddy system, watch for symptoms, and have a plan to cool and get help fast. See [Emergency Response & First Aid](safe-emergency.md) for treatment.

| Heat index | General response (adjust to your written plan) |
|---|---|
| 80–90°F (Caution) | Basic controls: water, rest, shade, acclimatization |
| 91–103°F (Extreme Caution) | Increase breaks, active monitoring, remind on hydration |
| 103–115°F (Danger) | Frequent rest, reschedule strenuous work, close supervision |
| 115°F+ (Extreme Danger) | Reschedule non-essential work; heightened emergency readiness |

> **Warning:** Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Confusion, slurred speech, collapse, or hot/dry (or heavily sweating) skin means call 911, begin aggressive cooling immediately (cold water/ice), and do not delay. It can be fatal within minutes.

## Weather and Lightning Stand-Down

Open sites offer no protection from lightning, and metal racking, piles, and cranes are attractive strike points.

- **Lightning:** Follow the **"30-30 rule"** — if thunder follows lightning by 30 seconds or less, take shelter; wait **30 minutes** after the last thunder/lightning before resuming. Suspend elevated and crane work first.
- **Shelter:** Enclosed vehicles or substantial buildings — not equipment cabs assumed to be safe by default, and never open structures or under isolated objects.
- **High wind:** Stop crane and elevated work per equipment wind limits; secure loose materials and modules.
- **Extreme cold/ice:** Manage slip and cold-stress hazards; de-ice access ways.
- **Stand-down authority:** Give supervisors and workers clear authority to stop work for weather without penalty.

## Site Traffic Management

- **Separate pedestrians from equipment** with barriers, designated walkways, and controlled crossings.
- **Speed limits and haul routes** posted and enforced; dust control on roads to preserve visibility.
- **Flaggers and signage** at public-road interfaces and internal intersections; follow the applicable temporary traffic control (MUTCD) practices.
- **Parking and laydown** separated from active work areas.

## Housekeeping and Struck-By / Caught-Between

Good housekeeping directly reduces trips, punctures, fires, and struck-by events.

- **Clear travel paths** of debris, offcuts, banding, and packaging.
- **Manage materials:** Stack and band module pallets and racking so they cannot topple; secure against wind.
- **Control sharps and protruding rebar/pile ends** with caps and guards.
- **Nail/screw discipline** and immediate cleanup of fasteners and offcuts.
- **Waste and combustibles** removed promptly, especially near operations that could ignite them.

> **Safety Disclaimer:** This article is a general educational reference, not a substitute for OSHA standards, manufacturer instructions, or qualified professional judgment. OSHA construction standards (29 CFR 1926), state-plan requirements (e.g., Cal/OSHA), and their interpretations change over time — always work to the current standards and any stricter state or local rules. Trenching, crane, pile-driving, and heat hazards can be fatal within seconds and require competent/qualified persons, engineered protective systems, and site-specific plans. Nothing here authorizes any specific work method. When lives or compliance are at stake, consult your safety professional, a competent person, and, where required, a registered professional engineer.
