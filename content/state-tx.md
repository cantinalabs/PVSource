# Texas Solar Incentives & Policy

Texas has the nation's fastest-growing solar market by installed capacity, but its **deregulated, utility-by-utility structure** means solar policy looks very different from California or the Northeast. This guide covers the current landscape for **residential and commercial** systems, with emphasis on the competitive retail market and where to verify time-sensitive values.

> **Educational reference only — not tax or legal advice.** Programs change frequently. Verify every value below with the relevant agency, your utility or Retail Electric Provider, and [DSIRE](https://programs.dsireusa.org/) before making decisions.

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Policy / Incentive | Status | Notes |
|--------------------|--------|-------|
| Statewide net metering | No | **Utility/REP-specific "solar buyback"** plans instead |
| SREC market / RPS solar carve-out | No SREC market | RPS met; no distributed-solar carve-out |
| State personal income-tax credit | No | Texas has **no state income tax** |
| State corporate income-tax credit | No | No state income tax (franchise tax only) |
| Federal residential credit (§25D) | Ended | Expired after Dec 31, 2025 (OBBBA) |
| Federal commercial ITC (§48/§48E) | Yes | See federal pages; phase-out schedule applies |
| Utility rebates / storage incentives | Yes (local) | Austin Energy, Oncor, CPS Energy, others |
| Sales-tax exemption | Varies | No blanket statewide solar exemption; see below |
| Property-tax exemption | Yes — **§11.27** | 100% exemption of added value; file Form 50-123 |
| Competitive retail electricity market | Yes (ERCOT) | Most Texans choose a Retail Electric Provider |

## Overview

Texas has an outstanding solar resource — roughly **5.0 to 6.0 peak sun-hours per day**, with West Texas and the Panhandle among the sunniest regions in the country. The state leads the U.S. in new utility-scale solar and battery additions, and residential adoption is rising fast.

The defining feature of Texas is its **electricity market structure**:

- Most of the state is served by the **ERCOT** grid and operates under a **deregulated, competitive retail market**. In these areas, customers choose a **Retail Electric Provider (REP)** for their energy supply, while a separate **Transmission & Distribution Utility (TDU)** — e.g., **Oncor, CenterPoint, AEP Texas, TNMP** — owns the wires and handles interconnection.
- **Municipal utilities** (Austin Energy, CPS Energy in San Antonio) and **electric cooperatives** are **not** part of the competitive market and set their own solar rules and rebates.
- Areas outside ERCOT (parts of East, West, and the Panhandle served by SPP/other RTOs) may be traditionally regulated.

Key regulators: the **Public Utility Commission of Texas (PUCT)** oversees the retail market and TDUs; ERCOT operates the grid.

> **Key point:** In Texas, **the deal you get for solar exports depends on your specific REP or municipal utility — not a statewide rule.** Identify the utility/REP first.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

**Texas has no statewide net-metering mandate.** There is no state law requiring utilities or REPs to credit solar exports at retail rate.

Instead:

- In **deregulated ERCOT areas**, individual **REPs offer "solar buyback" plans** that credit exported energy — terms vary widely (full retail-equivalent 1:1 credit on some plans, wholesale-ish or capped credits on others, sometimes only up to your consumption). Shopping REPs is essential.
- **Municipal utilities and co-ops** set their own policies:
  - **Austin Energy** uses a **Value of Solar (VoS) tariff** — all solar production (gross generation, not just exports) is credited at a Value-of-Solar rate (**~9.91¢/kWh**; reviewed roughly every 3 years — verify current value), rather than netting against consumption. Credits roll over monthly but cannot be cashed out.
  - **CPS Energy** (San Antonio) and various co-ops have their own net-metering-style or buyback arrangements.

For how buyback and net-billing mechanics compare, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

> **Key point:** **Compare REP solar-buyback plans carefully.** Two customers on the same street can get very different export credit depending on which plan they choose.

## SREC / RECs

Texas has a **Renewables Portfolio Standard** that was met years ahead of schedule (largely by wind), and it **does not operate a tradable SREC market or a distributed-solar carve-out.**

- **No homeowner-sellable SRECs** in Texas.
- Utility-scale RECs are traded for compliance and voluntary green-power purposes, but residential rooftop systems do not generate a retail SREC income stream.

For how SREC markets function in states that have them, see [SREC Markets Deep-Dive](incentive-srec-markets.md).

> **Key point:** **No SREC income in Texas.** Value comes from bill offset, buyback plans, and local rebates.

## State Tax Credits

**Texas has no state income tax**, so there is **no state personal or corporate income-tax credit for solar** — and none is needed at the income-tax level.

- **Personal:** no state income tax; no solar income-tax credit.
- **Corporate:** businesses pay a **franchise (margin) tax**, not a corporate income tax; there is no dedicated solar income-tax credit, though the property-tax exemption below applies to business property.

Texas solar value instead comes from the **§11.27 property-tax exemption, local/utility rebates, avoided energy costs**, and (for businesses) the **federal ITC and depreciation**. See [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md).

> Verify at [DSIRE Texas](https://programs.dsireusa.org/system/program?state=TX).

---

## Federal Tax Credits

- **Residential (§25D Residential Clean Energy Credit):** **ended after December 31, 2025** under OBBBA. Homeowner purchases placed in service in 2026 or later cannot claim the 30% residential credit. See [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md).
- **Commercial (§48 / §48E ITC and MACRS depreciation):** remains available in 2026, subject to OBBBA's phase-out timeline and begin-construction / placed-in-service deadlines. Domestic-content, energy-community, and low-income adders may apply. See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

> Federal rules changed materially under OBBBA and are date-sensitive. Confirm with a tax professional and the [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/).

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

Because Texas is decentralized, **the best solar rebates are local**, offered by municipal utilities, co-ops, and TDUs:

| Provider | Program (verify current) | Notes |
|----------|--------------------------|-------|
| **Austin Energy** | Residential rebate + Capacity/Performance commercial incentives + Value of Solar tariff | See detail below; Austin territory only |
| **Oncor** (TDU) | Residential/commercial solar + storage incentive | Battery pairing often required; annual budget window; amount varies |
| **CPS Energy** (San Antonio) | Solar and battery rebates | Program terms change annually |
| **CenterPoint / AEP / TNMP** | Varies | Some energy-efficiency/DR programs |

Many programs are **budget-limited, seasonal, and change year to year** — several run only during defined enrollment windows.

### Austin Energy — the details

Austin Energy (municipal utility) has the state's most structured solar-incentive program. **All amounts below are the new rates effective July 1, 2026** — anyone who has not yet installed is eligible at the new rates.

**Residential rebate**

| System size | Previous | As of July 1, 2026 |
|-------------|----------|--------------------|
| **> 3 kW** (per project) | $2,500 | **$4,000** |

To earn the residential rebate you must: **complete Austin Energy's free Solar Education Course**, use an **Austin Energy Participating Contractor**, install a **new system ≥ 3 kW (DC)** that is largely unshaded, and receive a **Solar Rebate Confirmation Letter *before* installation** (systems already installed are not eligible).

**Commercial — pick ONE of two options** (eligibility is set by **kW-AC ≈ 83% of the kW-DC** system rating):

*Capacity-Based Incentive (CBI) — one-time payment, per watt-DC*

| Customer / size | Previous | As of July 1, 2026 |
|-----------------|----------|--------------------|
| **Nonprofit, < 200 kW** | $0.60 / W | **$1.00 / W** |
| **For-profit business, < 100 kW** | $0.50 / W | **$0.70 / W** |

*Performance-Based Incentive (PBI) — paid on production for 5 years, per kWh*

| System size | Previous | As of July 1, 2026 |
|-------------|----------|--------------------|
| **< 400 kW** | $0.08 / kWh | **$0.10 / kWh** |
| **400 – 999 kW** | $0.06 / kWh | **$0.08 / kWh** |
| **≥ 1,000 kW (1 MW)** | $0.04 / kWh | **$0.06 / kWh** |

CBI rebates are **capped** (historically ~$168,675 for nonprofits and ~$60,240 for businesses < 100 kW — verify the current cap under the new rates). Commercial projects go through a **reservation process**: the contractor applies, Austin Energy issues an **Encumbrance Letter** then a **Letter of Intent (LOI)**; Austin Energy makes **no financial commitment until the LOI is issued**, and if the project isn't finished before the LOI expires you must reapply at the then-current rate. **Funding is first-come, first-served.** Nonprofits must submit 501(c)(3)/(4) documentation.

**Value of Solar (VoS) tariff** — Austin Energy uses **VoS instead of net metering**. Every solar system is automatically credited at **~9.91¢/kWh for *all* energy it produces** (gross production, not just exports). The rate is **reviewed every ~3 years**, credits **roll over** month to month against electric charges only, and **excess credits cannot be cashed out** (don't oversize).

**Batteries:** there's no upfront battery rebate, but battery owners can earn rewards through Austin Energy's **Power Partner** demand-response (virtual-power-plant) program. **Note:** third-party solar **PPAs are not allowed** in Austin Energy's territory.

> **Key point:** **Check your specific utility's live rebate page.** Amounts, contractor requirements, and enrollment windows change frequently, and storage rebates increasingly require batteries paired with solar.

For storage design, see [Battery Storage](battery-storage.md); for the residential-side view of these programs, see [Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md).

## Sales Tax Exemption

Texas does **not** offer a clean, blanket statewide sales-tax exemption for all residential solar equipment the way Florida does.

- There are **franchise-tax deductions/exemptions** relating to solar energy devices for businesses, and some equipment may qualify for specific exemptions, but a typical residential rooftop purchase should assume **standard state and local sales tax generally applies** to equipment unless a specific exemption is confirmed.
- Because Texas sales-tax treatment of solar is nuanced, **do not assume equipment is tax-free.**

> Verify applicability with the [Texas Comptroller](https://comptroller.texas.gov/) and [DSIRE](https://programs.dsireusa.org/).

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

Texas offers a strong, well-established **property-tax exemption under Texas Tax Code §11.27**.

- **The added value of a solar or wind-powered energy device is 100% exempt from property tax.** Installing solar does not increase your assessed value or property-tax bill for the value the system adds.
- **Residential:** file **Comptroller Form 50-123** ("Exemption Application for Solar or Wind-Powered Energy Devices") with your **county appraisal district** — commonly due by the statutory deadline (often around **April 30**). **The exemption is not fully automatic; the application must be filed.**
- The exemption applies to both residential and, in applicable cases, business/commercial solar property.

> **Key point:** The §11.27 exemption is valuable and permanent, but **you must file Form 50-123 with your county appraisal district.** Confirm the current deadline and process with your appraisal district and the [Texas Comptroller](https://comptroller.texas.gov/).

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- Texas has a **large, fragmented AHJ landscape** — many independent cities, counties, and jurisdictions, each with its own permitting process. Some jurisdictions have limited building-code enforcement in unincorporated areas.
- **Interconnection** in ERCOT areas is handled by the **TDU** (Oncor, CenterPoint, AEP Texas, TNMP), each with its own distributed-generation interconnection process and timelines; municipal utilities and co-ops handle their own.
- Adoption of **SolarAPP+** automated residential permitting is growing in Texas but is not universal — availability depends on the specific city.
- Timelines vary widely: some fast-permitting cities approve quickly, while others and TDU interconnection can add weeks.

See [Residential Permitting](residential-permitting.md) and [Commercial Permitting](commercial-permitting.md).

---

## Bottom Line

Texas is a booming solar market with an excellent resource and a strong, permanent **§11.27 property-tax exemption**, but it has **no statewide net metering, no state income-tax credit, and no SREC market** — so export value depends entirely on your **REP's solar-buyback plan or your municipal utility's tariff**, and the best cash rebates are **local (Austin Energy, Oncor, CPS Energy, and others)**. Because the competitive retail market and local rebate programs change constantly, **compare REP plans and verify current rebates with your specific utility, the Texas Comptroller, and DSIRE before committing.**
