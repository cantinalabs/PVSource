# Residential Federal Solar Tax Credit (§25D)

For most of the past two decades, the single largest incentive for a U.S. homeowner going solar was the **federal residential tax credit** under **Internal Revenue Code Section 25D** — the **Residential Clean Energy Credit**. This page explains what that credit covered, how homeowners claimed it, and — most importantly for anyone reading this in 2026 — the **early sunset** enacted by the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA / H.R.1)** and what federal value, if any, still exists for homeowners.

> **Most important fact (verified June 30, 2026):** The **Section 25D Residential Clean Energy Credit (30%)** was **terminated early** by the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, H.R.1, signed July 4, 2025)**. Under the amended statute, **25D does not apply to expenditures made after December 31, 2025** — and an "expenditure" is treated as made **when the original installation is completed**. In plain terms: a homeowner-owned system **placed in service after Dec 31, 2025 generally earns no federal residential credit.** Verify your situation with the [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/residential-clean-energy-credit) and a tax professional.

## What §25D Covered (Through 2025)

Section 25D provided a **nonrefundable tax credit equal to 30%** of the cost of qualified residential clean-energy property, with **no overall dollar cap** for solar. It applied to a taxpayer's **primary or secondary residence** (not rental property the owner doesn't live in) and covered both equipment and installation labor.

| Qualified property | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Solar electric (PV) panels** | Modules, inverters, racking, wiring, and installation labor |
| **Battery storage** | Standalone or paired batteries with **capacity ≥ 3 kWh** (a rule added by the Inflation Reduction Act, effective 2023) |
| **Solar water heating** | Must be certified (e.g., SRCC); pool/hot-tub heating excluded |
| **Geothermal heat pumps** | ENERGY STAR-qualified systems |
| **Small wind / fuel cells** | Fuel cells subject to a per-kW cap |
| **Sales tax, permitting, related costs** | Generally included in the credit basis |

Key characteristics while the credit was in force:

- **30% rate**, no cap for solar PV or battery (unlike the §25C efficiency credit, which has annual caps).
- **Nonrefundable** — it offsets federal income tax owed but is not paid out as a cash refund.
- **Carryforward** — any portion you could not use in the tax year could be carried forward to future years.
- **Ownership required** — only the **owner** of a purchased system (cash or loan) could claim it. Leased/PPA systems did not qualify the homeowner (see below).

## How Homeowners Claimed It — IRS Form 5695

A homeowner claimed §25D by filing **[IRS Form 5695, Residential Energy Credits](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-5695)** with their federal return for the tax year the installation was **completed (placed in service)**.

1. Enter qualified costs (equipment + labor + related soft costs) on Form 5695, Part I.
2. Multiply by **30%** to compute the credit.
3. Apply the credit against tax liability via Schedule 3 / Form 1040.
4. Carry any unused amount forward using the worksheet on future-year Form 5695.

> **Placed-in-service date controls.** The credit was earned in the year the system's **original installation was completed** — not when you signed a contract or made a deposit. This same rule is exactly what makes the OBBBA cutoff date so decisive.

## The OBBBA Sunset — Dated and Verified

Before OBBBA, §25D was scheduled to remain at 30% through 2032, then step down (26% in 2033, 22% in 2034) and expire after 2034. OBBBA replaced that long runway with a **hard early termination**.

| Item | Status (verified June 30, 2026) |
|---|---|
| Statute | **IRC §25D**, Residential Clean Energy Credit |
| Change | **OBBBA (H.R.1)** amended §25D(h): the credit **terminates for expenditures made after December 31, 2025** |
| "Expenditure made" | Treated as made **when the original installation is completed** — i.e., the **placed-in-service date** governs |
| Last qualifying date | Installation **completed on or before Dec 31, 2025** → claim on the 2025 return (filed in 2026) |
| After the cutoff | Installation **completed on/after Jan 1, 2026** → **no §25D credit** for a homeowner-owned system |
| Carryforward | Unused credit from a **qualifying pre-2026** installation **still carries forward** to later years |

> **Watch for misleading marketing.** Advertising that claims "you can still get the 30% homeowner credit in 2026" is generally **inaccurate for a purchased, homeowner-owned system.** The IRS has confirmed that completion after Dec 31, 2025 disqualifies the §25D credit. See the [IRS OBBBA FAQ](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/faqs-for-modification-of-sections-25c-25d-25e-30c-30d-45l-45w-and-179d-under-public-law-119-21-139-stat-72-july-4-2025-commonly-known-as-the-one-big-beautiful-bill-obbb).

## What Still Works for Homeowners After 2025

Federal tax value did not vanish entirely — but the **path** changed, and it usually no longer flows directly to the homeowner.

### 1. Third-party-owned systems (lease / PPA)

When a **third party owns** the system — a solar **lease** or a **Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)** — the *provider*, not the homeowner, can claim the **commercial** clean-electricity credit (now **§48E**, the technology-neutral Investment Tax Credit that replaced the old §48 ITC). The provider may then reflect part of that benefit in a **lower monthly lease or PPA price**.

- The homeowner does **not** claim any federal credit.
- Whether this actually saves you money depends entirely on the **contract terms** — escalators, buyout options, and term length.
- The commercial §48E has its **own** deadlines and requirements (broadly tied to beginning construction and placed-in-service timing under OBBBA); these are provider-side concerns but affect whether the deal pencils out. See [Homeowner Financing 101](residential-financing.md).

### 2. Carryforward of a prior-year §25D credit

If you **qualified** — that is, your system was completed on or before Dec 31, 2025 — and your tax liability was too small to absorb the full 30% in that year, the **unused credit carries forward** to future tax years. That carryforward survives the OBBBA repeal; only *new* expenditures are cut off.

### 3. State, utility, SREC, and net-metering value

None of these depend on §25D. See [State Residential Tax Credits & Rebates](resinc-state.md), [Utility Residential Rebates & Programs](resinc-utility.md), [Residential SRECs for Homeowners](resinc-srec.md), and [Residential Net Metering & Net Billing](resinc-net-metering.md). Look up your exact location on [DSIRE](https://www.dsireusa.org/).

## Worked Example — Before and After the Cutoff

Assume a **$24,000** homeowner-owned residential PV system (cash or loan).

**Scenario A — installation completed on or before Dec 31, 2025 (§25D applies):**

```
  System cost ........................ $24,000
  Section 25D credit (30%) ........... -$7,200   (nonrefundable)
  Net federal cost ................... $16,800

  - If 2025 federal tax liability < $7,200, the unused portion
    CARRIES FORWARD to later tax years.
  - State/utility rebates, SRECs, and net-metering savings STACK
    on top of this and are evaluated separately.
```

**Scenario B — same system, installation completed in 2026 (homeowner-owned):**

```
  System cost ........................ $24,000
  Section 25D credit ................. $0   (TERMINATED for expenditures
                                            made after 12/31/2025)
  Net federal cost ................... $24,000

  Possible federal value path: a LEASE/PPA, where the PROVIDER
  claims the commercial 48E credit and MAY reflect part of it in
  a lower monthly price. Homeowner claims NO federal credit.
```

> The §25D credit was always **nonrefundable** — it offset tax owed rather than generating a cash refund — but a qualifying pre-2026 system's unused amount **carries forward.** State credits, exemptions, SRECs, and net-metering/net-billing value are separate and additive. See the [Residential Incentives](residential-incentives.md) overview for how the full stack fits together.
