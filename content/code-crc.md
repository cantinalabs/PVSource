# California Residential Code (CRC) & Solar

The **California Residential Code (CRC)** is **Title 24, Part 2.5** of the California Code of Regulations. It is based on the **International Residential Code (IRC)** published by the ICC, then amended by California agencies (principally HCD and the Office of the State Fire Marshal). The CRC is the governing code for **detached one- and two-family dwellings and townhouses up to three stories** — the bulk of residential rooftop solar. It covers **residential rooftop-PV access, structural attachment, and roof requirements**, and it works alongside California's **residential solar (PV) mandate** in the Energy Code.

For the model-code framework, start with [Building Codes & Standards Explained](codes-standards-overview.md). Larger and non-residential buildings fall under the [California Building Code (CBC) & Solar](code-cbc.md); the electrical and fire sides are the [California Electrical Code (CEC) & Solar](code-cec.md) and [California Fire Code (CFC) & Solar](code-cfc.md).

> **The AHJ is authoritative.** The CRC is on a **triennial cycle**. The base IRC edition, referenced structural standards, section numbers, and California amendments change between cycles, and jurisdictions add local amendments. Confirm the edition in force before relying on any value below.

## How the CRC Relates to the IRC

The CRC adopts the corresponding **IRC** edition by reference and layers on California amendments. Residential rooftop **fire access/pathways/setbacks** are enforced through the fire code (CFC), but the CRC provides the residential structural and roof provisions, and it references the residential PV mandate and structural design expectations. Because a house may be reviewed under both the CRC and CFC, treat the two together.

| Title 24 (CRC) Cycle | Base IRC Edition | Published | Typical Effective Date |
|----------------------|------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| 2016 CRC | 2015 IRC | July 2016 | January 1, 2017 |
| 2019 CRC | 2018 IRC | July 2019 | January 1, 2020 |
| 2022 CRC | 2021 IRC | July 2022 | January 1, 2023 |
| 2025 CRC | 2024 IRC | July 2025 | January 1, 2026 |

## Residential Rooftop-PV Access, Pathways & Setbacks

Residential fire-access requirements are enforced primarily through the **California Fire Code (CFC)** (see [California Fire Code (CFC) & Solar](code-cfc.md) for the full treatment and citations). For quick reference, the established Group R-3 model is:

```
   RIDGE ---------------------------------------------------
        18 in setback (array <= 33% of roof plan area)
        36 in setback (array > 33% of roof plan area)
   -----------------------------------------------------------
   [ PV ARRAY ]     36 in pathway     [ PV ARRAY ]
   -----------------------------------------------------------
   >= 2 total 36-in pathways on separate roof planes,
      at least one on the street/driveway side
   EAVE / ROOF EDGE ----------------------------------------
```

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Pathways to ridge | Not fewer than **two 36-inch-wide pathways** on separate roof planes; at least one on the **street/driveway side** |
| Per-array pathway | At least **one 36-inch pathway** per roof plane bearing an array |
| Ridge setback (array ≤ 33%) | **18 inches** both sides of a horizontal ridge |
| Ridge setback (array > 33%) | **36 inches** both sides of a horizontal ridge |

> Exceptions and alternatives exist (roof geometry, non-combustible construction, module-level shutdown). Values are edition- and jurisdiction-specific — verify with the fire marshal.

## Structural Requirements

The CRC governs how rooftop PV attaches to residential roof framing. Key considerations:

- **Added dead load** on rafters/trusses (typically ~3–5 lb/ft² for framed roof-mount systems) must be supported by the existing structure.
- **Wind uplift** at the attachments — highest in **roof edge and corner zones** — drives fastener type, spacing, and pull-out capacity. California references **ASCE 7** wind provisions through the code.
- **Seismic** effects apply given California's high seismicity, though for attached (penetrating) residential systems the dead load is usually the controlling gravity concern.
- **Prescriptive vs. engineered:** Many jurisdictions accept **prescriptive attachment schedules** (for example, from expedited-permitting guidance) for standard framed roofs within defined limits; anything outside those limits — or heavier tile/ballast conditions — may require an engineered, PE-stamped design. Re-roofing interactions matter; see [Residential Re-roofing & Solar](residential-reroofing.md).

> **Truss and rafter capacity is the common residential structural check.** Verify member size, spacing, and condition; older or undersized framing, or added point loads at attachments, can require reinforcement.

## The California Residential Solar (PV) Mandate — Energy Code Cross-Reference

California is unique in **requiring solar PV on most new homes**. This mandate lives in the **Title 24, Part 6 Energy Code (California Energy Code)**, administered by the **California Energy Commission** — a separate document from the CRC (Part 2.5), even though both are Title 24 parts.

- **Effective January 1, 2020 (2019 Energy Code),** newly constructed low-rise residential buildings must include a **solar PV system** sized to the building's electricity needs, with defined exceptions (shading, small/steep roofs, alternative compliance).
- The **2022 Energy Code** extended PV/storage expectations and strengthened **solar-ready** and battery-storage provisions for additional building types.
- The **2025 Energy Code** (effective January 1, 2026) continues and refines these requirements.

> **Do not confuse the two Title 24 parts.** The **CRC (Part 2.5)** covers structural/roof/access. The **Energy Code (Part 6)** contains the **PV mandate, solar-ready, and battery-storage** requirements. A home must satisfy both. Confirm current sizing and exception rules with the California Energy Commission and your building department.

Solar-ready concepts in California parallel the **IECC solar-ready appendices** — see [IECC (Energy Code) & Solar](code-iecc.md). For the commercial mandate and broader Title 24 energy-code interactions, see [California Title 24 & Commercial Solar](california-title-24.md).

## Cycle Notes

### 2016 Cycle
Based on the **2015 IRC** (effective January 1, 2017). Predates the residential PV mandate; residential access/setback model per the 2016 CFC.

### 2019 Cycle
Based on the **2018 IRC** (effective January 1, 2020). Aligns with the **2019 Energy Code**, which introduced the **new-home solar PV mandate** (Part 6) effective January 1, 2020.

### 2022 Cycle
Based on the **2021 IRC** (effective January 1, 2023). Pairs with the **2022 Energy Code**, which expanded PV/storage and solar-ready provisions.

### 2025 Cycle
Based on the **2024 IRC** (published July 2025, effective January 1, 2026), paired with the **2025 Energy Code**. Newly effective — confirm local adoption and errata.
