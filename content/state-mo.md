# Missouri Solar Incentives & Policy

Missouri mandates **statewide net metering** for investor-owned utilities on systems **up to 100 kW**, and offers a **sales-tax exemption pathway** for solar equipment. However, two important caveats shape the current picture: the once-headline **utility solar rebates (Ameren/Evergy) have largely expired**, and the Missouri Supreme Court **struck down the residential solar property-tax exemption as unconstitutional in 2022**. Missouri has **no state income-tax credit** for solar.

This guide covers both **residential** and **commercial** solar. For core concepts, see [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md) and [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md).

## At-a-Glance Summary

| Incentive / Policy | Residential | Commercial | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Net metering** | Yes — IOUs, ≤ 100 kW | Yes — IOUs, ≤ 100 kW | Credit rate/rollover varies by utility — *verify* |
| **SREC / REC market** | No compliance SREC market | No compliance SREC market | RES met; RECs voluntary |
| **State income-tax credit** | None | None | Missouri offers no solar income-tax credit |
| **Federal tax credit** | §25D **ended after Dec 31, 2025** | §48/§48E ITC available | See [federal page](federal-tax-credits.md) |
| **Utility rebates (Ameren/Evergy)** | **Largely expired** | **Largely expired** | Ameren rebate closed after 2023 — *verify current status* |
| **Sales-tax exemption** | Exemption pathway exists | Exemption pathway exists | Aimed at systems producing electricity for resale/retail — *verify at MO DOR* |
| **Property-tax exemption** | **Struck down (2022)** | **Struck down (2022)**; Chapter 100 abatement possible | *Johnson v. Springfield Solar 1* — *verify* |

> **Bottom line up front:** Missouri's durable benefits are **net metering (≤100 kW)** and a **sales-tax exemption pathway**. The former **property-tax exemption was ruled unconstitutional in 2022**, and the well-known **Ameren rebate has expired**. There is no state income-tax credit.

## Overview

Missouri is served primarily by two large investor-owned utilities — **Ameren Missouri** (eastern/central, St. Louis area) and **Evergy** (western Missouri, Kansas City area) — plus municipal utilities and rural electric cooperatives. State solar policy for the IOUs is governed by the **Missouri Public Service Commission (PSC)** and statute (notably the Net Metering and Easy Connection Act and Missouri's Renewable Energy Standard, or RES).

Missouri's **Renewable Energy Standard** applies to IOUs but its solar targets have generally been met, so there is **no active SREC compliance market** driving ongoing demand. As a result, the practical economics of Missouri solar rest on net metering, the sales-tax exemption, the federal ITC (commercial), and system fundamentals.

## Net Metering / Net Billing

Missouri's **Net Metering and Easy Connection Act** requires **investor-owned utilities** (Ameren, Evergy) to offer net metering for customer systems **up to 100 kW**. Municipal utilities and cooperatives set their own policies — confirm with your provider.

**Compensation varies by utility and is set in each tariff:**

- **Evergy Missouri:** Has offered **one-to-one (full retail) net metering** for qualifying customers. *Verify current tariff.*
- **Ameren Missouri:** Credits **excess** generation at a lower, tariffed rate rather than full retail (historically seasonal avoided-cost-style values). *Verify current $/kWh values in Ameren's net-metering tariff.*
- **Credit rollover:** Net excess generation credits generally **expire after 12 months** if unused. Confirm the reconciliation month and rollover rules with your utility.

> Because credit rates and rollover treatment differ sharply between Ameren and Evergy, model your own utility's tariff. See [Net Metering, Net Billing & Rate Structures](grid-net-metering.md).

**Residential vs. commercial:**
- **Residential:** Systems are almost always under 100 kW and fully covered by the mandate; net metering plus self-consumption drives savings.
- **Commercial:** The **100 kW cap** is a real constraint. Systems **above 100 kW** fall outside the net-metering mandate and are handled under other tariffs/interconnection agreements — larger commercial projects should model export value, demand charges, and any parallel-generation rates explicitly.

## SREC / RECs

Missouri's RES created a compliance framework, but with targets generally satisfied there is **no robust ongoing SREC compliance market**. RECs from Missouri solar are largely **voluntary-market** instruments today.

- **Residential:** REC value is typically minimal absent a voluntary buyer.
- **Commercial:** Larger projects may transact **unbundled RECs** in voluntary markets, but revenue is market-dependent and should not be assumed. *Verify current market conditions.*

## State Tax Credits

**Missouri offers no state income-tax credit for solar** — residential or commercial. There is no state-level equivalent of the federal residential credit.

## Federal Tax Credits

- **Residential (§25D):** The residential clean-energy credit **ended after December 31, 2025** under the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**. Systems placed in service on or after **January 1, 2026** do **not** qualify for the 30% residential credit.
- **Commercial (§48 / §48E):** Businesses may still claim the **commercial Investment Tax Credit** under the technology-neutral **§48E** framework, subject to prevailing-wage/apprenticeship rules, bonus adders (domestic content, energy community, low-income), and OBBBA timing/phase-down provisions.

See [Commercial Solar Federal Tax Credits](federal-tax-credits.md) for current commercial ITC rules and deadlines. Confirm all dates against the IRS.

## Utility Incentives / Rebates

Missouri's utility rebates were once a national selling point, but they have **largely wound down**:

- **Ameren Missouri:** The Ameren Missouri solar rebate program **closed to new systems after December 31, 2023** (systems installed and inspected by that deadline were the last eligible). **No new-installation rebate is currently offered.** *Verify current status directly with Ameren.*
- **Evergy Missouri:** Historically ran periodic solar rebate/incentive programs; availability and funding vary and can sell out. *Verify current Evergy Missouri offerings.*

> **Verify current $/watt rebate availability directly with Ameren and Evergy** before counting on any rebate. Do not assume the historical figures still apply — they have changed.

## Sales Tax Exemption

Missouri provides a **sales-and-use-tax exemption pathway** for solar equipment under **RSMo Chapter 144** (e.g., §144.030). Important nuance:

- The exemption covers **solar photovoltaic energy systems, components, and materials/supplies used to construct or improve such systems**, but the statutory language and Department of Revenue rulings tie the exemption to systems that are **sold/leased to an end user or used to produce, collect, and transmit electricity for resale or retail sale.**
- In practice this is **most clearly applicable to commercial/production systems**; the applicability to a typical owner-occupied residential system is more nuanced and has been the subject of interpretation. *Verify current treatment and any documentation requirements with the Missouri Department of Revenue.*

## Property Tax Exemption / Abatement

**This is a key correction to older guides.** Missouri's former statutory property-tax exemption for "solar energy systems not held for resale" (RSMo 137.100(10)) was **ruled unconstitutional by the Missouri Supreme Court in 2022** (*Johnson v. Springfield Solar 1, LLC*). The Court held that the Missouri Constitution enumerates an exclusive list of permissible property-tax exemptions, and solar systems are not on it.

- **Effect:** Solar energy systems are **no longer categorically exempt** from Missouri property tax. Added value from a system **may be assessable** — confirm treatment with your **county assessor**, as local practice can vary.
- **Commercial alternative — Chapter 100:** Because the statutory exemption fell, developers have increasingly used **Chapter 100 bond financing** and local **enhanced-enterprise-zone**/abatement tools to negotiate property-tax relief on a project-by-project basis with local governments. These are **negotiated, local incentives**, not an automatic statewide exemption. *Verify current availability with local authorities and DSIRE.*

## Permitting & Interconnection Notes

- **Interconnection** with Ameren/Evergy follows the **Easy Connection Act** process and each utility's tariff; ≤100 kW systems use the net-metering interconnection path.
- **Municipal utilities and cooperatives** set their own interconnection and compensation rules — obtain their application packet early.
- **Permitting** is **local (city/county)**; requirements vary. Coordinate structural, electrical, and (for commercial) fire-access/setback requirements with the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ).
- Systems **above 100 kW** and larger commercial projects fall outside the net-metering mandate and require separate interconnection study/agreements — budget lead time accordingly.

## Bottom Line

- **Residential:** With §25D gone after 2025, the Missouri residential case leans on **net metering** (rate depends heavily on Ameren vs. Evergy) and the **sales-tax exemption pathway**. Do **not** assume the old property-tax exemption (struck down in 2022) or the expired Ameren rebate still apply. There is no state income-tax credit.
- **Commercial:** The **federal §48E ITC** is the primary driver. Watch the **100 kW net-metering cap**, pursue the **sales-tax exemption** where the system qualifies, and explore **Chapter 100 / local abatements** for property-tax relief since the statewide exemption is gone. Verify REC value before relying on it.
