# Building Codes & Standards Explained

Solar photovoltaic (PV) systems sit at the intersection of several regulatory worlds: structural building codes, fire codes, electrical codes, energy codes, and product-safety standards. A single rooftop array can be governed by a half-dozen different code documents at once, each published by a different organization and adopted on its own schedule. This page explains the **model-code system**, defines the acronyms solar professionals run into daily, and shows how a national model document becomes an enforceable local requirement.

> **The single most important concept:** Model codes are not law until a state or local jurisdiction *adopts* them — usually with amendments. The **Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)** is the final word on what applies to your project. Always confirm the exact edition and amendments in force locally before relying on any section number cited anywhere, including here.

For project-specific detail, see the [Residential Solar Codes & Fire Requirements](./residential-codes.md) and [Commercial Solar Codes & Fire Requirements](./commercial-codes.md) pages.

## What Is a "Model Code"?

A **model code** is a complete, ready-to-adopt regulatory document written by a standards-development organization (SDO) but with **no legal force of its own**. States, counties, and municipalities adopt model codes by reference into their own statutes and ordinances. This division of labor lets a national body of experts maintain technically rigorous, regularly updated documents while letting each jurisdiction tailor them to local conditions (wind, snow, seismicity, wildfire, climate, utility practice).

The two SDOs that matter most for solar are:

- The **International Code Council (ICC)**, which publishes the family of **I-Codes** (IBC, IRC, IFC, IECC, and others) on a three-year cycle.
- The **National Fire Protection Association (NFPA)**, which publishes the **National Electrical Code (NEC / NFPA 70)**, **NFPA 1 (Fire Code)**, and **NFPA 855 (Energy Storage Systems)**, among hundreds of others.

## The ICC I-Code Family

The ICC publishes a coordinated set of codes. The ones a solar professional must know:

| Code | Full name | What it governs | Buildings it applies to |
|------|-----------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| **IBC** | International Building Code | Structural design, fire-resistance, means of egress, roof loads, general building construction | Commercial and most multifamily (3+ units) and other non-IRC structures |
| **IRC** | International Residential Code | Standalone, self-contained code for construction of houses | One- and two-family dwellings and townhouses ≤ 3 stories |
| **IFC** | International Fire Code | Fire prevention, firefighter access, hazardous materials, rooftop PV access pathways and setbacks, ESS | All occupancies (works alongside IBC/IRC) |
| **IECC** | International Energy Conservation Code | Energy efficiency of the building envelope and systems | Residential and commercial (separate chapters) |

A few clarifying points:

- The **IRC is a standalone code** — for a typical house it covers structure, fire, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, and energy in one book, drawing on the other I-Codes for substance. PV provisions live in **IRC Section R324** (and energy-system provisions in the IRC electrical chapters reference the NEC).
- The **IBC does not contain electrical provisions of its own** — it (and the IRC) point to the **NEC** for electrical work.
- The **IFC and IBC are coordinated**: rooftop PV access and pathway rules appear in **IFC Chapter 12 (Energy Systems)** and are cross-referenced in IBC and IRC.

## The NFPA Codes

| Code | Full name | Solar relevance |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| **NEC / NFPA 70** | National Electrical Code | The electrical bible for PV: **Article 690** (Solar PV Systems), **Article 705** (Interconnected Power Production Sources), **Article 691** (Large-Scale PV ≥ 5 MW), **Article 706** (Energy Storage Systems) |
| **NFPA 1** | Fire Code | NFPA's fire code, an alternative to the IFC adopted in some states (e.g., portions of the Northeast); contains PV and ESS access provisions paralleling IFC Chapter 12 |
| **NFPA 855** | Standard for the Installation of Stationary Energy Storage Systems | Separation, fire suppression, explosion control, and siting for batteries paired with PV |

## Decoding the Acronyms — Including California

National (ICC/NFPA) versus California (Title 24) versions of the same code:

| Acronym | Stands for | What it is |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| **IBC** | International Building Code | National model building code (commercial/multifamily) |
| **IRC** | International Residential Code | National model code for 1–2 family dwellings |
| **IFC** | International Fire Code | National model fire code |
| **IECC** | International Energy Conservation Code | National model energy code |
| **NEC** | National Electrical Code (NFPA 70) | National model electrical code |
| **CBC** | California Building Code | California's Title 24, **Part 2** — the IBC plus California amendments |
| **CRC** | California Residential Code | California's Title 24, **Part 2.5** — the IRC plus California amendments |
| **CFC** | California Fire Code | California's Title 24, **Part 9** — the IFC plus California amendments |
| **CEC** | California Electrical Code | California's Title 24, **Part 3** — the NEC plus California amendments |

> **Key insight for California:** CBC, CRC, CFC, and CEC are *not separate codes* — they are the national model codes (IBC, IRC, IFC, NEC) republished with California-specific amendments as parts of the **California Building Standards Code, Title 24, California Code of Regulations**. **Title 24, Part 6** is the separate California Energy Code (the analogue of the IECC), which drives California's residential PV mandate. Many other states do the same thing under different names (e.g., the Florida Building Code, the New York State codes).

## How the Adoption Chain Works

```
   MODEL CODE                STATE ADOPTION              LOCAL AMENDMENT             ENFORCEMENT
   (ICC / NFPA)        →      (legislature / state   →   (county / city            →  (AHJ:
   published on a            agency adopts edition,     adds stricter or              building official,
   3-year cycle              often with amendments)     climate-specific rules)       fire marshal,
                                                                                       electrical inspector)
```

1. **Model code published.** ICC publishes I-Codes every three years (…2018, 2021, 2024…). NFPA publishes the NEC every three years (…2017, 2020, 2023, 2026…).
2. **State adopts an edition** — sometimes the latest, often one or two cycles behind — usually with statewide amendments. Some states adopt statewide and prohibit local weakening; "home-rule" states let localities choose.
3. **Local jurisdiction amends** for local hazards (high wind, snow load, wildland-urban interface, seismic).
4. **The AHJ enforces.** Plan-checkers, building officials, fire marshals, and electrical inspectors interpret and apply the adopted code to your permit. Their interpretation, within the adopted text, governs.

> **Warning:** Two neighboring jurisdictions can enforce *different editions* of the same code, with *different amendments*. A design compliant in one city may fail plan check next door. Editions and section numbers shift between cycles — never assume a section number is stable across editions.

## Current Editions as of 2025–2026

The picture in mid-2026 is genuinely mixed; adoption always lags publication.

| Code | Latest published edition | Commonly enforced in 2025–2026 |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **NEC (NFPA 70)** | **2026** (published Aug 2025) | Mostly **2023**, with many states still on **2020** or **2017**; a first wave of states (e.g., CO, OR, WA, UT, MA, NH, VT, ID) moving to 2026 |
| **IBC / IRC / IFC** | **2024** | Predominantly **2021**, many on **2018**; 2024 adoptions ramping up through 2026–2027 |
| **IECC** | **2024** | Mostly **2021** / **2018**; energy code lags further |
| **California Title 24** (CBC/CRC/CFC/CEC + Energy Code) | **2025 edition** | **2025 edition effective January 1, 2026** statewide (based on the 2024 model-code cycle) |

> California's electrical code (Part 3) typically tracks the NEC one full cycle behind the national release because of its triennial schedule; confirm whether your project is under the 2025 Title 24 (NEC-2023-based) or a prior edition.

## Product-Safety Standards: UL and IEEE

Codes require equipment to be **listed and labeled** to recognized safety standards. These standards certify the *product*; the codes above govern the *installation*.

| Standard | Applies to | What it certifies |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **UL 61730** (and legacy **UL 1703**) | PV modules | Module construction and electrical/fire safety; UL 61730 is the current standard harmonized with IEC 61730 (UL 1703 is being phased out) |
| **UL 1741** | Inverters, converters, charge controllers, interconnection equipment | Safety and grid-interaction of power-conditioning equipment |
| **UL 1741 SA** | Smart inverters | "Supplement A" — grid-support functions for older interconnection rules |
| **UL 1741 SB** | Smart inverters | "Supplement B" — aligned with **IEEE 1547-2018** advanced grid-support and ride-through; the current requirement in most interconnection jurisdictions |
| **IEEE 1547** | Distributed energy resources (DER) | Interconnection and interoperability: voltage/frequency ride-through, anti-islanding, reactive power |
| **UL 3741** | PV Hazard Control Systems (PVHCS) | System-level approach to firefighter shock-hazard reduction, recognized as a rapid-shutdown compliance path inside the array boundary |
| **UL 9540** | Energy storage systems (ESS) | System-level safety of the complete battery + inverter package |
| **UL 9540A** | ESS | Test method for thermal-runaway fire propagation; informs NFPA 855 siting/separation |
| **UL 1973** | Batteries for stationary use | Cell/module/pack-level battery safety |
